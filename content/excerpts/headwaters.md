The ice-keeper's whole liturgy fits in one gesture: a cupped hand.

The pilgrims arrive in their thirteenth year, by foot for the last three days because the Trust permits nothing faster, and they are tired and altitude-light and entirely unprepared for how loud the silence is. The keeper leads them to the meltwater channel below the woven shade sails, and shows them how to drink.

*This water is not yours,* the keeper says, the same words every season for a hundred years. *You are borrowing it from a child who will be born after every name you know is forgotten. Drink, and be the kind of ancestor worth borrowing from.*

The pilgrims drink. The snow holds its breath. Somewhere below, five rivers leave the mountains and go down to the bright and patient country, carrying the morning with them.
