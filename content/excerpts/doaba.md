The seed librarian of Phagwara kept a drawer she never catalogued. In it: a handful of wheat from 1947, carried sewn into a coat hem; an apricot stone from a Kabul garden; kinnow pips mailed from California in an envelope that still smelled, faintly, of someone's kitchen.

*These are the books people checked out and returned a lifetime late,* she told the apprentices. *We do not charge fines.*

When the Returned came home in the famine generation, they came with suitcases of soil-stained paper packets, and the Graft Country received them the only way it knew how — by planting everything, and waiting to see what Panjab had been keeping safe for Panjab, all those years, in the gardens of the scattered.
