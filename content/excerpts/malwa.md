A dust storm is coming, and the steppe is folding its hands.

Section by section the canopies tilt, feather, and close — a hundred kilometres of mirrors turning their faces away with the unhurried courtesy of camels kneeling. Beneath them the shepherds whistle the flocks into the lee walls. The balladeer on walkway nine finishes her verse anyway. Her audience is three sun-tenders and a wall of advancing brown sky, and she has sung for worse.

The old cotton country died of debt and thirst, and the people of Malwa do not pretend otherwise; the names of the lost are sung at Vaisakhi, every year, before anything else is sung.

Then the storm passes, the canopies open like a held breath let go, and the steppe goes back to its one long task: turning light into enough.
