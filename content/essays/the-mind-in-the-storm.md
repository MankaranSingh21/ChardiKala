summary: >-
  Sukhmani Sahib has been prescribed for anxious minds for four centuries — but Chardi Kala was never a command to pretend you are fine. On depression, anxiety, and the sangat's unfinished work.
---

Start by clearing away the worst misreading in modern Sikh life: **Chardi Kala does not mean "act fine."** The doctrine of ever-rising spirits was forged by people who wept, raged, and grieved on the record — a Guru who watched his sons die and *said so* in the Zafarnama; a scripture whose tenderest poet, Sheikh Farid, wrote some of its darkest lines about the body's long night. The bani does not skip the grief; it refuses to let grief be the last word. A tradition that sings Anand Sahib at funerals is not denying death. It is holding two truths at once — which is precisely what a healthy mind does and a depressed mind, through no fault of its own, temporarily cannot.

That distinction — between the discipline of hope and the illness that disables it — is the conversation the community still owes its members. Punjabi culture's vocabulary for psychological suffering remains thin and punishing: *pagal* as a slur, therapy as scandal, the family's izzat managed by silence. The costs are countable — in Punjab's suicide data, in diaspora emergency rooms, in elders medicating loneliness with whisky and youth with worse. None of this silence comes from the Gurus. The bani talks about mental states **constantly** — it is, among other things, the most sustained piece of introspective literature in Punjabi — and it talks about them as *weather*, not as character: the mind wanders, burns, sinks, races; the texts name the states without shaming the experiencer, and then offer practice.

What does the tradition actually offer? Three things, honestly labelled. **Practice**: simran and nitnem are attention-training with five centuries of user reports and growing clinical company; Sukhmani Sahib has been read at bedsides as anxiety care since the 1600s. These help the way exercise helps a heart — genuinely, preventively, and not as a substitute for surgery. **Sangat**: the strongest protective factor research keeps rediscovering — belonging, routine, being expected somewhere — is the gurdwara's default product; loneliness has no easier first treatment than a langar shift. **Permission**: this is the one the community must build. Seva includes therapy. Hukam includes serotonin. The Guru who kept a hospital at Kiratpur (Har Rai) and the Guru who tended Delhi's epidemic sick (Har Krishan) did not treat medicine as a failure of faith — and a Sikh on antidepressants is not in spiritual deficit; she is, in the tradition's own language, tending the body the One issued her.

If you are in the storm now: the tradition's word for you is not weakness. The Ardas names people who were *cut joint by joint* and calls their composure a gift — it has never once been embarrassed by human breaking. Tell someone. Tell a professional. And let the sangat do the one thing it was engineered for: sit beside you, at the same level, without asking you to deserve it.
