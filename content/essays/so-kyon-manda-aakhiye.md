  Guru Nanak's argument for women was settled in scripture by 1604. The practice still lags the text — and the text, recited daily, keeps the pressure on.

Somewhere in the first decades of the 1500s, Guru Nanak composed, in Asa ki Var, the stanza Sikh women have been quoting at Sikh men for five centuries. Of woman we are born, of woman conceived; to woman engaged, to woman married; from woman comes the next woman; without woman there is no one at all — **so kyon manda aakhiye jit jamme rajaan** — *then why call her lesser, from whom kings are born?* Note the method: not chivalry, not pedestal-praise — an audit. The stanza simply itemizes the dependency and demands the logic. It is the earliest text in any major scripture to argue gender equality as a matter of *reason*.

The Gurus legislated on the argument. Sati — the burning of widows — condemned; purdah refused (Guru Amar Das would not address a veiled woman's veil, only the woman); widow remarriage sanctioned; women appointed to head teaching districts in the 1550s. The amrit of 1699 was sweetened, tradition holds, by a woman's hand, and women take the same initiation, wear the same five kakaar, and receive the name **Kaur** — sovereign — which a Sikh woman keeps for life, exchanging it at marriage for nothing. No Sikh rite requires a male officiant. The tradition's exemplars run from Mata Khivi, canonised in scripture for running the faith's central institution, to Mai Bhago, who re-armed forty broken men with her own shame-proof courage and rode as the tenth Guru's bodyguard.

That is the text and the design. Now the honesty the text itself demands: **the practice lags.** Punjabi patriarchy did not dissolve on contact with the bani. Dowry persists against explicit condemnation; son-preference wrote itself into Punjab's birth ratios so starkly that the community's own institutions had to campaign against feticide; gurdwara committees skew male; the granthi profession is overwhelmingly male though the rulebook is not; and at certain historic shrines, women's participation in specific ceremonial seva remains restricted by custom that reformers — armed, pointedly, with the Rehat Maryada and the bani — continue to contest. The diaspora adds its own ledger: the same communities that produce Kaur surgeons and Kaur cabinet ministers still, in places, police daughters as izzat and indulge sons as investment.

What distinguishes the Sikh situation is not that the gap exists — every tradition has one — but that the **standard sits inside the canon**, recited daily, impossible to repeal. A community that sings *so kyon manda aakhiye* at its own services has pre-installed the rebuttal to its own worst habits; its daughters do not need to import their argument, only to quote it. The work of this century is the same as the work of every century since 1604: close the gap. The text is finished. The sangat is not.
