The last salok of Japji Sahib — recited by observant Sikhs every single dawn for five centuries — is an ecology lesson disguised as a benediction: **pavan guru, pani pita, mata dharat mahat**. Air is the Guru. Water is the father. Earth is the great mother, and all of us children playing in the lap of day and night, the two nurses. The verse does not say nature is sacred *property*; it says nature is sacred *family* — and family is a category you can fail.

This is not an isolated proof-text. The bani's God is not enthroned beyond creation but saturating it — *the One fills the filled*; Guru Nanak's hymns hear the wind, water, and fire singing in the cosmic court; the tradition's seventh Guru, Har Rai, kept gardens and hospitals and is remembered weeping for blossoms his robe had broken. The shrines are built around water; the festivals follow the harvest; the very name of the faith's holiest city means *pool of nectar*. A tradition of farmers, addressed by farmers' metaphors — the body as field, naam as seed, the harvest as character — does not need an encyclical to discover the environment. It needs only to reread itself.

Then history made the rereading urgent, and the place it chose was the tradition's own heartland. The **Green Revolution** asked Punjab to feed India, and Punjab did — and the procurement economics locked the land into a rice-wheat rotation it was never suited for. Two generations on, the bill is in: a water table in freefall chased by ever-deeper tubewells, soils thinned by chemistry, pesticide residues in bodies and groundwater, autumn skies opaque with stubble smoke, and a debt cycle whose human cost Punjab counts in funerals. The province whose scripture calls water *father* is draining the father at catastrophic speed. There is no sharper case study on earth of the gap between a community's theology and its agronomy.

The response, characteristically, has been institutional. **EcoSikh**, founded in 2010, turned Guru Har Rai's accession day into Sikh Environment Day, marked now by thousands of gurdwaras — solar roofs, plastic-free langars, sermon themes; its Guru Nanak Sacred Forest project has planted hundreds of dense native micro-forests on gurdwara and village land, the 550-forest target for the founder's 550th anniversary long surpassed. Decades earlier, Bhagat Puran Singh — the saint of Pingalwara, gathering waste paper and printing warnings about trees and water tables when the vocabulary barely existed in Punjabi — had already modelled the disposition: ecology as seva, frugality as devotion.

The Sikh contribution to the climate century, then, is not a new doctrine; it is an old chain of custody. The earth is not a resource to manage or even a cause to champion. She is **mata** — the mother — and the tradition's question to every Sikh, and to every reader of any persuasion, is the question children eventually face about their parents: she carried you; how is your care of her?
