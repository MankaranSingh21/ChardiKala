This site carries the complete Sri Guru Granth Sahib — Gurmukhi, transliteration, and English, all 1430 angs. That is a door, not a syllabus. Here is the practical companion: how a newcomer, of any background or none, actually begins.

**Start at ang 1**, but with modest ambitions: read the Mul Mantar and Japji Sahib — the first eight angs — and stop. Japji is the whole tradition in seed form; the Gurus themselves put it first as the orientation lecture, and Sikhs recite it every dawn for life without exhausting it. Read it slowly, twice. Then, rather than marching page by page into the liturgical section, **jump by waypoints**: Anand Sahib at ang 917 for the tradition's theory of joy; Sukhmani Sahib at 262 for its medicine for anxiety; Asa ki Var at 462 for its social conscience (the famous stanza on women is here); Sheikh Farid's saloks near 1377 for tenderness; Kabir near 1364 for fire. The reader's waypoint list on the Gurbani page is built for exactly this kind of travel.

**Understand the structure you are moving through.** After the opening prayers, the Granth is organized by *raag* — musical mood — not by topic or author. Within each raag, the Gurus' hymns come in order (each signed *Mahalla 1, 2, 3...* — "body" first, second, third: the same light, successive lamps), followed by the bhagats — the Muslim and Hindu saints canonised as equals. You are not reading a treatise; you are reading a **songbook**, and the apparent repetition is the repetition of music: themes returning in different keys until they penetrate.

**Read aloud when you can**, even in translation, even badly. The bani was composed for the breath and the ear; the transliteration line on this site's reader exists so your mouth can participate before your vocabulary does. Punjabi speakers' grandparents were right about this: the sound is doing work the silent eye misses.

**What to do with difficulty.** You will hit lines that are opaque (medieval ragas of metaphor, untranslatable wordplay) and lines that are uncomfortable (the bani audits everyone). The tradition's advice is the farmer's: do not stop the plough for every stone. Mark it, move, return. For depth, the katha tradition — five centuries of recorded exposition, much of it now in English online — is the commentary track; no one reads the Granth alone, and you are not expected to either. And remember what the translation is: a faithful aid (this site uses Dr. Sant Singh Khalsa's, with attribution), never the thing itself. Translations argue; the original sings.

**Last — the posture.** You need not believe anything to read. Come as auditor, as heir, as sceptic; the founder himself began by questioning everything in sight. The single requirement is the one the langar teaches: come honestly hungry. The Granth's own first question is *how is truth obtained?* — and its answer, *by walking in the order of things*, is testable from any starting point, including unbelief. Ang 1 is one click away. **Shuru karo** — begin.
