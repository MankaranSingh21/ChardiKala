Every generation of Sikhs inherits the same assignment with a new exam paper. The teaching does not change; the century does. And the strange, almost suspicious good fortune of this tradition is how precisely the questions of the twenty-first century map onto answers the Gurus filed five hundred years ago.

The century's question of **identity** — who counts, who belongs, whose face is welcome — was answered in 1469 by a man who looked at the world's first available division and declined it: *there is no Hindu, there is no Musalman*. The century's question of **inequality** was answered with a floor: everyone sits at the same height, everyone eats the same dal, and the kitchen never closes. The question of **ecology** was answered in the closing salok of the morning prayer, recited daily by millions before the word "environment" existed: air the Guru, water the father, earth the great mother. The question of **migration** was answered by a community that has been crossing oceans with a rolling pin and a flag since the 1890s, planting kitchens wherever it landed. Even the century's quietest epidemic — **loneliness** — was anticipated by a tradition that made congregation itself the sacrament: sangat, the company that makes you what it is.

This is not an argument that Sikhi is modern. It is older than modernity and indifferent to its approval. The argument is that Sikhi is *load-bearing* — that its institutions were engineered for exactly the stresses this century applies. A faith built by refugees, tested by holocausts, and run for three centuries without a priesthood does not need to be updated for hard times. Hard times are its native climate.

But an inheritance is not a possession; it is a job. The langar feeds millions — and caste prejudice still shadows Sikh matrimonial columns, which the Gurus would have burned. The bani's gender theology was settled in 1604 — and the practice still lags it. The tradition's environmental scripture is luminous — and Punjab's water table falls anyway. The gap between the teaching and the community is the work, and pretending the gap away is the one disloyalty the Gurus never forgave. They audited their own society relentlessly; their students owe their own society the same.

This platform was built for the student standing at the threshold — heritage learner, curious outsider, sceptic, seeker. The essays that follow take the century's subjects one at a time: being seen, feeding strangers, keeping the planet, opening the door, equality, justice, the digital sangat, and how to actually begin reading. The premise of all of them fits in the two words this site is named for. **Chardi Kala** — the rising — is not a mood the lucky have. It is a discipline the committed keep, and it has been keeping people upright through worse centuries than this one.

The light is old. The morning, as always, is new. Begin anywhere.
