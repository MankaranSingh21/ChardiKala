title: Kirat in the Age of Algorithms

kicker: On work, money, and machines
order: 13
summary: >-
  Honest work was a pillar when work meant a plough. What does kirat karo demand in the gig economy, the attention economy, and the age of AI — and what does vand chhakna do about a world of billionaires?
---

Guru Nanak's economics fit on a postcard and have not aged: earn by honest labour (**kirat karo**), share before you consume (**vand chhakna**), and remember what the wealth is for (**naam japna** — the audit that keeps the other two honest). The sakhi of Bhai Lalo set the test case: bread earned by work runs with milk; bread extracted from others' misery runs with blood. The Guru wrung both loaves in public. The question for this century is only where the blood is hiding now.

Some of it is easy to find. The gig platforms that price a delivery rider's day below subsistence while the app's owners compound; the payday loan, the crypto rug-pull, the attention economy's core trade — engineering compulsion in teenagers and selling the harvest — all fail the Lalo test without needing a theologian. A Sikh in those boardrooms has a tradition with standing to ask the only question kirat karo ever asks: *who bled into this margin?* And a Sikh on those apps — because Punjabis drive, deliver, and dispatch across the planet's logistics — inherits the other half of the teaching: the work itself, however ranked by the economy, is **honourable**; the tradition that made a carpenter's bread holier than a banquet has never once been impressed by job titles. There are no menial occupations in Sikhi. There are only menial margins.

Then the harder, newer question: what happens to a theology of work when the machines work? If AI does to cognitive labour what the tractor did to the plough — and faster — a tradition with *labour as worship* in its second pillar must answer carefully. Notice first what kirat karo actually sanctifies: not employment, but **honest effort directed at the common good** — the verb, not the payroll entry. Seva is kirat without a wage; the langar kitchen has run the largest volunteer labour force on earth for five centuries, and no economist has ever managed to call it unemployment. A future with less compulsory toil and more chosen seva is not a threat to the pillar; it is, read one way, the pillar's fulfilment — *if* the abundance is shared. Which it will not be by default. The machine's harvest, like every harvest, arrives pre-encumbered with everyone's claim — vand chhakna was always a distribution doctrine, and dasvandh always a transfer payment. A community that tithed itself through famines has standing to say so about compute.

And the billionaire question, since the century keeps asking it: the bani's verdict on accumulation is the crane hunched over the pond, hoarding what it cannot keep. Wealth in Sikhi is held, not owned — a sarai, the old metaphor goes: you are the innkeeper, not the inheritance. The tradition does not envy the rich. It pities the ones who never learned the second pillar, and it sets the table — same floor, same daal — where they can begin.
