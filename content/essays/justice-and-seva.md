kicker: On standing where it is uncomfortable

summary: >-
  From the Ghadar press to the farmers' protest solidarity convoys, the diaspora's pattern is constant — read of injustice at home or next door, and immediately organize.

There is a repeating figure in Sikh history: the Punjabi abroad who reads the news from home and, instead of grieving privately, founds an organization. In 1913 it was sawmill workers and Berkeley students in San Francisco, hand-setting Gurmukhi type for the Ghadar press and then sailing home by the thousand to take on an empire. In 1914 it was the shore committees of Vancouver, feeding the quarantined passengers of the Komagata Maru. In 2020 it was the grandchildren — tractor convoys in Surrey and protests outside Indian consulates from Melbourne to Toronto, while their cousins camped at Delhi's borders; the langar at Singhu was stocked, in part, by remittance and freight from twelve time zones.

The pattern is not coincidence; it is doctrine with a passport. **Miri-piri** — the two swords — never permitted the spirit to abandon the public square, and the diaspora carried the doctrine into societies with free presses and ballot boxes, where the miri sword takes the shape of a law degree, a newspaper, a campaign. The community that learned organizing in eighteenth-century survival assemblies and perfected nonviolent struggle in the morchas of the 1920s arrived in the West pre-trained for civic life. It shows. Sikhs are statistically minute and politically conspicuous — cabinet ministers in Canada in numbers that outrun the demography, the British Parliament's turbaned members, American civil-rights litigation that has widened religious liberty for everyone downstream of each precedent.

But the tradition's test of justice-work is not whether it serves the community; it is **sarbat** — all. And the record, at its best, meets the test. The langar trucks that follow hurricanes do not check the survivors' politics. Khalsa Aid's first convoy went to Kosovo's Muslims; its recent ones to Ukraine's Orthodox Christians. The Sikh coalition lawyers who won workplace turban cases widened the same statutes that protect the hijab and the yarmulke. The gurdwaras that became vaccination centres and food banks in the pandemic served their postcodes, not their membership rolls. Seva that stops at the community's edge is community maintenance — respectable, but not the doctrine. The doctrine is Bhai Kanhaiya's: the field has no teams the water-skin recognizes.

There is a harder edge to carry too, and honesty includes it: diaspora politics can curdle — grievance without the discipline of the morcha's vows, factional feuds shipped intact from Punjab, the occasional confusion of volume with justice. The tradition's own correctives are old: the jatha swore nonviolence *before* marching; the Sarbat Khalsa set feuds down at the door; the Ardas remembers the martyrs and then asks blessing **for everyone**. Justice in the Sikh grammar is seva's public form — and seva, by definition, is the one thing you cannot do only for your own.
