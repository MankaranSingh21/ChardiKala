---
title: A Door With No Lock
gurmukhi: ਸਭ ਲਈ ਥਾਂ
kicker: On acceptance — including the unsure and the unbelieving
order: 5
summary: >-
  No one is asked their religion at a langar. Atheist, agnostic, seeker, other-faith, ex-faith — the tradition's door was built without a lock, and this platform keeps it that way.
---

Begin with the observable fact, because it is the whole argument in miniature: **no one is asked their religion at a langar.** Not at Harmandir Sahib, where a hundred thousand eat daily; not at the storefront gurdwara in Athens or Anchorage. You sit, you are fed, you may come back forever, and nobody will ever ask what you believe. The most sacred recurring act in Sikhi — the meal at the Guru's kitchen — runs on a complete, deliberate indifference to the diner's metaphysics. A tradition reveals itself in its checkpoints. This one built its central institution without one.

The door's openness is not modern marketing; it is original specification. The scripture canonised Muslim saints and so-called untouchables as equals — other faiths' voices *inside* the holy book, not politely outside it. The ninth Guru gave his head for the worship-rights of a religion he did not practise. The daily prayer's closing line asks welfare for **all** — sarbat — a word with no footnote excluding the unbaptized, the lapsed, or the unconvinced. And the Rehat Maryada states what the kitchens had practised for centuries: a person of any faith, **or none**, is welcome in the sangat.

So what about the atheist, specifically? Honesty first, because hospitality built on fudge insults its guests: Sikhi is not a secular philosophy. Ik Onkar — One — is its first word and load-bearing wall; the tradition believes what it sings. But notice the *kind* of claim it makes and the kind it refuses to make. The Mul Mantar describes the One as truth, creativity, fearlessness, absence of hatred — attributes, not tribal ownership; an invitation to investigate, not a loyalty oath at the door. The bani's harshest words are reserved not for unbelievers but for **hollow believers** — the ritualist with the rosary and the crooked ledger. Guru Nanak's standard was never creedal correctness; it was *truthful living*, which an honest atheist can practise and a dishonest devotee cannot. And the tradition has no hell for the out-group, no missionary quota, no doctrine that the unconvinced are fuel. Conversion is not sought; coexistence is assumed; the Guru's own first companion, lifelong, was a Muslim who never converted and is honoured as the first Sikh anyway.

There is even a place in the tradition's own method for the sceptic's temperament. Sikhi began as an *audit* — a boy interrogating empty ritual and refusing the sacred thread; the bani proceeds by argument, challenge, and test as often as by ecstasy. The seeker who arrives saying "I cannot believe, but I want to understand" is not a problem the tradition must manage. She is using the founder's own entrance.

What the tradition asks of its guests is what any home asks: respect — the covered head, the bowed courtesy to what others hold dear, the shoes at the door. What it offers is everything on the floor: the food, the music, the texts (this site carries all 1430 angs, translated), the history, the company. Belief may come, or not, or come and go; the daal does not check. The door, as the Gurus built it, has hinges and a threshold and no lock — and this platform was built to the same code. Whoever you are, however you believe or don't: **ji aaian nu.** You are welcome. You were always going to be.
