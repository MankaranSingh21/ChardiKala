It is easy to forget that the Guru Granth Sahib is itself a technology decision. Where other traditions located authority in lineages of living masters, the tenth Guru placed it in a **book** — the most advanced information-storage medium of his century — precisely because a book can be copied, carried, and consulted without an intermediary, and cannot be assassinated. The tradition that made that call in 1708 has never since been shy about media. The Singh Sabha met the colonial crisis with **printing presses**; the twentieth century's granthis took to radio and cassette; Harmandir Sahib's kirtan has been broadcast continuously for decades. The livestream is not a rupture. It is the lineage.

Today the digital sangat is, by raw count, the largest assembly in Sikh history. The scripture is searchable in milliseconds in apps that fit in a pocket — Gurmukhi, transliteration, translations layered like this site's own reader. Recorded **katha** in three languages explains any hymn from ten interpretive angles. The diaspora child in a town with no gurdwara attends Asa ki Var at Amritsar before school. Research archives put manuscripts online that scholars once travelled continents to see. And in the pandemic years, when every physical door on earth closed, the sangat moved online essentially overnight — and the langar, pointedly, did not close at all: the kitchens cooked while the kirtan streamed.

The tradition's own concepts supply the cautions, which are real. **Sangat** means the company that shapes you — and the algorithmic feed is also a company that shapes you, optimized for agitation rather than elevation; five centuries of warnings about *kusangat*, degrading company, apply to subscription lists. Katha without a teacher's correction can harden into confident error; anonymity, which the dastar was designed to make impossible, enables in some online Sikh spaces exactly the cruelty visibility was meant to prevent. And no bandwidth carries the thing itself: the floor under crossed legs, the weight of the serving pot, the neighbour's shoulder in the line. The digital sangat is a true sangat the way a letter is a true conversation — real, precious, and incomplete.

This platform lives inside that honest assessment. Every ang of the Guru is here, translated; the history is here, unsoftened; the encyclopedia links onward, entry to entry, the way katha has always linked hymn to story to practice. What is not here is the daal. The site's sincerest hope is to be a *door* in the oldest sense — and doors are for walking through: find the building with the tall saffron flag, cover your head, take off your shoes, eat something. The wifi in the langar hall, you will find, is usually terrible. That, too, is part of the design.
