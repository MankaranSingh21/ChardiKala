In the middle of a siege, around 1704, Sikh soldiers filed a complaint against their own water-carrier. Bhai Kanhaiya was reviving the enemy wounded along with their own — some of the revived were getting up and fighting again — and the soldiers wanted it stopped. Summoned before Guru Gobind Singh, Kanhaiya said the only thing he could see from inside his own eyes: *on the field I saw no Mughals and no Sikhs; I saw the Guru's face in everyone.* The Guru's ruling has governed the tradition ever since. He blessed the water-carrier — and handed him ointment, so the wounds could be bound as well as watered.

Notice what the ruling did *not* do. It did not say empathy was harmless — the complaint was militarily correct; mercy had a price and the Guru paid it knowingly. It did not privatize compassion into a feeling — Kanhaiya was given supplies and sent back out: empathy was operationalized. And it did not exempt the army from the lesson — the soldiers were taught, in public, that the war had limits the water-carrier could see better than they could.

That is the Sikh theory of empathy in one scene: not a sentiment but a **perception with a supply chain**. The bani trains the perception — the same light in all, recited at dawn until it becomes the default setting of the eyes. The institutions handle the logistics. The langar is empathy with a procurement schedule: it does not wait to feel moved by a particular hungry person; it cooks for the hunger that is statistically certain to walk in. Khalsa Aid — which cites Kanhaiya by name as its charter — is the water-skin scaled to container ships: Yemen, Ukraine, Haiti, whoever, because the founding line of its theology, *sarbat da bhala*, has no eligibility clause.

The modern world tends to treat empathy as a mood to be summoned by sufficiently moving footage — and mood-based compassion has the shelf life of a news cycle. The tradition's alternative is unglamorous and durable: build the kitchen *before* the famine; train the eyes *before* the battle; put the tenth of income aside *before* the appeal arrives. When the farmers' protest fed the police sent to disperse it, when Oak Creek's bereaved sangat enlarged its langar the week after the massacre, observers called it extraordinary. It was the opposite. It was ordinary practice, maintained under extraordinary load — which is the entire point of practice.

Empathy that depends on the other person deserving it is a transaction. Empathy that depends on your own training is a character. The tradition asks for the second kind, and supplies the gym: the kitchen is open, the rota has gaps, and the water-skin, as ever, needs carrying.
