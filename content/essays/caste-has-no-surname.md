title: Caste Has No Surname
gurmukhi: ਜਾਤ ਨਾ ਪੁੱਛੋ
kicker: On finishing the Gurus' oldest fight
order: 11
summary: >-
  Singh and Kaur were designed to delete caste in one generation. Five centuries on, matrimonial ads still filter by it. The tradition's most explicit teaching is its most unfinished.
---

No teaching of the Gurus is more explicit, more repeated, or less ambiguous than this one. *Recognize no caste* — Guru Nanak sat with the lowest first, and said so in verse: **neechan andar neech jaat** — I am with the lowest of the low, and the One's grace is found where they are. The scripture canonised a cobbler and a weaver above kings. The amrit of 1699 made five castes drink from one bowl and replaced every clan surname with two: **Singh, Kaur** — names engineered to make a person's ancestry unsearchable. The langar floor was built as a daily, edible refutation of purity. If commitment could be measured by institutional design, no tradition ever committed harder against caste.

And yet. Open a Punjabi matrimonial page — in Jalandhar or in Brampton — and the columns sort by Jat, Khatri, Ramgarhia, Ravidassia. Villages in Punjab still have **two gurdwaras**, and everyone knows, without it being said, which one is whose. Gurdwara committee politics in the diaspora can be read, with depressing accuracy, as caste blocs in procedural dress. The 2009 Vienna shooting and its aftermath tore the seam open in public: Ravidassia congregations — followers of the bhagat whose hymns sit *inside* the Guru Granth Sahib — felt unwelcome enough in mainstream Sikh spaces that many formalized a separate path. That is not their failure. It is, by the tradition's own arithmetic, ours.

Be precise about what survived and how. Caste among Sikhs persists not as doctrine — no granthi will defend it from the bani, because the bani offers nothing to stand on — but as **kinship habit**: marriage networks, land history, the soft machinery of "people like us." Habit is in some ways harder than doctrine; you cannot excommunicate an instinct. But the tradition has beaten habit before, and its method is on the record: not exhortation but **architecture** — the shared bowl, the shared floor, the shared name. Where the architecture is used as designed, caste measurably thins; where community life routes around it (separate shrines, sorted marriages), caste regenerates like a pruned weed.

So the work is unglamorous and specific. Strike caste from the matrimonial filter — your own, before anyone else's. One gurdwara per village is a cause worth the committee war. Bhagat Ravidas's hymns deserve the same airtime as any Guru's — sing Begampura at the festival and mean it. And when the aunty asks, of a prospective match, *what are they?* — the tradition long ago issued the only Sikh answer to that question: **Singh. Kaur.** That was always the whole point of the names: that the question should come back empty.

The Gurus finished the doctrine in 1699. The sangat's deadline, five centuries gentle, has not been extended. It has merely been ignored.
