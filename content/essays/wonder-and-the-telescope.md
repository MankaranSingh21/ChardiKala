summary: >-
  "Countless worlds beyond worlds" — Guru Nanak's cosmology never needed defending from astronomy. On why Sikhi has no quarrel with science, and what each offers the other's blind spot.
---

There is a stanza in Japji Sahib that astronomers of Punjabi background tend to discover with a start: **paataalaa paataal lakh aagaasaa aagaas** — worlds below worlds, hundreds of thousands; skies beyond skies. The Vedas and the Semitic books, Guru Nanak notes in the same breath, each give their count of creation; his own verdict is that the count is beyond counting, and only the One knows it. Five centuries before the deep-field image, the founder of Sikhi looked at the night and declined, on principle, to make the universe small enough to flatter us. The tradition has never had to retreat from a single telescope since.

This is not an accident of luck but of method. The Gurus made almost no claims of the kind science falsifies — no creation date, no flat earth held by doctrine, no census of species on a boat. Their subject was the **orientation of consciousness**, and their characteristic mode before nature was **vismaad**: wonder, astonishment — Nanak's hymns of the wind and water and fire *singing*, the seasons as musicians in a court. Where some traditions met each scientific revolution as a siege, Sikhi's texts read more like an advance welcome: a bigger cosmos means more vismaad; the bani had already over-budgeted for awe. Hukam, the divine order the Sikh is asked to walk within, is not violated by the laws of physics. On any honest reading, the laws of physics are **what hukam looks like written down**.

So a Sikh student need not choose between the lab and the gurdwara, and the community's record says so: the tradition's modern saints include engineers and physicians in unembarrassed numbers; the Singh Sabha built science colleges with the same hands that edited scripture; Punjab's farmers conducted, for better and worse, one of history's largest agronomic experiments. The faith's signature posture — *test it, then trust it* — extends naturally to method: the bani itself proceeds by challenge and audit, and the founder's first recorded acts were experiments on empty ritual.

What, then, do the two offer each other? Science, to Sikhi: the humility-discipline of being checkable — a community whose scripture mocks unexamined inheritance should be the *least* tempted by pseudoscience, WhatsApp cures, and astrological wedding dates (the Rehat Maryada banned the last explicitly; the aunties have appealed the ruling). And Sikhi, to science — or rather, to the scientist: what the method by design cannot supply. The telescope finds no purpose in the nebula; the assay finds no mercy in the molecule; the grant cycle does not teach what the knowledge is *for*. The bani's answer has not changed: it is for vismaad, and for seva — wonder at the order, service to everything inside it. Knowledge without those two, the tradition has watched empires demonstrate, is just power with good instruments. The morning prayer still ends the same way it did before the first lens was ground: air the Guru, water the father, earth the great mother — a working cosmology for anyone, of any belief, who has ever stood under the sky and been accurately small.
