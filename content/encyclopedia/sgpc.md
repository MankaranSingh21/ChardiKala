The Shiromani Gurdwara Parbandhak Committee — universally the SGPC — is the statutory, **elected** body that manages the historic gurdwaras of Punjab (with Haryana's now under a parallel committee, after long dispute), Harmandir Sahib and the Akal Takht at the head of its charge. Created by the reform movement in November 1920 and clothed in law by the Sikh Gurdwaras Act of **1925**, it administers the shrines, their vast langars and serais, and an institutional empire the movement's martyrs could not have imagined: colleges and schools by the dozen, hospitals, printing presses (it is the principal publisher of the scripture), museums, and the payroll of thousands of granthis, ragis, and sevadars.

Its constitutional importance exceeds its budget. The SGPC is elected by universal adult Sikh franchise — women voting from 1925, a fact worth restating — and it earned, early, the nickname **"the Sikh parliament"**: the one body that can claim a mandate from the Panth's ordinary millions. It convenes the appointment of the Akal Takht's Jathedar, publishes the authorized Rehat Maryada, and stands, formally, as the morchas' permanent yield: the shrines held in trust by the community's own ballot.

An honest entry records the standing critique, which Sikhs voice more sharply than outsiders: elections mean parties, and the committee's capture by Punjab's dominant Sikh political machine across decades — patronage, delayed polls (the general house has gone unrenewed for long stretches), and the tangled question of whether Jathedars serve the Panth or the party — is the community's longest-running institutional argument. The structure remains what 1925 made it: democracy applied to sacred trust, with democracy's diseases included in the licence.
