Bhag Bhari of Jhabal, in the Majha, was raised in a family of early Sikhs and trained — unusually for her time, unremarkably for her temperament — in arms and horse. When the forty deserters of Anandpur came home in 1705 carrying their signed disclaimer of the Guru, it was Mai Bhago who made their position unlivable: she rode the villages, the tradition records, putting the question to the men in public — and to their wives, who closed their doors — until shame reorganized itself into resolve. Then she armed, mounted, and **led them back herself**, the column's commander by moral force.

At the pool of Khidrana she fought in the line — the battle that broke the imperial pursuit at the cost of nearly all forty, redeemed by the Guru's own hand tearing their disclaimer. Mai Bhago, grievously wounded, was found alive among the fallen. Recovered, she asked neither discharge nor honour but proximity: she served thereafter in Guru Gobind Singh's personal guard, armed and present, to Nanded itself — a woman bearing weapons at the Guru's shoulder, in the tenth Guru's own arrangement of his court, which settles certain modern arguments rather efficiently.

After his passing she travelled south and lived to great age at Jinvara, near Bidar, in contemplation — the sant after the sipahi, the full sequence of the ideal. Her spear and her hut's site are kept at Hazur Sahib; gurdwaras and Sikh women's institutions across the world carry her name. The tradition's summary of her is structural: when the men's courage failed, the supply was restored from the women's reserve — which the Gurus had been filling, deliberately, for two centuries.
