Sant-sipahi — saint-soldier — is the human ideal of the Khalsa, and everything depends on the word order. The sant comes first: a person of dawn prayer, melted ego, and universal love. The sipahi is what that person becomes when the innocent are threatened — and *only* then. A soldier who is not first a saint is merely armed; the tradition has a long memory of what armed men without inner discipline do, having buried their victims.

The synthesis was built deliberately across the Guru period. Guru Nanak's fearlessness was unarmed — confronting Babur's atrocities in verse, at imprisonment's risk. Guru Hargobind added the sword under miri-piri after the first martyrdom. Guru Gobind Singh completed the design at Vaisakhi 1699: the Khalsa initiate vows simultaneously to rise for dawn meditation *and* to defend the defenceless — the kirpan worn on a body that also carries the comb of order and the discipline of prayer.

The tradition polices the balance with its stories. Bhai Kanhaiya, serving water to wounded *enemies*, is honoured as the ideal's purest expression — and pointedly, Guru Gobind Singh sided with him against the soldiers who complained. The Guru's own arrow-quiver, tradition says, carried gold tips so that a man he wounded could fund his recovery or his family. Mai Bhago took up arms exactly once, to retrieve deserters back to a defence, then lived out her years in contemplation. In each case the violence is bounded, reluctant, restorative in intention — and the sainthood is not.

Modern sant-sipahi rarely involves swords. The form persists as the disposition: the nurse who prays at five and fights hospital bureaucracy for her patients at nine; the lawyer at the deportation hearing; the volunteer between a mob and its target. Contemplation that stands its ground. That is the whole specification.
