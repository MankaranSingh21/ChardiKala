On 5 February 1762, near the village of Kup in the Malwa, the Afghan emperor **Ahmad Shah Durrani** — moving with a speed the Sikhs did not believe possible, two hundred kilometres in under forty-eight hours — fell upon the slow-moving baggage train of the Khalsa: the *vaheer*, the nation's families, elders, and children, screened by its fighters. His orders made no distinctions. The Sikh horse fought a moving battle the length of the day, forming a cordon around the families and carrying the column toward water; the cordon broke and reformed and broke. By evening, an estimated **twenty to thirty thousand** were dead — in one day, perhaps a third of the entire Sikh population then living. The chronicles call it the Wadda Ghallughara, the great holocaust; on his return march Durrani blew up Harmandir Sahib with gunpowder and defiled the sarovar with slaughtered cattle.

What happened next is why this entry sits in an encyclopedia of an optimistic faith. The tradition records a survivor's assessment that has become the community's permanent self-description: the Khalsa was *fine* — the dross had merely been tested from the steel. Within **three months**, the survivors attacked Durrani's governor at Sirhind. Within **nine**, they fought Durrani himself to a standstill at Amritsar amid the ruins of their shrine. By **1765** they had taken Lahore and begun rebuilding Harmandir Sahib; by 1799 Punjab was theirs entirely.

The Wadda Ghallughara is mourned in the Ardas daily. It is also, unavoidably, the central exhibit of **Chardi Kala**: the doctrine that despair is a surrender no enemy can compel. The community buried a third of itself and concluded it had won. History, within a generation, agreed.
