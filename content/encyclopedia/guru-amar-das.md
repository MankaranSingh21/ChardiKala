Amar Das came to Sikhi old — past sixty — after a lifetime of pilgrimages that had not answered him. He heard his nephew's wife, Bibi Amro, singing Guru Nanak's hymns at dawn, went to meet her father-in-law Guru Angad, and gave the rest of his life, becoming Guru at seventy-three. The lesson sits quietly inside the tradition: it is never too late, and the door into the faith was opened for him by a woman's voice.

His genius was institutional. He took the langar's implicit equality and made it a rule with teeth: **pangat before sangat** — no one, emperor or beggar, could see the Guru without first sitting on the floor and eating with everyone else. When Emperor Akbar came, Akbar sat in the line and ate, and that meal is among Sikh history's proudest entries.

He attacked the cruelties of his time at their gendered root: he condemned **sati** (the burning of widows), opposed **purdah** (the veiling and seclusion of women), sanctioned widow remarriage, and appointed women among the heads of his **manji** system — twenty-two teaching districts that organized the growing faith. He established Goindval as a centre with a stepped *baoli* well whose deliberate point was that everyone drew the same water.

He also gave Sikhi its song of joy: **Anand Sahib**, the Song of Bliss, composed in old age by a man who had found late what he had searched for early. It is sung at every Sikh wedding, every funeral, every conclusion of worship — joy as a discipline, not a mood, which may be the third Guru's most Sikh idea of all.
