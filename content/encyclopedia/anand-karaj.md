Anand Karaj — "the work of bliss" — is the Sikh marriage rite, and its core is four slow circuits of the Guru Granth Sahib while the **Lavan** are sung: the four stanzas Guru Ram Das composed in Raag Suhi, each marking a stage of the soul's union with the One — duty, awakening, detachment-into-love, union. The couple walks each round together, joined by a palla (scarf); the architecture of the rite states the theology of the institution: the partners do not face each other at an altar but walk **side by side around the Guru** — marriage as two travellers taking the same road, the scripture at the centre, not the contract.

The ceremony's deliberate simplicity is doctrine with a history. The Gurus and the reform movements fought the wedding's enemies on two fronts: **ritual complexity** (no fire, no priest required — any Sikh may officiate; no astrology, the date set by convenience, a stand the Rehat Maryada makes explicit) and **expense** (dowry condemned outright; the Anand Marriage Act of 1909, a Singh Sabha victory, gave the rite civil standing precisely to free Sikh weddings from borrowed rites). Anand Sahib is sung — the ceremony is named from it — ardas offered, hukam taken, prashad shared; the whole fits inside an hour, langar following.

Equality is structural: the same vows, the same rounds, no giving-away that transfers a woman as property — though honest reporting notes Punjabi wedding culture's gravitational pull (lavish spending, dowry's stubborn persistence) against which the rite's plainness is a standing rebuke. The community's current arguments — interfaith couples at the Granth, recognition questions — are conducted, characteristically, with the Rehat Maryada in one hand and the langar serving everyone regardless.
