The Guru Granth Sahib is 1430 **angs** — "limbs," the respectful word used instead of "pages" — containing nearly 6,000 hymns. Three facts about its construction tell you most of what Sikhi is.

**It is plural on purpose.** Alongside six of the Sikh Gurus stand the compositions of Hindu bhagats and Muslim saints — Kabir the weaver with over 500 hymns, Farid the Sufi, Ravidas the leather-worker, Namdev the calico-printer, and a dozen more, several from castes their society called untouchable. They are not quoted as guests or refuted as rivals; their words carry the same authority on the same pages. No other major scripture on earth canonised the saints of other religions as equals.

**It is organized by music.** After the opening liturgical section (Japji Sahib and the daily prayers), the body of the Granth is arranged into thirty-one **raags** — melodic frameworks of North Indian music, each suited to an hour and an emotional weather: Asa for hope before dawn, Sri for dignified twilight, Basant for spring. Within each raag, the Gurus' hymns come in order of succession, then the bhagats'. The index of the scripture is a map of moods; truth is filed by how it should be *sung*.

**It is fixed.** Guru Arjan compiled the Adi Granth in 1604 against forgery and dilution; Guru Gobind Singh completed the canon (adding his father's hymns, and none of his own); and since 1708 not a syllable may be added or altered. Translations — like the English one on this site — are aids to understanding, never replacements: the Guru is the original, in Gurmukhi, in raag.

Its first word is **Ik** — One. Its final composition is a salok of gratitude, and then, like a meal, it ends with sweetness: the Raagmala, a garland of the music it is made of.
