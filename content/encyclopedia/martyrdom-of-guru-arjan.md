In 1606 the new emperor Jahangir — whose own memoirs record his irritation that the Guru's "shop" of faith drew Hindus and Muslims alike, and his decision to put a stop to it — found his occasion in politics: the Guru was accused of blessing the emperor's rebel son Khusrau. The sentence was death by torture, *yasa o siyasat*; the fine first levied went unpaid because the Guru refused to accept guilt where there was none.

Tradition preserves the sequence with terrible clarity. In Lahore's summer heat, Guru Arjan was made to sit on a heated iron plate while burning sand was poured over his body; he was offered release for conversion, for miracles, for an apology, and answered each offer with equanimity. The Sufi saint **Mian Mir**, his friend, came in anguish and (the tradition says) offered to intercede or worse; the Guru's reply has become the Sikh sentence for every hard hour since: **"Tera kia meetha lagai"** — *sweet is Your will, the gift of Your Name Nanak asks for alone.* After days of torture he was taken to the river Ravi to bathe and did not come out of the water. He was sixty-three.

The community's response, channelled by his son, redrew the tradition. Guru Hargobind took the throne wearing **two swords**, raised the Akal Takht, and began the arming of a congregation that had concluded — reasonably — that the empire would not let it merely sing. Every Sikh discussion of power, protection, and the limits of nonviolence runs back to this June; so does every recitation of sweetness in suffering. The hot plate and the calm voice upon it are, together, the tradition's deepest image of what it costs to refuse to lie.
