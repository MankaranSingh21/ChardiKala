Kirat karo — work, earn, by your own honest labour — is the second pillar of Sikh life, and it carries a double refusal. Against the religious culture of his time, Guru Nanak refused **mendicancy** as a spiritual credential: the holy man living on others' alms while claiming superiority over them struck the Guru as having the ledger backwards. And against the economy of his time and ours, the pillar refuses **exploitation**: earnings extracted by fraud, hoarding, or another's misery are *haram* to a Sikh whatever their legality — the bani's image is eating the bread of someone else's blood.

The founding demonstrations are famous. Guru Nanak ended his life as a farmer at Kartarpur — the founder of the faith, hands in the soil, modelling that no one graduates from work into pure holiness. The sakhi of Bhai Lalo makes the economics explicit: the Guru chooses the carpenter's coarse bread over the landlord's feast, and when pressed, wrings both — milk from the worker's, blood from the exploiter's. Dignity follows the sweat, not the spread.

Three consequences shaped Sikh culture visibly. First, the absence of a begging monastic class: Sikhi has granthis and scholars, but they eat from the same kitchen they serve, and the tradition of the Gurus taking no offering for themselves runs deep. Second, the consecration of ordinary occupations — farming, soldiering, trade, transport — as fields of practice, which is part of why Sikh communities worldwide cluster in visible, laborious work and treat diligence itself as piety. Third, the link to the third pillar: only honestly earned wealth is fit to share. The langar must be cooked from clean money; vand chhakna without kirat karo is laundering.

The pillar's modern edge cuts at both ends of the economy: it indicts the idle rich and the wage-thief equally. Work, the tradition says, is how love of the One takes bodily form on a Tuesday.
