Dasvandh — "the tenth part" — is the Sikh practice of dedicating ten percent of one's earnings to the community's needs: the langar's flour, the gurdwara's roof, the school, the clinic, the flood relief. It crystallized in the period of Guru Arjan, who organized the growing Panth's voluntary offerings through the masand collection system to fund the great works of the era — the temple, the tank, the towns — and it survives today as a personal discipline rather than an enforced levy: no one audits a Sikh's dasvandh but the Sikh.

Its logic completes the three pillars. Kirat karo sanctifies the earning; vand chhakna mandates the sharing; dasvandh gives the sharing a *number*, which matters psychologically more than it sounds. A proportion decided in advance defeats the nightly renegotiation in which generosity always loses to anxiety. The tenth is allocated before wants expand to fill the income — sharing as infrastructure, not impulse.

The tradition attaches conditions to both ends of the flow. The money must be clean: dasvandh from exploitation is not purification but laundering, and the bani's contempt for such offerings is explicit. And the giving should be quiet: the donor's plaque is foreign to the spirit of a practice whose model is the anonymous sack of flour at the langar door.

What dasvandh has built is simply visible. The global gurdwara network with its daily free meals; the historic shrines maintained without state funding; Khalsa Aid's trucks; the Punjab schools and hospitals of the Singh Sabha era — all run, ultimately, on millions of households doing private arithmetic each month. As a system it has financed five centuries of open kitchens without a single means test, which is a record worth any economist's attention.
