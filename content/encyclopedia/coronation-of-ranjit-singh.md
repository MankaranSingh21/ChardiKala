On Vaisakhi 1801, Ranjit Singh of the Sukerchakia misl — nineteen years old, blind in one eye from childhood smallpox, unlettered, and already master of Lahore for two years — was anointed Maharaja of Punjab, the saffron mark applied by a Muslim faqir's family's associate in a ceremony attended by every community of the city. The forty-year state he built, the **Sarkar-e-Khalsa**, ended a century of invasion across the Khyber: the road that had delivered nine Afghan incursions in two decades delivered none after him.

The coronation's most Sikh feature was an absence. The new sovereign struck **no coin in his own name** — the currency, the Nanakshahi rupee, bore the Gurus' names; the government was styled the Khalsa's, not the king's; and court protocol referred victory and authority to the Guru. Historians note how unusual this is: a conqueror constructing his legitimacy as *trusteeship*, removable in principle — and the Akal Takht once publicly fined him for a moral lapse, a sentence he accepted, baring his back for the lash that was then remitted.

His administration ran on deliberate plurality — Muslim foreign ministers (the Fakir brothers), Hindu finance (the Dogras, to the state's later cost), European generals drilling the army, and festival patronage across every faith; the death penalty fell into disuse. The era's failures are equally instructive: no succession architecture, no constitution beyond the king's character — and when the character died in 1839, the state had ten years to live.

He gilded Harmandir Sahib, giving English its phrase "Golden Temple." Punjab's Muslims, Hindus, and Sikhs would jointly mourn him — and the empire that annexed his kingdom kept his one-eyed portrait, with grudging admiration, in its histories of worthy enemies.
