The dispute was almost comically small: Akalis cutting firewood for the langar from a garden attached to the Guru ka Bagh gurdwara, twenty kilometres from Amritsar, on land a displaced mahant still claimed. The arrests began in August 1922; the SGPC, recognizing the stakes hiding inside the trivial — could Sikhs be barred by police from the Guru's own kitchen-woodlot? — made it a morcha, and the morcha made history.

Each day, a jatha of one hundred Akalis took vows at the Akal Takht — nonviolence absolute, no hand raised even to ward a blow — and marched the road to the garden. Each day, police under English officers met them with brass-bound lathis: blows to the head and groin, the fallen dragged by the hair and thrown in ditches, the next rank stepping forward over them, hands folded, reciting **Waheguru**. Day after day, week upon week — over five thousand beaten or arrested before it ended. The Church of England clergyman **C. F. Andrews**, who stood witness in September, wrote what became the campaign's permanent caption: he had seen "a new heroism, learnt through suffering," something that made him think of the Cross — and he begged the governor, successfully, to stop what officers were no longer able to perform. (The government engineered a face-saving exit: a Hindu philanthropist leased the land and let the wood-cutting proceed.)

Guru ka Bagh demonstrated, with experimental rigour, the morcha's core mechanism: organized cruelty requires functionaries, and functionaries have a breaking point that disciplined suffering can reach faster than disciplined suffering breaks. Gandhi studied it; the empire remembered it; and the Akalis, having won the firewood, went on to win the Act.
