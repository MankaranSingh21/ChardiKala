Amrit Sanchar is the rite of initiation into the Khalsa — the ceremony Guru Gobind Singh instituted at Vaisakhi 1699 and the Panth has performed, essentially unaltered, ever since. It cannot be administered by any individual, however exalted: it requires **five** initiated Sikhs in good standing, robed as the Panj Pyare, because the office of initiation belongs to the five collectively — the tenth Guru himself took amrit from his own first initiates, and the precedent is constitutional.

The rite's center is the **preparation**: in an iron bowl (*sarbloh* — the Khalsa's metal), water and **patase** (sugar crystals — Mata Sahib Kaur's addition: sweetness folded into steel), stirred continuously with a **khanda**, the double-edged sword, while the five recite the five banis over it in turn — Japji, Jaap, Tav-Prasad Savaiye, Chaupai, Anand — the full attention of five devout people concentrated into water for the better part of two hours. The candidates — bathed, in the five kakaar, any age of understanding, any sex, any former caste or faith — receive the amrit five times in cupped hands, five times in the eyes, five times on the head, each with the cry *Waheguru ji ka Khalsa, Waheguru ji ki Fateh*; they drink, pointedly, from the **same bowl**. The five then administer the vows: the daily nitnem, the kakaar, the code of conduct, the renunciation of caste and of former rites; a new name's first letter may be taken from a hukam; karah prashad is shared.

The tradition is explicit that the rite is a **beginning**, not a credential — the word sanchar means transmission, and what is transmitted is a discipline whose maintenance is daily. Those not ready are counselled to wait, not hurried: the bowl, like the door, does not close.
