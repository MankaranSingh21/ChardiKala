After the long siege of Anandpur ended in December 1704 — the garrison leaving under imperial oaths of safe conduct that were broken within hours — the Guru's column was attacked at the flooded Sarsa river and scattered. Guru Gobind Singh reached the village of Chamkaur with about forty Sikhs, including his two elder sons, and took shelter in a mud-walled double-storey house. The pursuing imperial force surrounded it; the chronicles' numbers vary wildly, but the proportion is not in dispute: dozens against an army.

What followed entered the marrow of Sikh memory. Sikhs went out in fives to fight and die in turn, holding the host at bay through the day. **Sahibzada Ajit Singh**, seventeen, asked his father's leave, was blessed and sent, and fell fighting; **Sahibzada Jujhar Singh**, fourteen, asked the same and followed his brother. The tradition preserves the Guru's bearing precisely because it is barely believable: he watched both sons die and is recorded thanking the One that the trust deposited with him had been redeemed.

As night fell, the surviving Sikhs invoked the authority the Guru himself had created: as the Five, speaking as the Khalsa to its servant, they **ordered** him to escape — the Panth could make more martyrs but not another Guru. He obeyed his disciples (the 1699 circle closing in battle), leaving his crest visible and clapping, by one tradition, to announce his departure to the besiegers — escape, but no deception unworthy of it.

Zafarnama would later distil Chamkaur into the couplet flung at Aurangzeb: what bravery is it, that a hundred thousand fall upon forty? The mud house still stands in memory as the tradition's measure of odds: count the cause, not the enemy.
