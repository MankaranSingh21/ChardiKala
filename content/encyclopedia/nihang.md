The Nihangs — the word carries senses of crocodile, fearless one, and (from Persian) the unencumbered — are the Khalsa's old standing army never quite demobilized: an order in **electric blue** robes and towering, weapon-dressed dastars (the *dumalla*, sometimes crowned with the farla of honour), bearing steel — kirpans, chakkars, spears — and a vocabulary of institutional cheerfulness called **Nihang bola**, in which a single Nihang is sava lakh (a hundred and twenty-five thousand), hunger is the divine kitchen's delay, and death in battle is a wedding.

Their lineage runs from the tenth Guru's court and the eighteenth century's guerrilla decades, when the Dal Khalsa's permanent cadre kept arms, shrines, and the martial curriculum while the peasantry farmed between wars; **Akali Phula Singh**, the order's most famous Jathedar, policed even Ranjit Singh's morals from the Akal Takht. Organized today in dals (the Budha Dal and Taruna Dal foremost) under their own Jathedars, the Nihangs live substantially on horseback and in moving camps (*chhaunis*), keep the old rehat with antiquarian rigour — and keep, too, customs the wider Panth debates, from shardai (the cannabis-laced draught they defend as battlefield tradition and others contest) to a jealously guarded ceremonial autonomy.

Their great public exercise is **Hola Mohalla** at Anandpur each spring: gatka, tent-pegging at the gallop, cavalry displays — the martial inheritance drilled in daylight. To the community they are something between honour guard, living museum, and conscience-in-arms: anachronistic by design, on the theory — not yet falsified — that someone should remember, in the body and not only in books, exactly what the Khalsa's eighteenth century knew.
