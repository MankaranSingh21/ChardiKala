Guru Arjan inherited a pool and left a civilisation. In the sarovar his father dug, he built **Harmandir Sahib** — deliberately low, so that one steps *down* into it, and deliberately open, with doors on all four sides for all four castes and every direction of the world. Tradition honours the Sufi saint **Mian Mir** of Lahore as the layer of its foundation stone: a mosque-saint founding the Sikhs' holiest shrine, which is exactly the point.

In 1604 he completed the work that secured the faith against forgery and fragmentation: the **Adi Granth**, the first authorised compilation of the Gurus' hymns — along with the bani of Hindu bhagats and Muslim saints, set as equals in the same volume. He installed it in Harmandir Sahib, appointed the aged Baba Buddha its first granthi, and slept on the floor beside it: the scripture above the Guru, a hierarchy that would become literal a century later.

He composed more of the Guru Granth Sahib than anyone — 2,218 hymns, including **Sukhmani Sahib**, the Psalm of Peace recited daily in millions of homes for the settling of the mind.

In 1606 the emperor Jahangir, irritated (his own memoir says so) by the Guru's growing following among Hindus and Muslims alike, had him tortured to death in Lahore — seated on a heated iron plate, burning sand poured over his body, then taken to the river Ravi where he disappeared into the water. Tradition keeps his response as a single line, still sung in every hard hour: **"Tera kia meetha lagai"** — *sweet is Your will*. He is the first martyr of the Sikhs, and the tradition's understanding of suffering — met without hatred and without surrender — begins with him.
