Sukhmani — the jewel of peace, or the mind at peace — is Guru Arjan's longest and most beloved composition: twenty-four *ashtpadis* (eight-stanza cantos) in Raag Gauri, beginning at ang 262. Each canto opens with a salok stating a theme and then turns it like a gem, facet by facet.

The first salok announces the medicine: remembrance. The opening canto then makes the largest claims in the calmest voice — in the remembrance of the One, the fear of death departs; in remembrance, no anxiety; in remembrance, the mind grows luminous. Sikh tradition has prescribed it accordingly ever since: Sukhmani is what is recited in hospital corridors, in houses of mourning, in the long nights of worry. Many Sikhs read a daily canto; many sangats hold weekly full recitations, which take about ninety minutes and leave a room measurably quieter than they found it.

Inside its cantos sit some of the Guru Granth Sahib's most quoted teachings. The canto on the **brahmgiani** — the God-conscious person — is a portrait of the tradition's ideal human: unstained as the lotus in water, even-eyed toward friend and stranger, gentle to all because the One is seen in all. Another canto contains the line that anchors Sikh universalism: the One is in all, and there is no one without that light.

The title offers the most honest summary. Sukhmani does not promise the removal of difficulty; it promises **sukh** — peace, ease, the unclenching of the inner fist — in the middle of difficulty. Four hundred years of anxious humans have tested the claim. The recitations continue, which is its own kind of evidence.
