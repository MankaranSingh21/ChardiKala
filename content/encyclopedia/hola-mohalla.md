In 1701, by the tradition's dating, Guru Gobind Singh instituted at Anandpur a pointed revision of the season's festival: where Holi splashed colour, **Hola Mohalla** — the masculine-grammared "charge and assault" — would drill. The Khalsa divided into companies, stormed an agreed position in mock battle, and reviewed the exercise with poetry and prizes: spring fever conscripted into readiness, play retooled as preparedness, the festival itself a sant-sipahi.

It remains exactly that, and has become one of the subcontinent's great gatherings: for the days around Holi, **Anandpur Sahib** fills — attendance runs into the high hundreds of thousands — with the **Nihang dals** at the centre of the program: gatka bouts, sword and spear displays, bareback and tent-pegging cavalry runs at the full gallop down the riverbed, the blue legions in their towering dastars conducting, in public, the eighteenth century's complete martial syllabus. Around the exercises, the festival is a city: kirtan darbars and political conferences, poetry in the old style, and **langar at planetary scale** — hundreds of improvised kitchens lining the roads, tractor-trailers as dining halls, the count of meals served exceeding all arithmetic but the tradition's own (everyone; thrice daily; ask nothing).

The festival's logic answers a standing question about the tradition: how does a community keep a martial inheritance without feeding militarism? Hola Mohalla's solution is **daylight**: the weapons drilled publicly, festively, annually — skill maintained as heritage and spectacle, sworn to the sant-sipahi's terms before the crowd. The procession ends, the langar continues; the colour Holi lost returns anyway, in saffron and electric blue.
