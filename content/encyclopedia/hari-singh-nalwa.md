Hari Singh earned the name *Nalwa* — by the favoured account, for killing a tiger that had taken his horse, with a hand weapon, dismounted — and the rest of his career kept the standard. Commander-in-chief of Ranjit Singh's campaigns across three decades, he took Kasur, Multan's approaches, Kashmir (whose governorship he held, striking coin), and finally the country no power had administered against its will since antiquity: the trans-Indus frontier of the Pashtuns.

His method was the fort. **Jamrud**, at the very throat of the Khyber Pass; Sumergarh at Peshawar (whose governorship he held with a firmness the frontier's ballads remember without affection); a chain of garrisons knitting the wild marches to Lahore. Under Nalwa the pass that had delivered every invasion of India for eight hundred years — Ghaznavid to Durrani — was **closed from the east**, a strategic inversion whose scale is best measured by its uniqueness: no one had done it before; the British, inheriting his line, never improved on it. Frontier mothers, the durable legend runs, hushed children with his name.

He was also, in the era's pattern, a builder beyond the military: the city of **Haripur** in Hazara — planned, walled, watered — bears his name; gurdwaras at Panja Sahib among his works.

He died in post. In April 1837, with the main army at a royal wedding, the Afghans came for Jamrud; Nalwa, ill, rose and broke the attack, taking mortal wounds — and ordered his death concealed until reinforcement arrived. The garrison obeyed; the fort held; the frontier did not learn the news, the story insists, until it was too late to matter. The empire he guarded survived him by barely a decade — frontiers, like the man said nothing about it, being mortal arrangements.
