In the mid-1960s, with India one failed monsoon from famine and dependent on humiliating American grain shipments, the state bet its food security on Punjab: high-yield dwarf wheat, assured procurement prices, subsidized fertilizer and power, and the canal-and-tubewell water to drive it. Punjab's farmers — the inheritors of kirat karo, newly secure on consolidated holdings — answered beyond every projection. Wheat yields tripled within a decade; the state, under **2% of India's land**, came to fill half or more of the national grain reserves; the famines stopped. As an act of collective labour it ranks among the century's great achievements, and it was achieved substantially by Sikh smallholders.

The bill arrived a generation later, and Punjab is still paying it. The rice-wheat rotation the procurement system locked in was an ecological mismatch — paddy in a semi-arid plain — and the **water table** began falling, then plunging; lakhs of tubewells now chase it downward at depths the small farmer finances on credit. Soils compacted and thinned under chemical loading; pesticide residues seeped into water and bodies (the train from the Malwa cotton belt to the Bikaner cancer hospital acquired a name no state wants: the Cancer Train); stubble burning fouled the air of half a subcontinent; and **debt** — the input treadmill's permanent passenger — became the region's quiet epidemic, with farmer suicides its terrible ledger.

The Green Revolution is thus Punjab's central modern paradox: the province performed a rescue and was consumed by the apparatus of the rescue. The 2020–21 farmers' protest, EcoSikh's replanting, and the slow turn to diversification are all, in their ways, negotiations with this inheritance — the harvest's children arguing with the harvest.
