Sarbat Khalsa — "the entire Khalsa" — is the general assembly of the Sikh people: the gathering, historically at the Akal Takht in Amritsar, where the community's gravest decisions were taken collectively, in the presence of the Guru Granth Sahib, as **gurmatta** — the Guru's resolution, binding because adopted before the Guru by the Panth in whom the Guru lives.

The institution flows directly from 1708. With the guruship vested jointly in Granth and Panth, authority had to have a procedure, and the eighteenth century — Sikhi's most desperate — built it under fire. Twice a year, at Vaisakhi and Divali, the hunted bands rode in from the forests to Amritsar; questions of war, alliance, succession, and strategy were debated openly; and decisions, once taken as gurmatta, bound even the chiefs who had argued against them. The misl confederacy — twelve sovereign bands cooperating without a king — ran on this operating system for half a century. Historians have called it one of the era's most remarkable experiments in deliberative governance: a stateless nation governing itself by twice-yearly general meeting.

Its discipline was the point. At a Sarbat Khalsa, rank was suspended — the voice of a barefoot soldier weighed with a chief's; decisions sought consensus before counting heads; and personal feuds were formally set down at the door, because one does not bring enmity into the Guru's court.

Ranjit Singh, ironically, suspended the institution his power grew from — emperors prefer not to be outvoted — and the colonial state kept it suppressed. Modern attempts to convene Sarbat Khalsas (1920, 1986, 2015) have carried weight and controversy in proportion to the Panth's divisions. The institution awaits, as constitutionalists say, better weather. The principle it guards is permanent: in Sikhi, the community is not the audience of authority. It is the seat of it.
