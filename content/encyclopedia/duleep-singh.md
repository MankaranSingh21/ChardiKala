Duleep Singh was five when the murders of the Lahore court made him Maharaja, ten when the annexation treaty was put before him for signature — the kingdom, the army, and the Koh-i-Noor itemized away over a child's name. The Company then performed one of imperialism's most intimate operations: separated from his mother, placed under a Scottish guardian, he was converted to Christianity at fifteen and transplanted to England — the squire of **Elveden Hall**, Suffolk; crack shot, party host, painted by Winterhalter; Queen Victoria's documented favourite, godfather-close to the royal children. The empire displayed him as its proof of benevolent digestion: the lion-cub turned country gentleman, presenting to the Queen — the scene is recorded — the diamond taken from him, as though giving it.

The unravelling began with his mother. Reunited with **Jind Kaur** in 1861, he heard across two years the unedited version of his own biography. After her death the questions compounded — into archives, lawyers, petitions over confiscated estates, and at last open revolt: in 1886 he sailed for India to reclaim his faith and his place, was **intercepted at Aden**, and there, in the port's limbo, **re-took amrit** — the Maharaja returning to Sikhi in a coaling station, which is somehow the era in one image. Exiled anew, he spiralled through Paris and a baroque conspiracy (Irish Fenians, Russian patronage) that came to nothing; broken and stroke-struck, he accepted the Queen's pardon and died in a Paris hotel in 1893.

He had asked to be buried in Punjab. He lies at Elveden, under English yew — the empire keeping, past death, the one thing he had finally clearly wanted returned.
