Gurdwara Darbar Sahib Kartarpur stands on the west bank of the Ravi in Pakistan's Narowal district, amid the fields **Guru Nanak farmed with his own hands** for the last eighteen years of his life — the place where the wandering ended, the first sangat gathered, the first langar was served, and the founder of Sikhi died in 1539 demonstrating that holiness and a wheat harvest are compatible occupations.

The site's double memorial is its oldest teaching. When the Guru died, the tradition records, his Hindu followers wished to cremate him and his Muslim followers to bury him; the quarrel found, under the sheet, only flowers — divided half and half. The compound today holds both a **samadhi** (cremation memorial) and a **grave**, side by side: the founder's last lesson, administered posthumously, in the architecture.

Partition placed Kartarpur four kilometres inside Pakistan while the bulk of the Panth went east — and for seventy years the shrine, white-domed above the floodplain, was the visible unreachable: pilgrims at the Indian border post of Dera Baba Nanak viewing it through fixed binoculars, the *darshan* of last resort. The river shifted, the shrine flooded and was restored (1920s, funded notably by the Maharaja of Patiala; again in the 1990s–2000s by Pakistani authorities and diaspora donations), and the binocular vigil continued until November 2019, when the **Kartarpur Corridor** opened for Guru Nanak's 550th birthday — visa-free passage to the farm, the first soft fold in the subcontinent's hardest border.

Pilgrims now walk the fields. Many, the staff report, simply touch the wheat.
