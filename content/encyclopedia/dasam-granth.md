The Dasam Granth — "the book of the tenth" — is the compilation associated with Guru Gobind Singh and his court of poets. It stands in a carefully different relationship to Sikhs than the Guru Granth Sahib: it is read, parts of it are recited daily, but it is not the Guru. Guru Gobind Singh himself settled that question by adding none of his own compositions to the canon he sealed — an act of authorial restraint with few parallels.

Its contents are diverse. **Jaap Sahib**, the rolling meditation on the names and namelessness of the divine, opens the daily nitnem alongside Japji Sahib; its rhythms are built for momentum, hundreds of salutations cascading — to the One beyond country, beyond garb, beyond name. **Bachittar Natak**, "the wondrous drama," is the Guru's verse autobiography — including his account of his father's martyrdom, told with a son's controlled grief. The **Zafarnama** is his Persian epistle of moral victory to the emperor Aurangzeb. Other large sections retell Puranic narratives and folk material from the Guru's literary court at Paonta, where fifty-two poets worked.

Students should know honestly that the Dasam Granth's textual history is debated within the community — which portions are the Guru's own pen, which are his poets', and how the volume should be treated have been argued for over a century, sometimes hotly. What is not debated: the compositions in the daily discipline (Jaap Sahib, Tav-Prasad Savaiye, Chaupai Sahib) and the Zafarnama's standing as a monument of moral literature — a bereaved man, militarily crushed, writing to his persecutor that the truth had won and history would agree. History, as it happens, agreed.
