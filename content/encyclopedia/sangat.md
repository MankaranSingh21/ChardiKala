Sangat — from *sang*, together — is the gathered community, and Sikhi assigns it a power that traditions centred on solitary attainment reserve for the master: the bani says that in the sangat, the seeker becomes what the sangat is. Bhai Gurdas calls it the touchstone that turns base metal gold. Where two or more sit with the Guru's word between them, the Guru is present — which is why the tradition has no monasteries: the human crowd, rightly aimed, *is* the ashram.

The premise is psychological realism. Character is contagious; we become our company. Most traditions know this; Sikhi institutionalised it. The gurdwara's daily rhythm is engineered to put the wavering next to the steady: kirtan sung *together* (the sangat sings, not a choir performing to an audience), prayers in the first-person plural, and the langar where you cannot avoid sitting beside a stranger. Even the most interior Sikh practice, simran, is commonly done in company at dawn.

Sangat is also where Sikh authority lives. Guru Gobind Singh vested the guruship jointly in Granth and **Panth** — scripture and gathered community. Decisions of weight are taken as **gurmatta**, resolutions of the sangat in the Guru's presence; the historic Sarbat Khalsa was this principle at the scale of the whole nation. No priesthood holds a monopoly: any competent Sikh, any gender, may lead prayer, and the sangat itself is the unit that confers or withdraws legitimacy.

Two practical notes for newcomers. First, sangat is open: a visitor of any faith or none, head covered and shoes off, is part of the sangat for that hour, no questions asked. Second, the tradition's warning runs the other way too — *kusangat*, low company, degrades as surely as sangat elevates. Choose the room; the room is choosing you back.
