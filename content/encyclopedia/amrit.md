Amrit means the nectar of deathlessness — *a-mrit*, the un-dying — an old Indic word the Gurus repurposed with characteristic precision. In the bani, amrit is rarely a liquid: the **Name is amrit**, the Guru's word is amrit, and the hours before dawn are **amrit vela** — the nectar-time, when the world's noise is low and remembrance pours easiest. The immortality on offer is not unending biography but contact with what does not die.

The liquid came in 1699, and its recipe is a theology. At the first initiation of the Khalsa, Guru Gobind Singh stirred water in an iron bowl with a **khanda** — a double-edged sword — while the five beloved recited bani over it; the Guru's wife, Mata Sahib Kaur by tradition, added **patase**, sugar crystals. Steel and sweetness; the resolve of the sword dissolved in the same water as gentleness — the sant-sipahi in beverage form. The initiates drank from one bowl, all castes' lips to the same vessel (a deliberate scandal in its century), and the Guru then knelt and received it from their hands.

That rite, **Amrit Sanchar**, continues unchanged: five initiated Sikhs prepare the amrit as the Panj Pyare did, and those who receive it take on the discipline of the Khalsa — the daily bani, the five kakaar, the vows of conduct. To "take amrit" remains the Sikh phrase for full commitment.

For the learner, the wider usage matters most. Amritsar is the *pool* of nectar; the morning discipline drinks at the *hour* of nectar; the scripture is read as nectar by the line. The tradition keeps insisting the immortal sweetness is ambient — bottled once at Anandpur, but on tap, every dawn, wherever attention turns.
