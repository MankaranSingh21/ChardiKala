The word is the doctrine: **gur-duara**, the Guru's *door* — not the Guru's house, fenced and owned, but the threshold anyone may cross. A gurdwara is any place the Guru Granth Sahib is duly installed, from Harmandir Sahib to a converted bungalow in Surrey to a single room in a Kenyan town; the building's grandeur is incidental, and the tradition has worshipped in forests when the buildings were burned.

Its anatomy is standard worldwide. The **darbar** hall, where the Granth sits enthroned under a canopy, attended with a whisk — congregation on the floor below it, no reserved seating, no pews ranking the sangat; kirtan sung, hukam taken (the day's command read at random opening), ardaas offered standing. The **langar** hall, where the free kitchen runs — for many gurdwaras, continuously. A **nishan sahib** flying outside, visible across the fields or the suburb: the flag that historically meant *food, shelter, and protection here*. Often now: classrooms, clinics, libraries, sports halls — the gurdwara as the community's full-service civic platform, which in the diaspora it has had to be.

The protocols at the door are few and universal: head covered, shoes off, no tobacco or intoxicants — and they apply identically to a Sikh elder and a curious tourist, because the entry rules are about respect, not membership. **No one is asked their religion at a gurdwara door**; the Rehat Maryada states what five centuries practised. There is no priesthood with sacramental monopoly — the granthi is a reader and caretaker, and any competent Sikh, woman or man, may conduct any ceremony. Management is by elected committee, with everything that implies; the institution survives its politics the way it survived everything else.

For newcomers the operational summary is simple: you are already invited. Cover your head, eat something, sit awhile. That was the design spec from the beginning.
