After 1947, India redrew itself along languages — Telugu, Marathi, Tamil states formed through the 1950s — but Punjabi's claim met a unique resistance: a Punjabi-speaking state would be the only one with a Sikh majority, and Delhi balked at the overlap of language and faith. The result was an asymmetry Sikhs read as distrust: every major language got its suba except theirs. The campaign that followed ran nearly two decades — morchas in the classical pattern, tens of thousands courting arrest in waves (the Akali Dal's machinery filling jails as it had in the 1920s), fasts by leaders Master Tara Singh and Sant Fateh Singh, and a counter-mobilization in which many Punjabi Hindus, urged by communal organizations, declared Hindi their mother tongue to the census — a falsification of the linguistic map whose bitterness outlived the question.

The 1966 Punjab Reorganisation Act finally conceded the state — and dismembered the province a second time in twenty years: Haryana cut away, the hill districts to Himachal, and the new Punjab left at a third of its pre-1947 self. The settlement's deliberate loose ends became the next era's tinder: **Chandigarh**, built as Punjab's capital, held as a shared union territory "temporarily" (the temporariness is now in its seventh decade); the **river waters** allocated by formulas Punjab has contested ever since; and Punjabi-speaking tracts left outside the state.

The Suba was a victory that taught a corrosive lesson: that even success arrived discounted, hedged, and late. The Anandpur Sahib Resolution of 1973 was drafted by men who had learned it; the catastrophe of the 1980s was kindled, in part, on its unresolved remainders.
