Waheguru is the name Sikhs most often use for the One — in greeting, in prayer, in danger, in gratitude, in the rolling congregational chant, and in the silent repetition that accompanies a Sikh through the day. Its construction tells you what Sikh theology is made of: **wah** is an exclamation — wow, wonder, astonishment — and **guru** is the light that dispels darkness. The divine name preferred by the tradition is not a title of command but a gasp of amazement that became a word.

This is theology by disposition. The bani's most characteristic mode is wonder — *vismaad* — Guru Nanak's hymns marvelling at wind, water, fire, the moonlit galaxies and the order that holds them, all singing. To name God "Waheguru" is to make that astonishment the default address: every utterance of the name is a small renewal of the decision to be amazed rather than entitled.

In practice, Waheguru functions as the **gurmantar** — the word given for remembrance. Sikhs repeat it in **simran** (meditative remembrance), aloud or silently, often coordinated with the breath; sangats sing it for hours; it punctuates ordinary Punjabi speech the way breath punctuates talk. The full greeting of the Khalsa wraps it around the community and victory alike: *Waheguru ji ka Khalsa, Waheguru ji ki Fateh* — the Khalsa belongs to the Wondrous One, and so does victory.

The bani is explicit that no single name exhausts the One — the Granth uses hundreds, from Hari and Ram to Allah and Khuda, sometimes in the same hymn, a multilingual refusal of monopoly. Waheguru is not the One's "real name" as against impostors. It is the Sikh way of saying: whatever else is uncertain, the wonder is not.
