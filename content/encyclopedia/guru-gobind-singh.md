Gobind Rai was nine when his father went to Delhi to die for someone else's freedom of worship. The guruship he inherited carried an unresolved question — how does a community of saints survive an empire of executioners? — and his answer, given on Vaisakhi of 1699, recast Sikhi permanently.

Before a vast assembly at Anandpur he asked, sword in hand, for a head. Five men rose, one by one — from five castes, five regions of India. These **Panj Pyare**, the five beloved, he initiated with **amrit**; and then, in the gesture that separates Sikhi from every cult of personality, he knelt and took initiation *from them*. The Guru became the disciple of his disciples. The **Khalsa** born that day carried new names — Singh and Kaur, lion and sovereign — that deleted caste from the surname, and five articles of faith binding them to visible, accountable identity.

What it cost him is almost beyond accounting. His two elder sons died fighting at Chamkaur; his two younger, aged about six and nine, were executed at Sirhind for refusing conversion; his mother died in custody. From the wilderness afterward he wrote the **Zafarnama** — the Epistle of Victory — to the emperor Aurangzeb in Persian verse: a defeated, bereaved man informing power that it had lost, containing the couplet quoted ever since: *when all other means have failed, it is righteous to draw the sword.*

He was also a poet and patron of poets, keeping fifty-two at his court; his Jaap Sahib piles up names of the divine like a waterfall. In 1708 at Nanded, dying of an assassin's wound, he performed his final act: he bowed to the Adi Granth and **closed the line of human Gurus forever**, vesting the office in the scripture and the community — Guru Granth and Guru Panth. No successor, no dynasty, no relic: a book of songs and a people responsible for themselves.
