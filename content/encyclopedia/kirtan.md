Sikh worship has no sacrifice, no eucharist, no votive rite at its centre: it has **singing**. Kirtan — the congregational singing of the bani — is the tradition's principal devotional act, and this is by construction, not custom: the Guru Granth Sahib is *organized as a songbook*, its hymns filed under thirty-one raags with their rhythms specified, composed to be sung and only secondarily to be read. The founding image of Sikhi is a duet — Guru Nanak with Bhai Mardana's rabab — and the institution has never stopped being one.

In the gurdwara, **ragi** ensembles (traditionally a trio: two vocalist-harmonium players and a tabla player; the older string tradition of rabab, saranda, and taus is being revived with some passion) sing from the Granth with the words displayed and, increasingly, translated on screens. The sangat is not an audience: joining the refrain is the practice, and the distinction between performer and congregation is doctrinally suspect — the music's purpose is to dissolve exactly such separations. At Harmandir Sahib, kirtan runs from before dawn to night and has for centuries: the scripture in essentially continuous performance, now streamed to the planet.

Why music as the primary channel? The tradition's answer is empirical: sung truth bypasses the argumentative mind and installs directly. A teaching debated can be deflected; a teaching *hummed for forty years* becomes character. The bani's deepest claims travel as melody — which is why the Gurus specified the raags: Asa's pre-dawn hope, Sorath's plain talk, Basant's spring — emotional weathers chosen so the meaning arrives wearing the right music. Kirtan is theology administered as song, the dose repeated nightly, the patient frequently observed singing back.
