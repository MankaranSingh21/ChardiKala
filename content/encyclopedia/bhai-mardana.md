Bhai Mardana was a Mirasi — a Muslim of a hereditary musician caste ranked low and patronized lightly — from Guru Nanak's own village of Talwandi, some ten years the Guru's senior, and his companion for the better part of five decades. When the Guru set out on the udasis, the journeys that would cover the breadth of Asia, he took one person: Mardana, with his **rabab**, the stringed instrument whose voice became the first accompaniment of Gurbani. The pattern of revelation in the Janamsakhis is a duet — the Guru, sensing bani descending, saying *Mardana, touch the strings*, and the hymn arriving on music. Sikh kirtan, the tradition's heart, begins in that partnership: a Muslim's instrument under the founder's words.

His standing in the tradition is unique and uniquely instructive. He was never asked to convert; he died, after a lifetime beside the Guru, a Muslim — and he is honoured as, by many reckonings, the **first Sikh**: the first to learn from Nanak, the first to sing him. Three saloks within the Guru Granth Sahib stand in his name. The tradition preserves his humanity with affection — the sakhis' Mardana is perpetually hungry, periodically exasperated, asking the questions a tired companion asks, which become the openings for the Guru's teaching. He is, structurally, all of us: the ordinary person walking beside the extraordinary, kept by loyalty and wonder.

His descendants, the **rababi** lineages, sang at Harmandir Sahib for four centuries until Partition severed them to Pakistan — a loss kirtan still feels. Where the Guru's universalism needs a face, the tradition points here: the first Sikh was a Muslim with a lute.
