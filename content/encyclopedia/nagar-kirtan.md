A nagar kirtan — "town-singing" — is the Sikh procession: the **Guru Granth Sahib**, enthroned on a flower-dressed float (the palki), taken through the streets of a city with the sangat walking and singing behind — the gurdwara turned inside out, the scripture making housecalls. Ahead of the Guru walk the **Panj Pyare**, five beloved with drawn swords, barefoot; ahead of them, sevadars *sweep and wash the road* the Granth will travel — the municipality's asphalt receiving, for one morning, the protocol of a throne room.

The form descends from the Gurus' own travelling sangats and the singing bands of the festival calendar; today it is the standard public face of every gurpurab and Vaisakhi on earth. The procession is a moving exhibition of the whole tradition: kirtan jathas on flatbeds; **gatka** squads sparring at the intersections; schoolchildren's floats; and on both kerbs for the route's whole length, **langar gone missionary** — stalls pressing fruit, water, pakoras, and full meals on every passer-by, shopper, police officer, and bus passenger within reach, the city fed whether it asked or not. Diaspora nagar kirtans — Surrey's Vaisakhi parade at half a million, Southall's, Melbourne's — are among the largest annual religious processions outside India, and double as the community's principal open house.

The theology of the form is hospitality's logical conclusion: if the door is open and the kitchen free, eventually the house itself must go out. A nagar kirtan is the gurdwara demonstrating, at walking pace and full volume, that it meant the signage — the Guru in the street, the langar on the kerb, the whole city briefly, deliciously, sangat.
