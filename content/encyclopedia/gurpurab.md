A gurpurab — "the Guru's day" — is the anniversary of a Guru's birth, accession, or martyrdom, and the cycle of them is the Sikh year's recurring curriculum: the community re-teaching itself its founders, one life at a time, with the same instruments each time — the **akhand path** (unbroken forty-eight-hour reading of the Granth) concluded on the morning itself; **nagar kirtan** processions through the streets behind the Panj Pyare; kirtan darbars late into the night; gurdwaras strung with lights; and langar scaled to the occasion, which is to say enormous.

The great fixed points: **Guru Nanak's birth gurpurab** (the full-moon of Kattak, November), the calendar's summit — at Nankana Sahib, at Amritsar's lit-up Darbar Sahib, and in every diaspora town with a school gym to rent; **Guru Gobind Singh's** (December–January); and the two **martyrdom gurpurabs** that keep the calendar honest — Guru Arjan's (June, remembered with **chhabeels**, the roadside stalls of cooled sweetened water served to all passers-by in the scorching month: the answer to the hot plate is cold water for strangers, the tradition's whole rhetoric in a paper cup) and Guru Tegh Bahadur's (November–December). Late December holds the heaviest week: the **Shaheedi days** of the Sahibzade, kept in deliberate winter austerity.

The form's genius is repetition with the grain of family life: a Sikh child absorbs the Gurus' biographies not as catechism but as the rhythm of festival — the year itself the textbook, lights and processions the footnotes, and the langar, as ever, the exam everyone passes.
