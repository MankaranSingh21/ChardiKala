Vaisakhi — the first of the solar month of Vaisakh, almost always **April 13th** — was Punjab's festival long before 1699: the wheat harvest's gate, the peasant year's hinge, the day the fairs filled. Guru Amar Das had already fixed it as a day the sangat gathered; **Guru Gobind Singh chose it deliberately** for the Khalsa's creation, grafting the new order onto the land's oldest festival — so that every harvest thereafter would also be *the* harvest, the day the five rose and the amrit was stirred.

The double festival is kept accordingly. In Punjab: the grain in, the fairs out, and at Anandpur and Damdama Sahib enormous gatherings; everywhere, gurdwaras hold **Amrit Sanchars** (taking initiation at Vaisakhi remains the classic timing), the **Nishan Sahib's chola is changed** — the flagpole lowered, washed by the sangat's hands, re-robed and raised to the jaikara — and **nagar kirtans** take the Granth through the streets behind the Panj Pyare, with gatka, floats, and the langar gone mobile: a free fair with scripture at the centre. Diaspora Vaisakhi parades — Vancouver's and Surrey's count in the hundreds of thousands — are among the largest Sikh public events on earth, and double as the community's open house: the city invited to eat.

History has twice marked the day in other ink: the empire chose Vaisakhi 1919 for **Jallianwala Bagh** — the massacre falling on the Khalsa's birthday in the Khalsa's holy city — and Sikhs keep both memories on the same date, the founding and the wound, without apparent contradiction: it is, after all, the festival of what rises.
