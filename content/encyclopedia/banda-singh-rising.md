At Nanded the dying Guru Gobind Singh transformed his last recruit: Madho Das, a Deccan hermit, became **Banda Singh Bahadur** — *banda*, the slave (of the Guru), armed with five arrows, a council of five Sikhs, and a commission: go north, and bring the oppressors of Punjab to account.

What followed astonished the empire. Rallying the Khalsa as he marched, Banda took town after town and, in May 1710 at **Chappar Chiri**, destroyed the army of Wazir Khan of Sirhind — the executioner of the Guru's youngest sons. Sirhind fell; the empire's eastern Punjab administration collapsed. In its place Banda did something stranger than conquest: he founded a state with the Gurus as sovereign — coins struck in the names of Nanak and Gobind Singh, a seal proclaiming the kettle and the sword (*degh o tegh*, sustenance and protection) — and enacted what historians count among the subcontinent's earliest land reforms: the **zamindari intermediaries swept aside, ownership vested in the tillers**. Peasants of every faith — the chronicles are explicit that Muslims who joined were free in their worship — became proprietors, and remained so in attitude long after the state fell; Punjab's stubborn yeoman pride traces here.

The empire's full weight returned, and after a starving eight-month siege at Gurdas Nangal, Banda was taken in 1715. Delhi staged the lesson: seven hundred Sikhs executed over days, each offered conversion first, the Mughal court's own news-writers recording their refusals and their composure as something beyond explanation. In June 1716 Banda was tortured to death, his small son murdered before him. The state had lasted six years; the precedent — Khalsa sovereignty, peasant ownership, plural protection — proved more durable than the empire that crushed it.
