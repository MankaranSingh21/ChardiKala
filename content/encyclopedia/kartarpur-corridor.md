Of all Partition's amputations, Kartarpur was the most visible: the gurdwara marking where **Guru Nanak farmed, taught, and died** stands barely four kilometres inside Pakistan, close enough that pilgrims on the Indian side spent seventy years viewing its white dome through coin-operated binoculars at the border post of Dera Baba Nanak — a pilgrimage of optics across one of the planet's most militarized frontiers.

In November 2019, for the **550th anniversary of Guru Nanak's birth**, the two states did something neither's politics had permitted in seven decades: they opened a **visa-free corridor** — a fenced, dedicated road and bridge over the Ravi's old course — allowing thousands of Indian pilgrims daily to walk or ride to Kartarpur Sahib and return by evening, passport in hand but no visa, no embassy, no years of waiting. Pakistan restored and vastly expanded the shrine complex for the occasion; the first jatha crossed on 9 November 2019, and the images — elderly Partition survivors touching the soil of the village their parents had described, weeping into the wheat — carried the event's meaning past all official communiqué.

The corridor has survived what it was reasonable to doubt it would: diplomatic ruptures, even the aftermath of military exchanges between the two states, and a pandemic closure — reopening each time, a four-kilometre exception both governments have found politically impossible to abolish. Sikhs note the symmetry without needing to embellish it: the man who said *there is no Hindu, there is no Musalman* lies in a mausoleum maintained by Muslims, visited by Sikhs and Hindus, across a border his shrine quietly refutes — the first place the Partition line has ever bent for a pilgrim.
