During the famine months of the Anandpur siege, forty Sikhs of the Majha had done what exhausted men do: they signed a *bedava* — a disclaimer, "you are not our Guru, we are not your Sikhs" — and went home. What met them there became one of history's great stories of shame transmuted. Their wives and mothers, the tradition records, refused them entry on the old terms; and **Mai Bhago** of Jhabal, a Khalsa woman of formidable will, gathered, armed, and led them back to find the Guru they had quit.

They intercepted him near the pool of Khidrana, in the dunes of the Malwa, with an imperial force in pursuit. The forty — with Mai Bhago fighting among them — took position at the water and broke the pursuit at the cost of nearly all their lives. The Guru came to the field afterward and moved among the dying, wiping faces, blessing each by name. The last alive, **Bhai Maha Singh**, asked with his remaining breath not for paradise but for paper: tear the bedava. Guru Gobind Singh took the document from his belt — he had carried their desertion next to his body — and tore it before Maha Singh's closing eyes, naming the forty the **Chali Mukte**, the Forty Liberated. Khidrana has been **Muktsar**, the Pool of Liberation, ever since.

The tradition's emphasis falls not on the victory but on the economy of forgiveness: the bond was real, the desertion was real, and the door back was real — at full price, and standing open. Mai Bhago, wounded, lived on as the Guru's bodyguard. Sikhs who have failed and mean to return still call the road back "going to Muktsar."
