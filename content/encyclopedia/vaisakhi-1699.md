On the spring harvest festival of 1699, Guru Gobind Singh summoned the sangat to Anandpur in unprecedented numbers — and appeared before them with a drawn sword, asking for a head. The crowd's terror is part of the story's honesty; this was not rhetoric, and people fled or froze. **Daya Ram** of Lahore rose first. The Guru took him into a tent; the tradition's staging has the sound of a blow and the Guru emerging with a bloodied blade to ask again. Four more rose: a farmer, a washerman, a water-carrier, a barber — five castes, five regions of India.

The five emerged robed alike, and the Guru performed the rite that remade the community: water stirred in an iron bowl with a double-edged khanda, bani recited over it, sweetness added by Mata Sahib Kaur's hand — **amrit**. The five drank from one vessel, shattering caste's deepest taboo, and received one surname: Singh. Then came the act with no precedent in religious history: the Guru knelt and **begged initiation from his initiates** — *Waho waho Gobind Singh, aape gur chela*: hail the master who is his own disciple.

Vows bound the new order: the five kakaar worn always; dawn remembrance; no caste observance; protection of the defenceless of any faith; force only as last resort. Tens of thousands took amrit in the following days — and, the chronicles note, some aristocratic Sikhs left in disgust at sharing a bowl with the low-born, an exodus the Guru accepted as the price of the point.

The Khalsa's birthday remains the Sikh calendar's pole star. Whatever else Vaisakhi is — harvest, new year — it is the day the faith ate from one bowl and meant it.
