In Asa ki Var, Guru Nanak takes up the question of woman directly and ends with the line Sikhs have quoted for five centuries: **so kyon manda aakhiye jit jamme rajaan** — *why call her lesser, from whom kings are born?* The argument around it is striking for its method: not chivalric praise but logical audit. Of woman we are born, to woman engaged and married, through woman the race continues; when one woman dies, another is sought — how can the indispensable be inferior?

The Gurus legislated accordingly, against the grain of their era. Guru Amar Das condemned **sati** and purdah, sanctioned widow remarriage, and appointed women among the heads of his teaching districts. The sangat and pangat admitted women from the start — the langar line has never been gendered. The amrit of 1699 was prepared, tradition holds, with Mata Sahib Kaur's sugar in the bowl, and women receive the same initiation, wear the same kakaar, and take a name of equal weight: **Kaur**, sovereign princess, a surname that means a woman never carries a father's or husband's clan name as her identity. No Sikh ceremony requires a priest who must be male; women lead prayer, perform kirtan, and read the Granth in sangats worldwide.

The history shows what the doctrine made possible: Mata Khivi running the institution that fed the faith; Mai Bhago leading deserters back into battle; Maharani Jind Kaur frightening an empire; Sophia Duleep Singh selling suffragette newspapers outside Hampton Court.

Honesty requires the other column. Punjabi patriarchy did not dissolve on contact with the bani: dowry, son-preference, and the gap between scriptural principle and village practice are real, and the community argues — as it should — about remaining ceremonial exclusions at certain historic sites. The tradition's own texts are the standing rebuke. On this question, the scripture finished its work in 1604; the sangat's work continues.
