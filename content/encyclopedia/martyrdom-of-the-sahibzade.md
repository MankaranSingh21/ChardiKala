In the scattering at the Sarsa river, the Guru's mother **Mata Gujri** and his two youngest sons — **Zorawar Singh**, about nine, and **Fateh Singh**, about six — were separated from the column and betrayed to the authorities by a former servant. They were imprisoned in Sirhind's **Thanda Burj**, the cold tower, in the December of Punjab's coldest weeks: an old woman and two small boys, held without fire.

Wazir Khan's court offered the children the standard exit, wrapped in kindness: convert, and live — be adopted into comfort, honoured, raised as the state's own. The chronicles give the boys answers that Punjabi mothers have sung to children ever since: they were the grandsons of the Guru beheaded in Delhi rather than convert, and the blood had not thinned. Day after day they were returned to the tower; their grandmother, tradition says, told them each night the stories of their grandfather's calm — and asked them, each morning, to remember whose they were. The Nawab of Malerkotla, **Sher Mohammad Khan**, rose in the court to protest that Islam did not war on children — a dissent Sikhs have honoured Malerkotla for, for three centuries; during Partition's slaughter, Malerkotla was left untouched.

The sentence stood. By tradition the boys were bricked into a wall alive, and when the masonry would not stand, executed by the sword. Mata Gujri, told in the tower, died — the tradition says — in the same hour.

The week of their martyrdom, late December, is now kept across the Sikh world as the **Shaheedi days**: a season of barefoot processions and unlit ceremonies between Christmas and New Year, when the Panth remembers that its smallest members held its heaviest line.
