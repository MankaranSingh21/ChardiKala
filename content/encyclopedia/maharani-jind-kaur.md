The youngest wife of Ranjit Singh, daughter of the royal kennel-keeper — the court never let her forget the kennels, and she never behaved as if she remembered them — Jind Kaur became, on her infant son Duleep's accession in 1843, regent of the collapsing Sikh state: the only adult in a court of murderers, holding the regency through the First Anglo-Sikh War with a blunt force of personality the British Resident's dispatches describe with open alarm. She tore her veil before the assembled sardars, the famous account runs, and asked who among them deserved it more. London's men on the spot wrote frankly that the "Messalina of the Punjab" (their courtesy) was worth regiments, and proceeded accordingly.

The Company's destruction of her was methodical: severed from her son in 1847 — he was nine; she would not see him for **thirteen years** — imprisoned in Sheikhupura, then the fortress of Chunar, from which, in 1849, she **escaped disguised as a beggar woman**, leaving (the story is hers and entirely in character) a note advising her jailers that she had gone by *magic*. Nepal granted cold asylum; the British confiscated her famous jewels and her pension; she went, in the long Kathmandu years, nearly blind.

In 1861 the empire, judging her harmless, permitted reunion. It miscalculated one last time: in two years in England with Duleep — the converted, anglicized squire of Elveden — she quietly returned him his memory: who he was, what had been signed away, where amrit was taken. She died in Kensington in 1863, age forty-six. Duleep's later rebellion, his return to Sikhi, his doomed petitions — historians trace them to those two years. The state had been lost; the mother retrieved the son.
