A generation after annexation, Sikh institutions were visibly eroding: missionary schools converted high-profile Sikh students (the 1873 baptism announcements of four Amritsar students were the proximate spark), the shrines lay with hereditary mahants drifting toward practices the Gurus had abolished, and census takers debated whether Sikhi was merely a Hindu sect. The response, founded at Amritsar in 1873 and at Lahore in 1879, was the **Singh Sabha** — not a political party but a renaissance: the decision to fight absorption with literacy.

Its instruments were schools and presses. **Khalsa College, Amritsar** (1892) anchored a network of Khalsa schools — including, notably early, schools for girls; Punjabi-language newspapers and tract societies put Sikh scholarship into village hands; and a generation of scholars — Giani Dit Singh the formidable polemicist (born into an "untouchable" caste, a fact his opponents never let him forget and he never let defeat him), Bhai Kahn Singh Nabha of the great encyclopedia *Mahan Kosh*, and **Bhai Vir Singh**, poet and novelist, who all but rebuilt Punjabi literature — gave the tradition its modern intellectual armature. Kahn Singh's pamphlet title became the era's banner: *Ham Hindu Nahin* — we are not Hindus — a scholarly assertion of distinctness, not enmity.

The movement had its costs, which honest history records: the drive for clear boundaries hardened some practices and polemics, and its Tat Khalsa orthodoxy marginalized folk syntheses with their own long roots. But its legacy is the infrastructure of modern Sikhi — the schools, the scholarship, the standardized rites that became the Rehat Maryada, and the awakened laity that would shortly take back the gurdwaras themselves.
