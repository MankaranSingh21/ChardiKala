The Sikh Gurdwaras Act, passed by the Punjab Legislative Council in July 1925, was the formal surrender document of a five-year war the empire had lost to unarmed volunteers. It transferred the historic gurdwaras of Punjab — Harmandir Sahib and the Akal Takht at their head — from hereditary mahants and government-appointed managers into the control of a statutory body **elected by the Sikh community**: the Shiromani Gurdwara Parbandhak Committee, the SGPC the movement had already created in fact and the Act now clothed in law.

Two of its provisions repay attention beyond Punjab. First, the franchise: every adult Sikh, **women explicitly included**, could vote for the committee — in 1925, when Britain itself had extended the vote to women on equal terms only partially, and most of the world not at all. The shrines of the Sikhs became, at a stroke, among the world's earliest institutions governed by universal adult suffrage. Second, the definition: the Act had to say in law who a Sikh was (its declaration formula survives, lightly amended, on SGPC voter rolls today), beginning a juridical conversation about identity that the community has conducted with statutes and courts ever since.

The settlement's legacy is double-edged, and Sikh commentary says so freely. It gave the Panth a democratic instrument of unmatched scale — the SGPC's budget and reach earned it the nickname "the Sikh parliament" — and it married that instrument to electoral politics, with everything elections bring: parties, patronage, and the permanent question of whether the Guru's houses are above the campaign or inside it. The Act, like the morchas that forced it, answered one question and endowed the next century's.
