After a quarter-century of journeys that had taken him from Mecca to Assam, Guru Nanak did the least dramatic and most consequential thing of his life: he stopped. On land along the river Ravi he founded **Kartarpur** — "the town of the Creator" — put away the traveller's robes, dressed as a farmer, and worked his fields until his death in 1539.

The settlement was the first laboratory of Sikh life, and its daily timetable became the religion. Before dawn, the sangat gathered for remembrance and the singing of the Guru's hymns — Japji at first light. Then everyone, the Guru included, **worked**: fields, trades, herds — kirat karo demonstrated by the founder's own calluses. And whatever came in was shared at a common table where guests of any caste, faith, or none sat in one line — the langar's seed, watered three times daily.

The radicalism is easy to miss under the pastoral surface. The greatest spiritual figure of his age spent his final eighteen years modelling that holiness requires **no renunciation** — no cave, no begging bowl, no abandoning of family (his wife and sons lived there; so did his elderly parents' memory and his successor-in-training). Seekers came expecting an ashram and found a working farm where the master grew wheat and the disciples included Hindus and Muslims who never stopped being householders.

It was at Kartarpur that Bhai Lehna served his way to becoming Guru Angad. And it is at Kartarpur — its shrine on the Pakistani bank of the Ravi, visible across the border — that the 2019 Corridor now lets pilgrims walk again to where the founder farmed, four kilometres and seventy years away.
