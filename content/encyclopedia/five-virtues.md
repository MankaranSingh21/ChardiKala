Opposite the five thieves, Sikh teaching sets five qualities to be cultivated — the *pañj guṇ*: **sat** (truth, truthful living), **santokh** (contentment), **daya** (compassion), **nimrata** (humility), and **pyaar** (love). They are not commandments but capacities, grown by practice the way muscle is grown by load — and the tradition is specific about the gym: sangat, seva, simran.

**Sat** leads, as it leads the Mul Mantar. But Guru Nanak's famous formulation aims it carefully: truth is high, *higher still is truthful living* — sat as alignment of word, deed, and ledger, not merely accurate speech. **Santokh** is contentment in the active sense: not resignation but the cessation of the inner auction, the discovery that enough exists and you have located it. It is the virtue that makes vand chhakna affordable and Chardi Kala stable. **Daya** — from the root "to feel another's pain" — is compassion as perception before action: the trained ability to register the other's suffering as data, which the langar then turns into lunch. The tradition's exemplar is Bhai Kanhaiya among the enemy wounded. **Nimrata** is humility with a spine — the fruit-laden branch that bends, the Guru who knelt before his own initiates. It is the antidote to the subtlest thief, pride, which can impersonate every other virtue. **Pyaar** is love as the summary virtue, the bani's final word on method: *those who love, find the One.*

A learner should notice the engineering: each virtue starves a specific thief — truth starves craving's fantasies, contentment starves greed, compassion starves anger, humility starves pride, love starves the loneliness that attachment mistakes itself for. The five are one strategy: a consciousness so well furnished that theft becomes impractical.
