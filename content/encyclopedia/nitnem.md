Nitnem — "the daily rule" — is the fixed cycle of prayers that structures a practising Sikh's day, and its architecture is a complete psychology of timekeeping: the day is fenced at both ends with remembrance, so that whatever fills the middle is contained by it.

**Morning** (amrit vela, ideally the still hours before dawn, after rising and bathing): **Japji Sahib**, Guru Nanak's foundational meditation; **Jaap Sahib**, the tenth Guru's cascade of the divine names; **Tav-Prasad Savaiye**, his ten quatrains dismantling ritualism; and by the Rehat Maryada's schedule, **Chaupai Sahib** and the first five stanzas with the fortieth of **Anand Sahib**. **Evening** (sunset): **Rehras Sahib**, the gathered hymns of the day's gate — So Dar's court of creation among them — prayed at the hinge when work releases its grip. **Night** (before sleep): **Kirtan Sohila**, the short, starlit composition that contemplates the lamp, the wedding, and death without fear — the tradition's lullaby, also recited at funerals, the same words for sleep and for the longer sleep, which is its quiet teaching.

Each session closes with **Ardas**. Amritdhari Sikhs take the discipline as a vow of initiation; many other Sikhs keep what they can — Japji on the commute, Rehras with the family, the apps and live-streams of the digital sangat counting millions of daily recitations.

The point, the tradition insists, is not quota but **saturation**: bani recited daily for decades stops being text and becomes the furniture of the mind — the lines surfacing unbidden at the diagnosis, the betrayal, the birth. Nitnem is how the Granth gets memorized into the nervous system, one ordinary dawn at a time.
