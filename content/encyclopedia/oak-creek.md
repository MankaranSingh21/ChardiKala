On Sunday morning, 5 August 2012, as the sangat of Oak Creek, Wisconsin prepared langar, a white supremacist and army veteran steeped in the racist music scene walked into the gurdwara and opened fire. He murdered six worshippers that day — Paramjit Kaur, Satwant Singh Kaleka (the gurdwara's president, who died charging the gunman with a butter knife), Prakash Singh, Sita Singh, Ranjit Singh, and Suveg Singh; a seventh, Baba Punjab Singh, paralyzed by a head wound, died of his injuries in 2020. Lieutenant **Brian Murphy**, first officer on scene, was shot fifteen times and survived; asked later why he waved the ambulances toward the sangat first, he answered that it was just what you do.

It was, at the time, the deadliest attack on a religious congregation in modern American history, and it landed on a community already eleven years into the post-9/11 wave of misdirected hatred. What Oak Creek did next became the reason the massacre is studied rather than merely mourned. The community reopened the gurdwara within days, scrubbed and repainted by its own youth; held its grief in public, inviting the town in; and pointedly enlarged the langar — the answer to a man who had come to a free kitchen with a gun was more free kitchen. **Pardeep Singh Kaleka**, son of the murdered president, eventually co-founded an organization against violent extremism with **Arno Michaelis** — a former leader of the very skinhead movement that had produced the killer — and the two now teach forgiveness as a discipline, together.

The phrase the community put on its banners that August was not new. It was the oldest one: *sarbat da bhala*.
