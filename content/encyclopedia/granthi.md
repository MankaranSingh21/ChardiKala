The granthi is the custodian and reader of the Guru Granth Sahib in a gurdwara: the one who performs the morning **prakash** (the ceremonial opening) and evening **sukhasan** (the rest), takes the **hukam** (the day's command, read from a random opening), reads the scripture for the sangat, and typically conducts ceremonies and teaches children their letters and their bani. The first was **Baba Buddha**, appointed by Guru Arjan at Harmandir Sahib in 1604 — a farmer of immense sanctity and no priestly pedigree whatsoever, which set the office's terms permanently.

What the granthi is *not* defines Sikhi's clerical theory. Not a priest: no sacrament requires granthi hands — any competent initiated Sikh, **woman or man**, may perform every rite the granthi performs, from naming to funeral; the granthi exists because congregations need a dedicated reader and caretaker, not because heaven requires an intermediary. Not an ordained caste: there is no apostolic line, no exclusive consecration — training (often at taksals or SGPC institutions) confers competence, not power. And not, ideally, an authority above the sangat: the granthi serves the institution governed by the congregation's committee, a relationship whose frictions — underpaid granthis, imperious committees — are the community's standing administrative penance.

Honest accounting includes the gap between theory and staffing: the profession is, in practice, overwhelmingly male, and the community's reformers press the point with the tradition's own documents in hand. The office's dignity, though, remains what 1604 made it: the person whose daily work is *proximity to the Guru* — first to the scripture at dawn, last at night, the keeper of the book that keeps the community.
