Before he was Guru Angad he was Bhai Lehna, a devout worshipper of the goddess Durga who heard a hymn of Guru Nanak's sung in his village and rode to Kartarpur to see its author. He stayed. The service he gave there became proverbial — carrying soaked grass at midnight in the rain, doing without question the tasks the Guru's own sons thought beneath them. When Guru Nanak chose his successor, he chose Lehna and renamed him **Angad** — from *ang*, limb: "of my own body."

His great structural gift to Sikhi was the script. He took the existing letterforms of the Punjab traders' hands, regularised and completed them, and made **Gurmukhi** — "from the Guru's mouth" — the vessel of the scripture. The choice was political in the deepest sense: scripture would live in the people's own tongue and alphabet, not in a priestly language requiring a priestly class to unlock it. Literacy itself became a devotional act; he opened schools for children and insisted adults learn too.

He also institutionalised the body. Wrestling akharas were attached to his sangat — care of physical health as part of spiritual practice, an idea that still shapes Sikh culture.

And the langar grew. His wife, **Mata Khivi**, ran the kitchen with such open-handedness that she is the only Guru's wife mentioned by name in the Guru Granth Sahib, where the langar is called hers: a kitchen like a leafy tree, giving shade to all. Between the script and the kitchen, the second Guru built the two technologies — literacy and lunch — on which everything else in Sikh history runs.
