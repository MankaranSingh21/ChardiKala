Dastar bandi — "the tying of the turban" — is the ceremony at which a Sikh youth (typically a boy at the threshold of adolescence, though the rite is performed for daughters who adopt the dastar too) is formally given the turban before the Guru Granth Sahib and the sangat: an elder or granthi winds the first full dastar over the topknot that has been, until now, covered by the child's patka, while kirtan is sung; ardas names the young Sikh; the sangat's jaikara confirms the promotion. It is the tradition's coming-of-age: not a graduation into privileges but into **legibility** — from this morning, the world will read this head as a Sikh, with everything the reading invites.

The ceremony is old in the houses of the Gurus (the tradition remembers turban-tyings at the successions) and deliberately simple in the Rehat Maryada's era: no fixed age, no fee, no priestly requirement — a family, a length of cloth, the Granth, and the community as witness. Its weight is entirely in what the cloth means: the entries on the dastar and the kakaar carry the doctrine — sovereignty issued to commoners, findability accepted as duty — and dastar bandi is the day a particular child signs for the consignment.

Diaspora families have, if anything, raised the ceremony's stakes and tenderness: tying the dastar on a boy who will wear it into a Western schoolyard is an act of considered courage by the whole household, and the celebrations — halls rented, grandmothers flown in — have the proportion of the commitment. The tradition's view of the trade has not changed in three centuries: the crown costs; wear it anyway; you will never be lost in a crowd, and neither will anyone looking for help.
