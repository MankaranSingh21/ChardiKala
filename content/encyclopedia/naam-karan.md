When a Sikh child is born, the family comes — at no fixed deadline; when mother and child are well — to the gurdwara, with gratitude and usually with karah prashad. The ceremony is brief and its mechanism is the tradition in miniature: after kirtan and ardas, the Guru Granth Sahib is opened **at random**, and the day's hukam read. The **first letter of the first word** of that hukam is announced to the family, who choose a name beginning with it — proposed on the spot or after deliberation; the granthi or any Sikh present proclaims it to the sangat's jaikara. To the name is added **Singh** for a boy, **Kaur** for a girl — the 1699 surnames that made every Sikh child caste-illegible at the registry.

The rite's theology repays a look. The random opening means the child is, in the tradition's accounting, **named by the Guru** — the first of a lifetime of hukams taken at thresholds — and the family's freedom within the letter keeps the partnership honest: guidance divine, choice human. Sikh names themselves carry the egalitarian design: overwhelmingly **unisex** (Harpreet, Jasleen, Amarjit serve all), built from the bani's vocabulary — love, light, victory, remembrance — so that a Sikh's name is typically a two-syllable sermon. Kaur deserves its own footnote: a woman keeps it for life, never exchanging it at marriage — sovereignty non-transferable.

No baptismal regeneration is claimed — the child needs no rescue, arriving already carrying the light — and the ceremony ends as everything ends: anand sahib's closing stanzas, ardas with the newcomer's name in it, prashad, and the langar, where the newest member of the sangat attends their first meal asleep.
