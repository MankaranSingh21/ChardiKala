Deep Singh's first vocation was the pen. An early initiate of Guru Gobind Singh's Khalsa, he worked with the scholar Bhai Mani Singh at Damdama Sahib on the final recension of the Guru Granth Sahib, and tradition credits him with preparing four of the great hand-copied volumes sent to the takhts — years of a master calligrapher's labour in the scripture's service. He headed the **Damdami Taksal**, the seminary of scriptural learning, and spent decades as its teaching elder: by every measure, the tradition's image of the scholar.

In 1757, Ahmad Shah Durrani's forces, retreating with plunder, demolished Harmandir Sahib and filled the sacred pool with refuse and slaughtered cattle. The news reached Damdama. Deep Singh — **seventy-five years old** — drew a line on the ground with his staff, and those who crossed it (five thousand, swelling as they marched) vowed with him to reach the sarovar or not return. Near Tarn Taran the Afghan force met them. The tradition's account of what followed is its most famous image of impossible resolve: mortally wounded in the neck, the old scholar fought on — supporting his head, by the iconic telling, with one hand — until he fell within the precincts of Harmandir Sahib, his vow kept by metres.

The spot is marked inside the complex today, and his double identity is the point the tradition presses: the hand that held the head had spent its life holding a pen. Sant-sipahi is usually explained as saint-then-soldier; Baba Deep Singh is its starkest case — the librarian as last line of defence, and the scripture's copyist dying to reclaim the scripture's house.
