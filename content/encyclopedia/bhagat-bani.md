Open the Guru Granth Sahib at random and you may find yourself reading not a Sikh Guru but a fourteenth-century Muslim Sufi, or a Hindu weaver, or a leather-worker whose touch his society called pollution. Fifteen **bhagats** — devotional saints from across India and across three centuries — have their hymns set inside Sikh scripture with the same canonical authority as the Gurus' own: Kabir (over 500 hymns), Sheikh Farid, Ravidas, Namdev, Dhanna the farmer, Sadhna the butcher, Sain the barber, Trilochan, Jaidev, Pipa, Ramanand, Parmanand, Surdas, Bhikhan, and Beni.

This was an editorial decision, made twice. Guru Nanak, tradition holds, collected hymns on his travels; Guru Arjan, compiling the canon in 1604, deliberately retained and arranged them — placing each bhagat's compositions within the raags, after the Gurus', with their names honoured in the headings. Where a bhagat's view diverged from Gurmat, the Gurus sometimes answered in adjacent verses — a canonised *conversation*, disagreement preserved inside scripture rather than edited out of it.

The selection has a visible center of gravity: a large share of the bhagats came from communities branded low or untouchable, and their hymns say so plainly. Ravidas sings of a city without sorrow where caste has no purchase; Kabir mocks the pundit and the mullah with equal affection; Farid's saloks of old age and humility are among the tenderest lines in the whole Granth, in Punjabi older than the Gurus' own.

For this platform's purposes, the Bhagat Bani is the standing answer to a frequent question: is Sikh universalism a modern gloss? No. It is bound into the spine of the scripture, where it has sat for four hundred years, in the handwriting of the Gurus themselves.
