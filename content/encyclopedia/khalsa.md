Khalsa derives from a revenue term of the Mughal empire: *khalisa* land was crown land — held directly by the sovereign, no intermediary landlords. Guru Gobind Singh's choice of the word was exact. The Khalsa is the community that belongs **directly** to the divine sovereign: no priesthood above it, no masand tax-farmers (he abolished them), no broker of any kind between the Sikh and the Guru. *Khalsa mero roop hai khaas* — the Khalsa is my own image — tradition records him saying; and at the first initiation he knelt to receive amrit from his initiates, making the circle of authority a genuine circle.

Membership is by **choice, marked visibly**. The initiate takes amrit, adopts the five kakaar, and accepts a discipline of dawn prayer and ethical conduct; men take the name Singh, women Kaur — lion and sovereign — names that overwrote caste surnames at a stroke and remain the most successful anti-caste technology ever deployed in South Asia. The visibility is the point and the price: a Khalsa Sikh cannot blend into a crowd, cannot quietly deny the faith under pressure, cannot watch injustice anonymously. The uniform makes the wearer findable — by the persecutor, and by the person who needs help and scans the crowd for a turban.

The Khalsa is also a constitutional body. Vested jointly with the Granth in 1708 (**Guru Panth**), it deliberates through sarbat khalsa assemblies and gurmatta resolutions; its ideal civic form is the sant-sipahi.

A clarification for new learners: not every Sikh is amritdhari (initiated), and Sikhi's door — sangat, langar, the Guru's teaching — stands open regardless. The Khalsa is not a tier of salvation. It is the order of those who have signed the ship's articles; the harbour remains free.
