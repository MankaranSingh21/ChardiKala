The Guru Granth Sahib states its anthropology in a family metaphor: **ek pita ekas ke ham barik** — one Father, and we, children of the One. Elsewhere the image is light: the same divine light in every creature, *sabh meh jot jot hai soi*. From this single premise, Sikhi derives its entire social program, the way geometry derives from axioms.

If one light, then **no caste**: Guru Nanak's hymns mock the pollution rules at their root — what is impure about a body the One built? The Gurus seated all castes in one row, baptized five castes from one bowl in 1699, and issued surnames (Singh, Kaur) that deleted clan markers. If one light, then **no chosen people**: the scripture canonises Muslim and Hindu saints as equals, and the daily prayer closes asking welfare for *all*, not for Sikhs. If one light, then **no holy war against persons**: the Mul Mantar's One is *nirvair*, without enmity, so enmity can never be holy — Sikh arms are licensed against oppression, never against peoples or faiths as such. And if one light, the stranger is a misperception: Guru Nanak's *na koi bairi nahi begana* — no enemy, no stranger — is sung in gurdwaras as a daily recalibration of the eyes.

The tradition proved the doctrine in its costliest currency. Guru Tegh Bahadur's head fell for the worship-rights of Hindus; Bhai Kanhaiya's water went to wounded Mughals; the langar feeds the policeman sent against the protest.

Where Sikhs have fallen short of this — and caste prejudice among Sikhs, in marriage and gurdwara politics, is a documented, ongoing failure — the doctrine indicts the failure rather than excusing it. That is what axioms are for. The oneness is not a Sikh achievement to advertise but a Sikh obligation, permanently senior to Sikh behaviour.
