Japji Sahib opens the Guru Granth Sahib and opens every Sikh's day: it is recited at **amrit vela**, the still hours before dawn, by observant Sikhs the world over. It is not addressed to a congregation and was never set to raag — it is a meditation, person to truth, and the tradition treats it as the distilled essence of Guru Nanak's entire teaching.

It begins with the **Mul Mantar** — the root statement of the One — and then immediately asks the question every spiritual tradition must answer: *how is truth obtained? how is the wall of falsehood broken?* Nanak's answer arrives in the first stanza and governs everything after: by walking in **hukam**, the divine order — not by ritual bathing, not by silence, not by accumulation, not by cleverness.

The 38 stanzas (pauris, "steps") climb deliberately. They dismantle ritualism and spiritual pride; they marvel at the uncountable scale of creation — countless worlds, countless ways of naming — in verses that read strikingly comfortably beside modern cosmology; they insist that the divine is written in nature rather than housed in temples. The famous twenty-seventh stanza, So Dar, pictures all of nature as a single court of musicians: wind, water, and fire singing, seasons singing, the whole cosmos in concert.

The final steps turn from wonder to work, naming the stages of spiritual development and ending at the forge: in the mint of discipline, with patience as the goldsmith and love as the crucible, character is *made* — hammered, not granted. The closing salok hands the whole world back to the reader: air the guru, water the father, earth the great mother, and all of us children playing in the lap of day and night, judged at last by our deeds.

Five to ten minutes to recite; a lifetime, Sikhs say, to mean it.
