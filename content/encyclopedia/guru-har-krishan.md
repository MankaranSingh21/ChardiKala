The eighth Guru received the responsibility at the age of five, and the tradition does not treat this as strange. Sikhi locates authority in the light, not the vessel; the question asked of a Guru is not how old, but how true.

Summoned to Delhi under imperial pressure, Guru Har Krishan arrived in a city dying of **smallpox**. What follows is the entire substance of his short guruship and one of the permanent images of Sikhi: a child moving through the lanes of the sick — Hindu, Muslim, casteless, all of them — bringing water, food, and care, while the healthy fled the quarters of the ill. The well at Bangla Sahib, where he stayed, is held by tradition to have given healing water to thousands.

He contracted the disease himself and died at eight years old, in 1664. His last words, **"Baba Bakale,"** pointed the community to its next Guru — his great-uncle Tegh Bahadur, living in obscurity in the village of Bakala.

Gurdwara **Bangla Sahib**, raised where he served, is today one of the largest free kitchens and free clinics in Delhi: tens of thousands fed daily, diagnostics offered at nominal cost to anyone, in direct continuation of a child's eight months of work. Among the Gurus he is the answer to a question modern readers often ask — what can one person possibly do, in so short a time, against so large a suffering? The tradition's reply: this much.
