Twice daily, every gurdwara performs the protocol that makes "living Guru" a practice rather than a phrase. At dawn: **prakash** — "illumination" — the Guru Granth Sahib carried from its rest, on a sevadar's head, in procession with the sangat's jaikaras, to the darbar; enthroned on the manji sahib under its canopy; robed in fresh rumalas; opened, and the day's first **hukam** taken — the random reading that gives sangat and granthi the morning's instruction. Through the day the Granth holds court — the chaur (whisk) waved over it, the old protocol of sovereignty — and is never left unattended. At night: **sukhasan** — "the posture of rest" — the Granth closed, wrapped, and carried with the same honours to its resting room (the sachkhand), often with Kirtan Sohila recited: the Guru retired for the night, the court adjourned.

The ceremonies are conducted in millions of homes too, wherever a household maintains a Granth with a proper room; families without one keep a **gutka** (prayer anthology) with proportionate respect. At Harmandir Sahib, prakash and sukhasan are the day's twin spectacles: the golden palki borne between the Akal Takht and the sanctum through crowds at four in the morning, the heaviest devotional traffic of the complex.

To a newcomer the protocol can read as worship of a book; the entry on the Granth-as-Guru addresses the theology, but the practice itself makes the cleanest argument. What the rites enact, precisely, is **personhood**: the Guru wakes, holds court, instructs, and rests. A community that has done this every dawn for three centuries does not *have* a scripture. It lives with one.
