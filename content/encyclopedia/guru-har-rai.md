Guru Har Rai kept twenty-two hundred horsemen and never once led them to war. That is the shape of his guruship: strength maintained, and entirely spent on protection, healing, and shade.

He maintained a renowned **herbarium and hospital** at Kiratpur, stocked with rare medicines from across Asia. When Dara Shikoh — son of the emperor Shah Jahan, the same dynasty that had tortured Guru Arjan — lay dying of poisoning, the request for a rare medicine came to Kiratpur, and the Guru sent it. Asked why he would save the house of his family's persecutors, his answer became a Sikh proverb: the seeker breaks flowers with one hand and offers them with the other, but the tree gives shade even to the axeman.

The tenderness was personal. The best-known story has him weeping as a young man because his robe, brushing through a garden, had broken blossoms — and pinning his clothes thereafter, walking carefully for the rest of his life. Modern Sikh environmentalism has adopted him as its patron: **Sikh Environment Day**, observed by thousands of gurdwaras worldwide through EcoSikh, falls on the anniversary of his gurgaddi.

His firmness was doctrinal. When his elder son Ram Rai, performing before the emperor Aurangzeb, altered one word of Guru Nanak's bani to flatter the court, Guru Har Rai refused ever to see him again and excluded him from the succession. A Guru who would medicine his enemies would not change a syllable of the truth for them: the two halves of his character are one teaching.
