In the autumn of 1708 at Nanded, on the Godavari in the Deccan, Guru Gobind Singh was stabbed by an assassin — the wound, half-healed, reopened fatally. What he did in his remaining hours was the most consequential succession decision in Sikh history, and its form was deliberately public and procedural: no deathbed whisper for factions to dispute.

He had the **Adi Granth** installed; he placed before it five coins and a coconut — the exact homage each preceding Guru had paid a successor — bowed, and declared the line of human Gurus closed. The Word was the Guru now: *Sabh Sikhan ko hukam hai, Guru maniyo Granth* — all Sikhs are commanded: know the Granth as Guru — with authority in the community vested as **Guru Panth**, the Khalsa deliberating in the Guru's presence.

The decision solved, permanently, the problems that had bled the community for two centuries: succession disputes (rival claimants had dogged nearly every transfer), the targeting of the Guru's person by empires (an empire cannot behead a scripture held in a million homes), and the gravitational drift of any faith toward its leader's cult. The tenth Guru had lost four sons; there was no dynasty to tempt him, and he had spent his life dismantling intermediaries — the masands abolished, the Khalsa initiated by its own five, himself initiated by his initiates. 1708 completed the architecture: a faith with a book for a sovereign and a congregation for a government.

He died on October 7, 1708. The community he left was leaderless by design and has never been leaderless since: the Guru sits, this morning as every morning, wherever the Granth is opened — including, as it happens, on this website.
