In the Diwali season, Sikhs keep **Bandi Chhor Divas** — the Day of the Liberator of Prisoners — and the story it carries is the tradition's theory of freedom in one scene. Guru Hargobind, imprisoned by Jahangir in **Gwalior Fort** (the empire's holding pen for inconvenient nobility), was granted release; he refused to walk out unless the **fifty-two rajas** held with him were freed too. The emperor's compromise was a courtier's trap: as many may leave as can hold your cloak. The Guru had a cloak made with **fifty-two hems** — and walked out of Gwalior with every prisoner attached. The fort's gurdwara is named for the deliverance; so is the day.

The festival's observance braids into Diwali's season — Amritsar's tradition says the Guru reached home at Diwali, and the **Golden Temple's illumination**, the lamps on the parikarma and the fireworks over the sarovar, is among the festival's oldest and most photographed forms (the historic phrase runs *Dal Roti Ghar Di, Diwali Amritsar Di* — bread at home, Diwali at Amritsar). Homes light divas, gurdwaras hold kirtan darbars, and the Sarbat Khalsa of the eighteenth century met at Amritsar each Diwali — the day already, then, the community's autumn assembly.

The teaching the day insists on distinguishes it from a festival of lights generically: the Guru's freedom was real only when it was **transferable** — liberation audited by how many others fit on the hem. Sikh institutions cite the cloak constantly (prisoner-aid charities are named for it), and the day functions as the annual examination question: this year, who left the fort holding yours?
