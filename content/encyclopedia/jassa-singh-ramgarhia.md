Jassa Singh Ramgarhia's surname is a fort and his caste-name became a community: a **Tarkhan** — carpenter — by birth in an order where that lineage was patronized as menial, he rose by pure capability to misl chieftaincy, and the artisan castes of Punjab who later adopted the name *Ramgarhia* for themselves took it from him: the man whose biography proved the Khalsa meant its erasure of birth-rank.

His name was made in 1748 at **Ram Rauni**, the mud fortress at Amritsar, where a few hundred Sikhs stood siege by the Lahore government. Jassa Singh — then in the governor's service, one of many Sikhs the era's politics had scattered across payrolls — abandoned the besiegers for the besieged, a defection at mortal risk that turned the defence; the rebuilt fort, Ramgarh, named him. His misl rooted in the Riarki tracts (Batala, Sri Hargobindpur), and his engineering eye — the carpenter persisting in the general — showed in the fortifications, bridges, and the buildings his line patronized.

He feuded famously with Ahluwalia and the Kanhaiyas — the misl era's fratricidal weather, which exiled him south of the Sutlej for years — and returned in strength for the great combinations: at the 1783 **Delhi** campaign, his column carried home the Mughal coronation slab from the Red Fort, hauled to Amritsar and laid — the gesture is the man — where it remains: not enthroned but at the **feet** of the Panth's authority, in the Ramgarhia Bunga by the sarovar. Emperors' marble, demoted to furniture, by a carpenter's son with a long memory. He died in 1803, his misl shortly absorbed into Ranjit Singh's consolidation — the confederate age ending, its proofs of social mobility permanent.
