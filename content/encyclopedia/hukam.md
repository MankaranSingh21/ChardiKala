Hukam — from the Arabic for command — is the Guru Granth Sahib's word for the divine order: the totality of how things actually are and unfold, from the orbits of moons to the arrival of grief. Japji Sahib's first stanza stakes the whole tradition on it. How is truth obtained, how is the wall of falsehood broken? **By walking in hukam** — by aligning with the order — and, the stanza adds, everything is within hukam; nothing is outside it.

It is crucial to hear what this does *not* mean. Hukam is not fatalism, and Sikh history is its loudest refutation — a people who accepted hukam and also built kingdoms, abolished customs, fought empires, and crossed oceans. The teaching distinguishes between the order and our arguments with it. Crops fail, bodies age, the beloved die: these are hukam, and raging at them is the ego demanding a private universe. But injustice, cruelty, caste — these are *human* arrangements, and confronting them is not resistance to hukam; it is obedience to it, since the order's deepest law is the one light in all.

The working Sikh art is telling the two apart. The tradition's tools are unglamorous: dawn recitation to start the day aligned rather than aggrieved; the phrase *bhana mannana* — accepting the will — said at funerals not as defeat but as the refusal to be poisoned by loss; and the test of action, asking not "do I like this?" but "what does this moment require of me?"

Modern readers sometimes notice the resemblance to Stoic *logos* or to serenity-prayer wisdom — change what can be changed, accept what cannot. The kinship is real. The Sikh addition is the temperature: hukam is not cold law but **sweet will** — *tera kia meetha lagai* — the words of a Guru on a burning plate.
