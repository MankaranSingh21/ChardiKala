Khalsa Aid was founded in 1999 by **Ravi Singh**, a British Sikh moved by news footage of Kosovo's refugee columns to test a proposition: that the langar — the Sikh kitchen that feeds anyone, anywhere, immediately — could be made *mobile*, and that the Ardas's closing line, **sarbat da bhala**, the welfare of all, was an operations order rather than a sentiment. The first convoy drove from Slough to the Albanian border. The organization has since worked in dozens of countries and most of the world's recent emergencies: earthquake Haiti and Nepal, flooded Pakistan and Kerala, Yazidi camps in Iraq, Rohingya camps in Bangladesh, Syria's borders, Yemen's ports, Ukraine's railway stations — and the unglamorous domestic ledger too: Grenfell Tower survivors, UK flood victims, homeless feeding runs, Punjab's drug-recovery and farmer-distress programs.

Its method is recognizably gurdwara-shaped: arrive early (its small teams are routinely among the first NGOs in), **feed at scale first** (hot meals and water purification as the spearhead, the langar instinct), source locally, hand to whoever is hungry with no registration of creed — the Bhai Kanhaiya protocol, which the organization cites by name: on the field, only the Guru's face, in everyone. Its funding is the diaspora's dasvandh, crowdsourced at speed when the news breaks.

The organization's visibility — turbaned volunteers ladling dal in places cameras go — has made it, half-intentionally, the most effective ambassador modern Sikhi has: for millions, the first Sikhs they could name. The community's pride in it is proprietary and precise: not that Sikhs are charitable, but that the *system worked as designed* — five centuries of kitchen drills, deployed.
