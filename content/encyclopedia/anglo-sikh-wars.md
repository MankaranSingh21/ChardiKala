Six years after Ranjit Singh's death, his state had burned through four rulers; his magnificent army, the Khalsa Fauj, had become a praetorian power its court feared; and the East India Company had massed troops and boats along the Sutlej in a posture nobody mistook. The First Anglo-Sikh War (1845–46) turned on a fact Sikh soldiers learned mid-battle: their own commanders — **Lal Singh and Tej Singh**, court creatures with private understandings with the Company — repeatedly withheld reserves, abandoned the field, and at **Sobraon** left the army's back to a flooded river with the bridge cut. The soldiery fought with a stubbornness the British official dispatches record with something near awe; the battles of Mudki, Ferozeshah (where the Company came as close to destruction as it ever did in India), and Sobraon filled British war memorials at home.

The peace was a dismemberment: indemnity, the Jalandhar Doab taken, a Resident installed — and **Kashmir sold** to the Company's ally Gulab Singh Dogra for 7.5 million rupees, a transaction whose consequences the subcontinent still administers. The regent Maharani **Jind Kaur**, too capable for the arrangement, was separated from her son and imprisoned.

The Second War (1848–49) began with rebellion at Multan and ended at Gujrat after **Chillianwala** — a mauling the British remembered for a generation. With it came annexation: the boy Maharaja **Duleep Singh** signed away his kingdom and the Koh-i-Noor at ten years old. The army that had nearly broken the Company was disbanded — and then, within a decade, recruited wholesale into it: the colonizer's tribute, in its way, to the only Indian army it never wished to fight twice.
