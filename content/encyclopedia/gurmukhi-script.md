Gurmukhi — "from the mouth of the Guru" — is the script in which the Guru Granth Sahib is written and in which Punjabi is written in India today. Its thirty-five core letters (hence the affectionate name *painti*, "the thirty-five") with later additions, its vowel signs, and its disciplined left-to-right horizontality were standardized by **Guru Angad**, the second Guru, working from letterforms already current among Punjab's traders and scribes.

Why a Guru would spend his short tenure on an alphabet is the real lesson. In the sixteenth-century Punjab, sacred knowledge lived in Sanskrit and Arabic — languages of trained elites, requiring a priestly intermediary between an ordinary person and their own scripture. Guru Angad's standardization was a deliberate severing of that dependency: the hymns would be written in the language people actually spoke, in letters a farmer could learn. The Gurus ran schools to teach it; literacy became seva. Five hundred years before "access" became a design principle, the second Guru made the user interface of revelation as simple as he could.

Technically, Gurmukhi is an abugida: consonants carry an inherent vowel, modified by signs above, below, and beside. It is almost perfectly phonetic — words are pronounced as written, which is why the transliterations on this site's Gurbani reader can guide a learner so directly. Three letters were added later for sounds from Persian and other borrowings; subscript forms preserve old conjuncts in sacred text.

For heritage learners, Gurmukhi is often the first homecoming: the moment the wall of script between you and your grandmother's prayers comes down. It was engineered, from the beginning, to come down easily.
