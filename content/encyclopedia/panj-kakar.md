Since Vaisakhi 1699, initiated Sikhs have worn five articles, each beginning with the letter *kakka* — hence the Five Ks. Together they form a uniform whose every element argues something.

**Kes** — unshorn hair. Acceptance of the body as issued; the form in which nature, and nature's One, made the human. Around the kes wraps the dastar (turban), its crown and protector. **Kangha** — a small wooden comb worn in the hair. Order and care: the kes is not neglected but groomed; Sikhi's acceptance of nature is stewardship, never abandonment. The renunciant's matted locks said *I have left the world*; the combed kes says *I am staying, and keeping it tidy.* **Kara** — an iron or steel bracelet on the dominant wrist. A circle without beginning or end on the hand that acts: the reminder, exactly where you will see it mid-deed, that the hand is the One's instrument and accountable. Iron, not gold — the Khalsa's metal is the worker's and the smith's. **Kachhera** — practical cotton breeches: readiness and self-restraint, the garment of a person who may need to act at a moment's notice and governs the body's appetites meanwhile. **Kirpan** — the blade of mercy: from *kirpa*, grace. Worn not as weapon but as oath — that the wearer stands between the defenceless and harm, with force as the last resort after all others fail.

The deepest function is what a uniform always does: it makes the wearer **accountable in public**. Marked unmistakably, a Khalsa Sikh cannot sin anonymously or hide from duty in a crowd — and for three centuries, people in danger have known what the visible Sikh signifies. Five small objects; one legible promise.
