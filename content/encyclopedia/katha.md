Beside kirtan, which sings the bani, sits **katha** — which explains it: the gurdwara's tradition of live exposition, in which a kathavachak (expounder) takes a hymn, an episode of the Gurus' lives, or a question of practice, and unfolds it for the sangat — grammar, context, story, application — in the vernacular, at length, with the Granth open before them. If kirtan is the tradition's liturgy, katha is its continuing-education department, and a full gurdwara program runs both: the truth sung, then unpacked.

The form is as old as the faith — Guru Nanak's discourses with yogis and qazis are katha's prototypes, Bhai Gurdas's vaars its first study guides, Bhai Mani Singh its martyred master-teacher — and its methods span a spectrum: the Damdami Taksal's line-by-line grammatical santhya; the sakhi-rich village style that teaches doctrine through story (the bleeding bread, the cobra's shade); the modern university-inflected katha of scholars with slides; and the diaspora's English-language katha, without which the tradition's grandchildren would meet the Granth as beautiful sound and closed meaning.

Its institutional weight is real: katha tradition is how a scripture in medieval Punjabi and braj remains *legible* to its community across five centuries of language drift — a distributed, never-adjourned commentary, transmitted teacher to teacher. The digital sangat has multiplied it beyond any historical scale: recorded katha is the bulk of Sikh online media, and a learner anywhere can now hear the same hymn expounded by ten schools in three languages — argument and all, since kathavachaks dispute interpretations freely. The tradition regards the disputation as healthy and always has: the Granth, after all, canonised its own debates.
