Facing Harmandir Sahib across the plaza, close enough that each is always in the other's sight, stands the **Akal Takht** — the Throne of the Timeless One — raised by Guru Hargobind in 1606 as the seat of miri, worldly authority, opposite the house of piri, prayer. The geometry is the constitution: in Sikhi the spiritual and the temporal answer to each other daily, at a distance of one courtyard, and neither may close its shutters to the other.

From its platform the sixth Guru heard disputes, received envoys, and reviewed horses and arms; from it, across four centuries, the Panth's collective will has been formally spoken: **hukamnamas** (edicts binding on Sikhs everywhere), the convening of Sarbat Khalsa assemblies, honours conferred and — rarely, gravely — penance imposed: Maharaja Ranjit Singh, emperor of Punjab at his height, was summoned, sentenced to the lash, and bared his back before it; the pardon that followed mattered less than the submission. The Takht's custodian, the **Jathedar**, speaks with an authority that is moral and procedural rather than papal — the throne's occupant is the Panth, not the appointee; the community's modern arguments about how Jathedars are chosen and removed are arguments about exactly this.

It is one of five takhts, and first among them. In June 1984, Operation Blue Star's tanks reduced it to rubble — of all the operation's wounds, the one Sikhs name first. The government's hasty reconstruction was **torn down by the sangat** as tainted, and the Takht rebuilt again by kar seva, voluntary hands and sangat money alone, gold-domed by donation. A throne, the rebuilding said, cannot be repaired by the power that broke it.
