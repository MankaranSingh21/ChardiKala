Born Ramji Das in 1904, converted to Sikhi by the open arithmetic of the langar (the temples of his boyhood fed Brahmins first; the gurdwara fed everyone, and the boy noticed), Puran Singh made his life's vocation out of what cities discard. It began at Lahore's Gurdwara Dehra Sahib in 1934 with a four-year-old left at the doorstep: paralyzed, incontinent, mute — **Piara Singh**, "beloved." For the better part of fourteen years, lacking any institution, Puran Singh *was* the institution: he carried Piara on his back — through Lahore, through Partition's refugee columns into Amritsar — the man with the boy on his back becoming, in Punjab's memory, what a saint looks like when the theory is removed.

In 1947, amid the largest refugee catastrophe on earth, he founded **Pingalwara** — "home of the broken" — in Amritsar: for the abandoned insane, the dying destitute, the limbless, the newborn left on its steps. He funded it by standing at the gurdwara gates with a tin; he staffed it substantially with its own recovered; he buried, washed, and fed with his own hands into his eighties. It shelters and treats thousands today.

His second prophecy got less attention at the time: decades before the vocabulary arrived, his hand-press pamphlets — printed on waste paper; he gathered paper obsessively, mended his own sandals, planted trees compulsively — warned Punjab in detail about deforestation, water tables, exhaust, and the costs of the chemical harvest. The environmental movement of EcoSikh's era cites him as ancestor. He died in 1992, his tin cup now in a museum — the rarest of saints: audited, replicable, and right twice.
