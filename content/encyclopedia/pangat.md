Pangat means the row — the line of diners seated together on the langar floor — and it is the most photographed idea in Sikhi: thousands of people of every description sitting at exactly the same height, eating exactly the same food, served by volunteers who will sit in the next row themselves.

The height is the point. In the caste order the Gurus confronted, *who may eat with whom* was the master question — commensality was the wall that kept hierarchy standing. Hindu, Muslim, twice-born, untouchable: the rules of the cooked meal enforced the whole architecture. The Gurus did not write a pamphlet against this. They built a floor. On it, food cooked by anyone is served to everyone seated anywhere, and the act of eating together does what no sermon could: it makes the participants *complicit in equality* three times a day.

Guru Amar Das gave the principle its teeth with the rule remembered as **pehle pangat, pichhe sangat** — first the row, then the audience. No one saw the Guru without first eating on the floor with whoever else had come. The emperor Akbar sat in the line; that is precisely why the story is told. Status could be checked at the door or the visit could be cancelled; there was no third option.

Pangat survives as more than mealtime mechanics. It is the Sikh template for institutional design: the same-level principle reappears in the gurdwara (no reserved seating, sangat on one floor), in the title-less honourifics (every man Singh, every woman Kaur), and in the modern langars at disaster sites and protest lines, where the row forms again wherever the need is. Sikhi's social theory fits in four words: sit down together, eat.
