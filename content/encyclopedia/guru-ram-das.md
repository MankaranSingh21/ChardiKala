Ram Das — born Jetha, an orphan who sold boiled grain in the lanes of Lahore — joined the sangat of Guru Amar Das as a young man and served it for decades, eventually marrying the Guru's daughter Bibi Bhani. When tested against the other candidates for the succession (the trials are remembered as exercises in ego: building platforms only to be told to tear them down and build again), he was the one who rebuilt without resentment, and in 1574 he became the fourth Guru.

His monument is a city. He acquired land and excavated a great pool — the **Amrit Sarovar**, pool of nectar — inviting merchants, artisans, and farmers of every community to settle around it. The town was called Ramdaspur; the world now calls it **Amritsar**, after the water. The decision to build the Sikh centre as an open commercial city rather than a cloister says everything about the tradition's direction: holiness in the middle of the market, not away from it.

He composed 638 hymns, among them the four **Lavan** verses around which every Sikh wedding still revolves — the couple circling the Guru Granth Sahib as the stanzas trace the soul's deepening union. That a Guru wrote the wedding liturgy, and that the ceremony's name is **Anand Karaj**, "the work of bliss," reflects his insistence that the householder's life — marriage, work, children — is the field where Sikhi is actually practised. Before his death he passed the guruship to his youngest son, Arjan, in whose hands the pool would receive its temple.
