The Janamsakhis — "birth testimonies" — are the traditional narrative cycles of Guru Nanak's life, composed and copied in the generations after him. They are not scripture, and the tradition knows the difference precisely: the Guru Granth Sahib is the Guru; the Janamsakhis are the family album, with all the warmth and embroidery family albums have.

Their stories are how most Sikhs first meet Guru Nanak as children. The boy whose herded buffaloes wandered into a field, and the ruined crop found miraculously whole. The cobra spreading its hood to shade the sleeping child. The **Sacha Sauda** — the "true bargain" — in which young Nanak, given money by his father for trade, spends it feeding hungry ascetics and reports the investment a success, a story so central that Sikhs named institutions after it. The hard-hearted rich man's feast refused, and bread taken instead at the carpenter Bhai Lalo's table — wrung in the Guru's hands, the rich man's bread dripping blood, the worker's dripping milk. At Mecca, the sleeping Guru scolded for pointing his feet toward the holy house, answering: turn my feet where God is not.

Scholars note that the cycles (Bala, Puratan, Miharban and others) differ, grew over time, and reflect the devotion of their writers as much as documentary fact; serious Sikh historiography reads them critically. But their *teaching* content is in perfect tune with the bani: every famous sakhi is a parable against empty ritual, caste pride, or hoarded wealth, and for honest work and the equality of eaters at one floor. As pedagogy they have worked for five centuries. Ask any Sikh child what bread bled in the Guru's hand, and you will get the whole economics of Sikhi in one image.
