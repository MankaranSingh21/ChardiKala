During the battles around Anandpur, Sikh soldiers brought Guru Gobind Singh a complaint of treason: Bhai Kanhaiya, who carried the water-skin, was moving across the field after each engagement giving water to the **wounded of both sides** — Sikh defenders and the very Mughal and hill-army attackers who had been killing them an hour before. Some of the revived enemies, the soldiers protested, were returning to fight again.

Summoned, Kanhaiya gave the answer that became one of the permanent sentences of Sikhi: *I saw no Mughals and no Sikhs on the field. I saw only the Guru's face in every one of them.* He was, he pointed out, only practising what the Guru's own bani taught — the same light in all. Guru Gobind Singh's ruling completed the lesson: he blessed Kanhaiya, and — by the tradition's account — gave him **ointment as well**, instructing him to bind the wounds of all he watered. The complaint had asked the Guru to discipline compassion; the Guru promoted it.

Kanhaiya was already a figure of standing — a disciple of the ninth Guru, founder of the **Sevapanthi** order, whose deras of quiet service (wells, rest-houses, healing) continue in his lineage. But it is the water-skin at Anandpur that made him the tradition's patron of impartial humanitarianism: the Red Cross story seventeen decades before the Red Cross — and Sikh aid organizations cite him as their charter. When **Khalsa Aid** trucks water into a war zone for whoever is thirsty, the manifest, spiritually speaking, is signed Kanhaiya.

The episode also fixed the sant-sipahi's internal balance: the same Guru who built an army ruled, when they conflicted, for the water-carrier.
