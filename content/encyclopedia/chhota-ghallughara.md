By the 1740s the Sikhs of Punjab lived under standing extermination orders — prices on heads, rewards graded by scalp, grain, and information; the daily Ardas's catalogue of those "cut joint by joint" is the memory of these years. In 1746 the policy became a campaign. After Jaspat Rai, brother of the Lahore minister **Lakhpat Rai**, was killed in a clash with a Sikh band, Lakhpat Rai swore the community's erasure — the chronicles record his vow to wipe the name of the Sikhs from the page of existence — and turned the apparatus of the province to it.

Sikhs in Lahore were arrested and executed; the sacred pool at Amritsar was defiled; and the field army drove the main body of the Khalsa — fighters encumbered with families — against the flooded Ravi in the Kahnuwan marshes near Gurdaspur. Trapped between water and cavalry, an estimated **seven to ten thousand** were killed, and thousands more captured were marched to Lahore and beheaded at the horse-market, at the site Sikhs keep today as **Shaheedganj**.

The community's response codified its survival doctrine. The remnant withdrew to the Malwa scrub and the hills, regrouped under Nawab Kapur Singh's Dal Khalsa structure, and within two years convened at Amritsar to reorganize as the misls — answering a campaign of erasure with an act of *administration*. The name itself is the tradition's measure of endurance: this massacre of thousands is the **chhota** — the *lesser* — Ghallughara, because sixteen years later came a greater one. A people who rank their holocausts and outlive their rankers had, by 1799, taken Lahore.
