Every Sikh prayer — whatever the occasion, the petition, the grief or the joy that prompted it — ends in the same four words: **sarbat da bhala**, the welfare of all. The placement is the theology. After remembering its own martyrs, after asking for its own protection, the community's standing instruction is to close the ledger by asking good for everyone — *sarbat*, the whole of existence, with no clause limiting it to the faithful, the deserving, or the human.

This is the Sikh answer to the oldest temptation of religious communities: the salvation in-group. Sikhi's prayer does not ask that everyone become Sikh; it asks that everyone be *well* — which is a different and much larger request. The roots run to the Mul Mantar (a One without enmity can have no out-group) and to Guru Nanak's formula *na koi Hindu, na koi Musalman*: the labels dissolve before the One, so welfare cannot be rationed by them.

It functions as the community's mission statement in the most literal way. Khalsa Aid, the global humanitarian organisation, prints it as its founding line and acts it out in Yemen, Ukraine, Haiti — aid keyed to need, never to creed. The langar is sarbat da bhala as a kitchen. The farmers' protest feeding the police sent to disperse it was sarbat da bhala under stress-test. Guru Tegh Bahadur dying for the worship-rights of a religion not his own is its outer limit: *all* turns out to include those whose prayers differ from yours, at the price of your head.

For readers who carry no faith at all, note carefully: you are inside the blessing's scope. Sarbat means you. The prayer was always asking for your welfare too — which is, perhaps, the gentlest possible introduction to what this tradition thinks religion is *for*.
