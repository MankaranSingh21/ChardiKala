Mata Khivi married Bhai Lehna years before he became Guru Angad, and when the guruship moved to Khadur Sahib she took charge of the community's kitchen — and built it from a teacher's table into the **institution** that has defined Sikhi ever since. Under her hand the langar at Khadur became famous across the Majha: rich kheer of rice and milk served to whoever walked in, prepared and distributed with such open-handed warmth that the kitchen was called hers in her lifetime and has been, in a sense, every Sikh kitchen since.

The scripture's testimony is the extraordinary part. In the vaar of Satta and Balwand preserved in the Guru Granth Sahib, she is praised **by name** — *Balwand Khivi nek jan, jis bahuti chhau patrali*: Khivi, the noble person, a thickly leafed tree under whose shade all may rest; in whose langar the kheer flows like ambrosia. She is the only one of the Gurus' wives named in the Granth, and the deed for which scripture names her is institutional hospitality — a canonization of kitchen management that tells you precisely what this tradition counts as glory.

Her significance compounds across the categories of this encyclopedia. As the langar's architect she ranks among Sikhi's institutional founders. As a woman wielding public authority over the community's central institution in the 1540s, she is the practical proof of the Gurus' gender teaching. And as the answer to the question "what should power look like?" the tradition offers her image without irony: a tree, with shade, and food underneath it. She served through four guruships, dying in 1582 — the langar already too large, by then, for any one lifetime to contain.
