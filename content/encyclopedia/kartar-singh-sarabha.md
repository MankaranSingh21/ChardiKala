Kartar Singh of Sarabha village, Ludhiana, reached San Francisco in 1912, a boy of sixteen aimed at the University of California; what he enrolled in instead was the awakening of the Pacific coast's Punjabis. Within a year he was the youngest engine of the **Ghadar Party** — hand-setting the Gurmukhi type of the *Ghadar* weekly in the Yugantar Ashram, writing its verses, the teenager whom the movement's greybeards deferred to for sheer voltage.

When the 1914 war opened the door, he was among the first home — and for months the rising's most reckless organizer: touring the cantonments seeding mutiny in the regiments, manufacturing bombs, robbing (the term in the trials was decoity; the funds were for revolution) — a campaign betrayed from inside before its February 1915 date. Captured, he conducted himself at the **Lahore Conspiracy trial** as if the dock were a platform: claiming his acts in full, correcting the prosecution's understatements, declining the defence the court found itself half-offering an obviously brilliant nineteen-year-old. The judgment called him the most dangerous of the accused. His reply to the sentence became Punjabi proverb: hang me quickly, that I may be **reborn the sooner** to take up the work again. He went to the rope at Lahore on 16 November 1915, nineteen years old, his weight, the lore adds, increased — he had been gaining, cheerful to the end.

His photograph lived for the next two decades in the pocket of **Bhagat Singh**, who called him guru, brother, and companion, and was hanged in the same city at the nearly same age. Punjab's mothers still sing the ballad: *the boy from Sarabha village.*
