Harmandir Sahib — the Temple of God, called Darbar Sahib by Sikhs and the Golden Temple by the world — stands in the middle of the Amrit Sarovar at Amritsar, reached by a causeway, ringed by white marble parikarma, and sheathed in the gold leaf Maharaja Ranjit Singh gave it. Its architecture is a sermon delivered in stone. Where temples of its era climbed — sanctity as altitude — Guru Arjan built it **low**: a visitor steps *down* into the complex, humility enforced by stairs. Where shrines guarded a single auspicious entrance, it opens on **four sides** — all four castes, all four directions, anyone. The Sufi saint Mian Mir of Lahore, tradition holds, laid its foundation stone.

Inside, there is no idol and no altar: the Guru Granth Sahib sits enthroned, and the day runs on kirtan — the scripture sung essentially continuously from before dawn until night, broadcast now to the planet. Across the water the langar hall serves on the order of **a hundred thousand free meals a day**, every one cooked and served by volunteers, the world's largest kitchen attached to the world's least exclusive door.

Its history bears the region's scars: demolished and defiled repeatedly in the eighteenth century (Baba Deep Singh and the Ghallugharas belong to its ledger), rebuilt each time by sangat hands; gilded under Ranjit Singh; stormed by the army in June 1984, with the adjacent Akal Takht destroyed. Each rebuilding is treated by Sikhs not as restoration but as doctrine — the building, like the community, practises Chardi Kala. Pilgrims of every faith walk its marble daily; the famous photograph of the temple's reflection in the water is, for millions, simply what peace looks like.
