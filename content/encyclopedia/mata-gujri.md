Mata Gujri's life spans the tradition's most harrowing arc, and at every station of it she is the one holding others upright. Married to Tegh Bahadur in 1633, she shared the decades of his contemplative obscurity at Bakala; she was mother to Gobind Rai, born in Patna during his father's eastern travels; and in 1675 she received in Anandpur the news from Delhi — her husband beheaded for another faith's freedom — and is recorded steadying the nine-year-old who had just become Guru, while her own world fell.

For twenty-nine years more she was the household spine of Anandpur — managing its affairs through the sieges, the mother-counselor the tradition calls simply *Mata ji*. Then came December 1704: the broken evacuation, the Sarsa crossing, and the separation that placed her — nearly eighty — alone with her two youngest grandsons, Zorawar and Fateh, aged about nine and six. Betrayed and imprisoned in the **Thanda Burj**, Sirhind's cold tower, in Punjab's bitterest weeks, she did the work for which the Panth remembers her morning and evening: each day, as the boys were taken to court and offered comfort for conversion, she told them again whose grandsons they were — of the Guru who had given his head and not his faith. Each day they came back unbent.

When they were executed, she died in the tower the same hour — of grief, by tradition; the accounts do not require improvement. Gurdwara Fatehgarh Sahib holds the sites. In the long Sikh argument about where courage comes from, Mata Gujri is the standing answer: it is *taught* — at the last, in the cold, by grandmothers.
