Simran comes from the Sanskrit *smarana* — remembering. It is the Sikh practice of meditation, but the choice of word over "concentration" or "trance" is deliberate: the premise is not that the One is far away and must be reached, but that the One is here and we keep forgetting. The practice is the maintenance of memory.

In form, simran ranges from the structured to the seamless. At its most formal: amrit vela, the still hours before dawn, repetition of **Waheguru** coordinated with breath, alone or with sangat, sometimes aloud, sometimes whispered, sometimes purely mental — the traditional ladder runs from the tongue to the throat to the heart to the breath, until the repetition continues by itself the way a mother, the bani says, never stops being aware of her child even while working. At its most seamless: the name surfacing at the red light, before the difficult conversation, in the hospital corridor — remembrance as the mind's screensaver, what consciousness returns to when not otherwise tasked.

The Gurus were practical about its effects, and modern readers may notice the overlap with what contemplative science reports: the settling of the threat-response, the loosening of rumination's grip, the widening of patience. The bani says it in its own register — simran extinguishes the fire of anxiety; in remembrance, fear finds no purchase. Sukhmani Sahib, the Psalm of Peace, is essentially twenty-four cantos of this claim with evidence.

But Sikhi refuses to let simran become self-care alone. The remembered One is in all beings; therefore real simran leaks outward as patience with the irritating colleague and food for the hungry stranger. The tradition's test of a meditation practice is not the depth of the sitting but the temperature of the life around it.
