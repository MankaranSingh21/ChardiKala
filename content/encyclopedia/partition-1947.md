Independence arrived in August 1947 with a cartographer's deadline: the Radcliffe Line, drawn in five weeks by a lawyer who had never been to India, fell across the middle of Panjab — and through the middle of the Sikhs, the one major community whose homeland, shrines, and population the line could not avoid bisecting. Sikh leaders had faced an impossible menu all year: too few for a state of their own, too concentrated to escape the border, they threw in with India and braced.

What followed remains among the largest and bloodiest forced migrations in recorded history. As authority dissolved, violence between communities — massacre, abduction, the burning of villages, trains arriving silent — killed, by serious estimates, **between half a million and a million people** of all faiths, and drove some **fifteen million** from their homes. Punjab supplied the worst of it: the two halves of the province effectively exchanged populations in months, columns of refugees forty miles long passing each other on the Grand Trunk Road. Sikhs and Hindus moved east; Muslims west; and the abducted women of every community — tens of thousands — became the tragedy's longest unfinished accounting.

The Sikhs lost, along with the dead, the geography of their origins: **Nankana Sahib** (the founder's birthplace), **Kartarpur** (his farm and resting place), Panja Sahib, Lahore of the Sikh court — left across a border that would harden into one of the world's least crossable. Pilgrims spent seventy years viewing Kartarpur through binoculars until the 2019 Corridor.

Nearly every Punjabi family — Sikh, Muslim, Hindu — carries Partition in its private archive, often unspoken. This site's fiction wing imagines that border composted into a garden. The history wing records what it actually cost.
