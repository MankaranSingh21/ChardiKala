He was born Lachman Dev in Kashmir; remorse over a doe he had shot turned the young hunter to renunciation, and as the ascetic **Madho Das** he kept a hermitage on the Godavari, dabbling — the chronicles enjoy this detail — in occult intimidations of visitors. In 1708 Guru Gobind Singh, in the Deccan with the emperor's camp, walked into the hermitage and out-sat its tricks. The exchange the tradition preserves is a complete conversion in four words: *Whose are you?* — **"Banda"** — *your slave.* The Guru initiated him, named him Banda Singh, and commissioned him north with five arrows, five counselors, and the unsettled account of Punjab.

What the hermit accomplished in eighteen months reorganized the subcontinent's assumptions: the rallying of the Khalsa, the string of towns, and at **Chappar Chiri** in May 1710 the annihilation of Sirhind's army and its governor Wazir Khan — executioner of the Guru's children. His regime, brief as it was, legislated like a sermon: coins in the Gurus' names, justice open to peasants, and the **abolition of zamindari** — title vested in the tiller, the boldest land reform the region had seen.

The empire required six years and its full weight to take him, starving out his last fort at Gurdas Nangal. Delhi made his end a spectacle of months — seven hundred companions beheaded, each first offered Islam, each refusing; then Banda, his four-year-old son killed before him, tortured to death in June 1716 with a composure the hostile court chroniclers recorded in something like bewilderment. Asked why a man of God had taken the sword, his answer survives: when men become so corrupt, the punishment arrives wearing someone; he had been the someone.
