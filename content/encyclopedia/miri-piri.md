When the eleven-year-old Guru Hargobind was invested in 1606 — weeks after the empire tortured his father to death — he asked for two swords and wore them crossed: **piri**, from *pir*, the Sufi word for a spiritual master, and **miri**, from *mir*, an Arabic-Persian title of worldly command. The image became the doctrine: spiritual and temporal responsibility, worn by the same body, neither delegated away.

It answers a question every contemplative tradition faces. If the inner life is what matters, what do the meditators owe the world's victims? Answers across history have included: nothing (renounce and withdraw), or everything (abandon the spirit for politics). Miri-piri rejects the menu. The same person prays at Harmandir Sahib in the morning and hears land disputes at the Akal Takht across the plaza in the afternoon — the two buildings face each other on purpose, accountability in architecture. Power without the spirit becomes tyranny; spirit without power becomes, at best, beautiful commentary while the innocent bleed.

The doctrine's guardrails matter as much as its license. The miri sword serves the piri values, never the reverse: protection, not conquest — Guru Hargobind won four defensive battles and annexed nothing. Force is lawful only as last resort, a line Guru Gobind Singh fixed in the Zafarnama: *when all other means have failed.* And the wielder must be the saint first — hence **sant-sipahi**, saint-soldier, with the word order non-negotiable.

Miri-piri's modern descendants are not primarily military. The doctrine underwrites Sikh engagement in law, politics, journalism, and protest — the conviction that gurdwara and courtroom are one jurisdiction. A tradition with miri-piri in its spine produces fewer hermits and more human-rights lawyers, which is exactly what it was designed to do.
