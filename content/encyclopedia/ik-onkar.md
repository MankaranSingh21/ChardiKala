Ik Onkar is the opening glyph of the Guru Granth Sahib and the most compressed statement in Sikhi: the numeral **੧** (one) joined to **ਓ** (the syllable of being, Oankar) with a long upward stroke like a banner. Guru Nanak put the digit there on purpose. Words can be reinterpreted; *1* cannot. There is one — not one *of ours* as against *theirs*, but one, full stop, before any tradition got a name.

Everything distinctive about Sikhi unfolds from this arithmetic. If the source is one, then creation is not divided into sacred and profane precincts — so Sikhi has no forbidden foods of purity, no polluted castes, no unholy lands. If the light is one, then it burns in every person identically — so the langar seats everyone at one level, and the scripture canonises saints of other faiths. If the One is **nirbhau** and **nirvair** (without fear, without enmity), then nothing fearful or hateful can be done in its name and remain honest.

Sikh thought is careful about what kind of "one" this is. Not a being among beings, counted alongside creation; the bani speaks of the One as both utterly beyond attributes (**nirgun**) and present in all attributes (**sargun**) — closer than the heartbeat, vaster than the cosmos, "filling the filled." The hymns gesture, they do not define: *those who taste, know.*

A note for skeptical readers, in this site's spirit of an open door: Ik Onkar is not a loyalty oath demanded at entry. It is a hypothesis the tradition invites you to live as if true — treat every person as carrying the same light — and see what happens to your ethics. The experiment is open to all, and the langar serves the control group too.
