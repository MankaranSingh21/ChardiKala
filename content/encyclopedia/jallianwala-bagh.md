On 13 April 1919 — Vaisakhi, the festival that fills Amritsar — thousands gathered in **Jallianwala Bagh**, a walled garden minutes from Harmandir Sahib: villagers in town for the fair, families, and citizens protesting the Rowlatt Act's detentions and the deportation of local leaders. The city was under a prohibition on assemblies, proclaimed that day with deliberate thinness. Brigadier-General **Reginald Dyer** marched fifty riflemen to the garden's single practical exit, gave **no warning and no order to disperse**, and commanded fire into the densest parts of the crowd — directing aim, by his own testimony, wherever people tried to flee or climb the walls. The shooting continued for about ten minutes, 1,650 rounds, until ammunition ran low; the troops withdrew leaving the wounded where they lay, the curfew ensuring many bled through the night. The official count of 379 dead is regarded by every serious account as a floor; the well in the garden gave up bodies for days.

Dyer told the inquiry he had meant to produce "a moral effect" throughout Punjab — the candour that made the massacre indefensible even by imperial standards. He was retired; the House of Lords commended him; a public subscription raised him a fortune. That sequence — atrocity, mild sanction, ovation — taught India more than the bullets had: Tagore returned his knighthood, Gandhi's movement found its mass base, and a Punjab orphan named **Udham Singh**, who carried the day inside him for twenty-one years, shot Sir Michael O'Dwyer — the governor who had approved Dyer's action — in London in 1940.

The garden is a memorial now, the bullet scars labelled in the brick. For Sikhs the wound is doubled by the calendar: the empire chose the Khalsa's birthday.
