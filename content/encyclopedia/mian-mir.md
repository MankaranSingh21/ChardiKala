Mian Mir — Baba Sain Mir Mohammed Sahib — was the most revered Sufi of Lahore, a Qadiri master of austere personal practice and zero appetite for courting power, famous for receiving emperors with the same flat courtesy as beggars and for telling Jahangir, when the emperor pressed past his doorkeeper, that the rich should wait like everyone else. Between this Muslim saint and **Guru Arjan** there grew a friendship the traditions of both communities preserve — visits exchanged between Lahore and Amritsar, a mutual recognition across institutional lines that needed no merger to be real.

Sikh tradition honours him with the most symbolically loaded act in its architecture: when the time came to lay the **foundation stone of Harmandir Sahib**, Guru Arjan invited Mian Mir to lay it. Historians note the earliest sources vary on the detail; the tradition has held to it firmly for centuries, and its meaning does not depend on masonry records — a shrine built with doors open to all four directions, founded by a hand from another faith, states the Sikh thesis about the One and the labels better than any text.

The friendship's last scene is its darkest and most telling. In 1606, as Guru Arjan was tortured in Lahore — Mian Mir's own city — the saint came to him in anguish and, tradition records, offered to intercede, by miracle or by influence. The Guru declined: the will was sweet, the lesson had to be witnessed. Mian Mir lived on to teach Dara Shikoh, the Mughal prince whose syncretic vision died with him, and was buried in Lahore in 1635, where his urs is kept still — by Muslims, with Sikh pilgrims among them.
