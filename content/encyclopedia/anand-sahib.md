Anand means bliss — not the spike of pleasure but the settled joy of a mind that has found its centre. Guru Amar Das composed Anand Sahib in Raag Ramkali late in a long life, and the tradition put it precisely where it belongs: everywhere.

It is sung at weddings (the ceremony itself is named after it — Anand Karaj, the work of bliss). It is sung at funerals. It is sung at the conclusion of virtually every Sikh service, before the ardaas, and its first five stanzas with the fortieth are part of the daily nitnem and of the amrit initiation itself. A Sikh hears Anand Sahib at every threshold of life, in grief and in celebration alike — which is the teaching: the bliss it describes does not depend on which threshold you are standing at.

The opening stanza sets the method — *anand bhaiaa meri maae, satiguru mai paaiaa*: bliss has come, my mother, for I have found the true Guru. What follows over forty stanzas is an anatomy of where joy actually lives. The composition addresses the mind directly, like a wise elder: stay home, mind; stop wandering. It addresses the body: you came into the world to harvest naam, not merchandise. It addresses the senses one by one — eyes, made to see the One in what you look at; ears, made for the true word.

For new students the radical claim is easy to miss because the melody is so sweet: Sikhi here declares that joy is the *normal* condition of a rightly-ordered consciousness — not a reward deferred to an afterlife, not an indulgence to be suspected, but the baseline to be recovered. Grief visits; the centre holds. Sung over a casket, that is not denial. It is the tradition keeping its deepest promise.
