Morcha is a soldier's word — the trench, the entrenched front line — that Sikhs of the twentieth century repurposed for a different weapon system: the organized, disciplined, *absorbent* nonviolent campaign. In a morcha, volunteers (the word used is jatha, a unit) present themselves in waves at the point of injustice, accept the consequences — beating, arrest, worse — without retaliation, and are replaced by the next wave, until the state or institution opposite exhausts its capacity for cruelty before the community exhausts its capacity for volunteers.

The classic demonstrations were the Gurdwara Reform morchas of the 1920s. At **Guru ka Bagh** (1922), jatha after jatha of Akalis walked forward to be clubbed down by police, hands folded, vows of nonviolence taken at the Akal Takht; observers including the clergyman C. F. Andrews wrote that they had seen something new under the sun. At **Jaito**, waves of five hundred marched for years. The campaigns won — the shrines passed to elected management in 1925 — and the technique entered the subcontinent's political vocabulary; Gandhi's movement and the Akali morchas studied each other closely, and argued about credit.

The morcha's Sikh signature distinguishes it from generic protest. It is sworn before the Guru — discipline is religious, not tactical, which is why it holds under blows. It is fed — the langar deploys with the front line, and feeds opponents too. And it is bounded by the sant-sipahi ethic: the goal is the unjust *arrangement's* defeat, never the opponent's humiliation.

The 2020–21 **farmers' protest** ran the full classical pattern at Delhi's borders for over a year — jathas, langar, discipline, repeal — and introduced the word morcha to a global audience that watched a community lay siege to a capital by cooking for it.
