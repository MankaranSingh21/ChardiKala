Namdev was a *chhimba* — calico printer, tailor-caste — from Narsi Bamani in Maharashtra, a founding voice of the Marathi Varkari tradition whose abhangs are still sung on the pilgrim roads to Pandharpur. That he also stands inside the Guru Granth Sahib — **sixty-one hymns**, in Gurmukhi, some composed in the mixed Hindi of his northern wanderings — makes him the scripture's longest-travelled voice, and the proof of how deliberately the Gurus' canon trawled the whole subcontinent's devotion for the genuine article.

His signature is intoxication with the **Name** — the practice the Sikhs call naam, which his usage prefigures: God remembered as constantly as the miser counts coin, as the calf thinks of the cow, as water lives in the fish. The tradition's stories about him are the theology dramatized: the image of the deity drinking the milk a child offered in guileless literalism; the temple that **rotated** to face him when brahmins expelled the low-caste devotee from its door — the shrine at Aundha Nagnath still presents the architectural anomaly as evidence; the Sultan's torture answered with serenity. The Granth's editors kept the wonder-stories' point while filing the polemic: several of his canonised hymns are blunt anti-idolatry, anti-ritual arguments — the stone god that cannot answer, the One that fills every pot.

He composed, by Varkari count, in the many thousands; he died at Pandharpur (or Punjab — Ghuman in Gurdaspur keeps a rival tomb and a living festival, the two claims sharing him amiably). In the Granth he is the bridge: Maharashtra's ecstasy, Punjab's canon — the Name, as he kept saying, travelling well.
