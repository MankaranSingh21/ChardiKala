Naam Japna — the repetition and remembrance of the Name — is the first of the three pillars on which Guru Nanak set Sikh life, alongside **kirat karo** (earn honestly) and **vand chhakna** (share before consuming). The triad is the whole religion in three infinitives, and its order is instructive: remembrance first, because the other two pillars are its consequences. A mind genuinely soaked in the awareness of one light in all beings will work honestly and share readily; a mind that is not will corrupt any rule you give it.

In practice, naam japna spans a spectrum. At the formal end: **nitnem**, the daily prayers; amrit vela recitation; simran with the breath; the sangat's collective chant. At the informal end — and the Gurus weighted this end heavily — remembrance threaded through ordinary occupation: the name on the tongue while the hands drive, cook, code, harvest. Guru Nanak's critique of the renunciants at the mountain was precisely that they had outsourced the world to others while perfecting their private practice; his alternative was the householder whose remembrance runs *concurrent* with duty, like a drone note under a melody.

The bani describes the goal state with a dyer's vocabulary: to be *rangia* — dyed — in the Name, colourfast through the washes of fortune. Not episodes of remembering, but a baseline.

For learners who stumble at the word "repetition" — wondering whether saying a word many times can possibly matter — the tradition's answer is unembarrassed: yes, the way a scale practised many times matters to a musician. The repetition is not magic aimed at the One, who needs no reminding. It is training aimed at the rememberer. What you return your attention to ten thousand times becomes the place your attention lives. Sikhi simply asks: choose that address deliberately.
