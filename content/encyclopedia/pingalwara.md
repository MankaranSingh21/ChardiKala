Pingalwara — from *pingla*, the crippled: "home of the broken" — is the institution Bhagat Puran Singh founded in Amritsar in 1947, in the middle of Partition's refugee catastrophe, for the people no system claims: the destitute dying, the severely disabled, the abandoned insane, the infant left on a doorstep, the elder left at a bus stand. Its admission policy then and now is the shortest in institutional medicine: **arrival**. No fee, no family guarantor, no test of faith, residence, or prognosis — the categories that elsewhere determine eligibility are, at Pingalwara's gate, merely description.

It began as one man with a handcart and a tin cup at the gurdwara gates — and a boy, Piara Singh, carried on the founder's back for years before there was a building. It is today a complex of homes across several campuses: thousands of residents, hospital wards, an artificial-limb workshop, dementia care, schooling for slum children, and the printing press through which the founder's other obsession — pamphlets on trees, soil, and the costs of the chemical harvest, printed on waste paper — still circulates by the free ton. The institution buries, with its own rites or the resident's own faith's, those who die in its care and were never claimed; the registers of names-unknown are their own archive of the society outside the gate.

It runs, in the founder's tradition, substantially on the **golak of the ordinary** — small donations, gurdwara collections, the diaspora's standing orders — and it functions in Sikh moral vocabulary as a calibration instrument: when the tradition needs to explain seva to a child or a parliament, it points across the Grand Trunk Road from the Golden Temple's gold to Pingalwara's laundry lines, and notes that the tradition itself sees one institution, in two buildings.
