Nanaki was five years old when her brother was born, and the tradition's accounts make her his first and steadiest recognizer: while the village puzzled over the boy who gave away goods to feed ascetics, while their father reached for the rod, Nanaki saw — before teachers, before saints, before the world — what had arrived in her parents' house. Sikhs honour her as the **first believer**, the original member of a faith that would number thirty million, and the fact that the first Sikh after the founder was a woman is not lost on the tradition's daughters.

Her role was practical as well as perceptive. Married to Jai Ram, a steward in the Nawab's administration at Sultanpur Lodhi, she brought her brother to the town and secured him his position at the granary — the storehouse where the famous accounting stopped at *tera*, "thirteen/yours," the clerk dissolving into the divine arithmetic. It was from Nanaki's town, in Nanaki's orbit, that the Guru went to the river Bein and returned with the words that founded Sikhi. Tradition also credits her with the gift that shaped the faith's sound: the **rabab** purchased for Mardana at her urging — the first patron of kirtan, in effect, buying the first instrument.

She asked of her brother only that he visit; he is recorded arriving, across the years of journeys, when she thought of him. She died at Sultanpur, by tradition with the Guru beside her, shortly before his own final settling at Kartarpur. The gurdwara of Bebe Nanaki at Sultanpur keeps her name, as does the tradition's quiet teaching through her: the family of the enlightened carries its own work — recognition, shelter, and the buying of instruments.
