When Guru Arjan compiled the Adi Granth, the scribe who took the dictation — checking every character of what would become eternal — was his uncle and counselor **Bhai Gurdas**. His own compositions, forty long ballads (vaars) in Punjabi and a set of kabitts in Braj, were left out of the canon by his own scruple. Tradition records Guru Arjan's verdict on them instead: they are the **key** to the Guru Granth Sahib — and they are among the very few works permitted to be sung as kirtan in gurdwaras besides scripture itself.

The vaars earn the title. Where the Granth's poetry is concentrated and oracular, Bhai Gurdas explains, narrates, illustrates. He is the first historian of Sikhi: his first vaar gives the earliest connected account of Guru Nanak's life and journeys — the debates at Mecca, the dialogue with the Himalayan yogis — written by a man who had known the Gurus personally. He is the first theologian: his stanzas on what makes a **gurmukh**, on sangat, on the difference between ritual and remembrance, turn the scripture's lightning into lamplight.

His images stay with readers. The Sikh of the Guru, he writes, is like a bee storing the pollen of holiness; sangat is the touchstone where base metal turns gold; humility is the strength that bends like a fruit-laden branch. And one of his lines became the community's compass on gender, quoted endlessly since: *from woman comes man, from woman all the world — why call her lesser, from whom kings are born?* (The thought is Guru Nanak's, from Asa ki Var; Bhai Gurdas carried it forward.)

For new students, the vaars remain what they were in 1604: the recommended front door.
