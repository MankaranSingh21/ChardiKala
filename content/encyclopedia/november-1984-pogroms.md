On 31 October 1984, Prime Minister Indira Gandhi was assassinated by two of her Sikh bodyguards — vengeance for Blue Star. What followed in Delhi and across northern India over the next four days was not a riot, though that word was used officially for decades: the inquiries, affidavits, and journalism assembled since describe a **pogrom** — organized, directed, and enabled. Mobs moved through Sikh neighbourhoods with **voter lists and ration rolls** identifying Sikh homes; kerosene and tyres appeared in quantity; Congress party functionaries were identified by survivors and commissions as instigators in locality after locality; and the police, where present, disarmed victims, opened jails, or watched. Men and boys were burned alive in front of their families with a method too uniform for spontaneity. The official toll in Delhi alone is **2,733**; serious counts run past three thousand there and thousands more across the country. Trilokpuri's Block 32 lost hundreds in a single night. The "Widow Colony" of Tilak Vihar holds the survivors' households to this day.

The accounting has been its own scandal: a procession of commissions (ten by most counts) across four decades; cases closed for "lack of evidence" against eyewitnessed leaders; convictions countable on fingers — the most prominent, Congress politician Sajjan Kumar's life sentence, arriving in **2018**, thirty-four years late. The Delhi High Court, affirming it, used the words "crimes against humanity."

For Sikhs, November 1984 sits beside June as the year's double wound — and it raised the harder question: the state had not merely failed to protect; parts of it had organized the burning. The insurgency that consumed Punjab for the next decade recruited heavily from that answer.
