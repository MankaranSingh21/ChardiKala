At Vaisakhi 1699, when Guru Gobind Singh stood before the great assembly with drawn sword and asked for a head, five men rose one by one: **Daya Ram**, a Khatri shopkeeper of Lahore; **Dharam Das**, a Jat farmer of Hastinapur; **Mohkam Chand**, a washerman of Dwarka; **Himmat Rai**, a water-carrier of Jagannath Puri; and **Sahib Chand**, a barber of Bidar. Five castes — including those the social order ranked near its bottom — and five corners of the subcontinent: the demographic spread is so pointed that it reads as the lesson it was.

They received the first amrit, took the name Singh, and became the **Panj Pyare** — the five beloved. Then the Guru knelt and asked them to initiate *him*: the disciples becoming the master's master, authority completing a circle instead of descending a pyramid. Sikhi's rejection of priesthood is usually argued from texts; here it was performed as theatre, once, unforgettably.

Their permanence is institutional. To this day, every Amrit Sanchar requires five initiated Sikhs standing as the Panj Pyare to prepare and administer the amrit — the rite cannot be performed by any individual, however holy, because the office belongs to the five, not to a one. Five beloved lead every Nagar Kirtan procession, swords drawn, barefoot, ahead of the Guru Granth Sahib. And in Sikh constitutional practice, a representative five — convened with prayer — can speak with the Panth's voice in deliberation, the quorum of the Guru's presence in the community.

The names of the first five are recited in gurdwaras every day. A shopkeeper, a farmer, a washerman, a water-carrier, a barber: the founding board of the Khalsa, and not a nobleman among them.
