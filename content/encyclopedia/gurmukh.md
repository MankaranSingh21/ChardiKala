The Guru Granth Sahib sorts humanity not by creed, caste, or conduct-score but by **orientation**: the *gurmukh* — literally, one whose face is turned toward the Guru — and the *manmukh*, whose face is turned toward *man*, the self-willed mind. The pair appears thousands of times in the scripture; it is the bani's basic psychology.

The distinction is not between religious and secular lives. A manmukh can be devout — performing every rite with the self still enthroned at the centre, piety serving image, the prayer a transaction. A gurmukh can be a farmer who never leaves the village: the difference is the axis of reference. The manmukh's day is curated by *I-want, I-fear, I-am-owed* (the haumai loop); events have meaning as they touch the self. The gurmukh's day runs on *what does this moment ask?* — attention aimed outward and upward, the self present but demoted from monarch to steward.

The bani's portraits are vivid. The manmukh is the deer with musk in its own navel, ransacking the forest for the fragrance it carries; the gambler losing a priceless birth at dice. The gurmukh is the lotus dry above the water it grows in; awake among the sleeping; recognizable, Bhai Gurdas adds, by small external signs with large internal causes — speech that heals, earnings that are clean, eyes that see the One in the passer-by.

Crucially, the categories are **permeable** — they name states, not castes of soul. The whole apparatus of Sikh practice (sangat, seva, simran, the dawn) exists as a turning mechanism, because faces can be turned. That is the entire optimism of the tradition: the manmukh is not condemned; the manmukh is *facing the wrong way*, and a door, by design, stands ninety degrees from every wall.
