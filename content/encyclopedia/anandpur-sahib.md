Guru Tegh Bahadur bought the land in 1665 — a ridge above the Sutlej under the Shivalik foothills, purchased from the hill state of Bilaspur — and named the settlement for bliss: **Anandpur**. Under his son it became the capital of the Khalsa's creation: the city where the ninth Guru's severed head was brought home from Delhi and cremated (Gurdwara Sis Ganj marks it); where the tenth Guru drilled, hunted, heard poets, and built his five forts; and where, on Vaisakhi of **1699**, before tens of thousands, the sword asked for a head and five men answered — Takht Kesgarh Sahib, second of the five thrones, stands on the spot, displaying the tenth Guru's own weapons.

The city's calendar made it the Khalsa's parade ground as well as its shrine. **Hola Mohalla**, instituted by Guru Gobind Singh as the answer to Holi — mock battles instead of coloured water — still fills Anandpur each spring: Nihang cavalcades, gatka displays, tent-pegging at the gallop, and langar at the scale of a small nation, the martial tradition exercising in public so it is never mistaken for a secret.

History priced the city accordingly. The sieges of 1700–1704 starved it; the evacuation of December 1704 — under imperial oaths broken at the Sarsa within hours — scattered the Guru's family toward Chamkaur and Sirhind. The Guru never returned; the city kept everything. Pilgrims climbing to Kesgarh today pass the forts' remnants — Anandgarh, Lohgarh, Fatehgarh — and the white town below: bliss, fortified, which is roughly the whole tradition in a postcard.
