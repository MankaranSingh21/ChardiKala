The Sikh Rehat Maryada is the modern Panth's agreed code of practice: a slim document hammered out over fourteen years of consultation among scholars and institutions, adopted by the SGPC in 1945. After two centuries in which competing rehatnamas (codes attributed to the tenth Guru's circle) and local custom had accumulated contradictions, the community did a very Sikh thing: it convened, argued, and standardized.

It opens by defining a Sikh — one who believes in the One, the ten Gurus and the Guru Granth Sahib, the amrit of the tenth Guru, and no other religion's rites as binding. It then organizes Sikh life under two headings the Gurus would recognize instantly: the personal discipline (naam and bani — the daily nitnem prayers, family life, honest labour) and the communal (sangat, langar, seva, the management of gurdwaras).

Its most consequential rules are the ones that keep the doors open. In the gurdwara, no ritual not consonant with Gurmat; no idols, no astrology, no caste observance; the langar floor seats everyone without distinction; and — stated explicitly — a person of **any faith, or none,** may enter, listen, and eat. The ceremonies it codifies (naming, Anand Karaj marriage, funeral, amrit initiation) are deliberately simple and free of priestcraft: any competent Sikh, woman or man, may conduct them.

Students should know its status honestly: most of the Panth accepts it as the working standard; some traditional orders (Nihangs, certain taksals and sampradas) keep older or stricter codes, and debates — for instance on meat, or on aspects of women's full equality in ceremonial roles at certain takhts — continue inside the community. The Rehat Maryada's own preface expected revision by future consensus. It is a constitution, not a cage.
