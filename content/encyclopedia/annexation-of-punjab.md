On 29 March 1849, in the Lahore fort his father had ruled from, ten-year-old Maharaja **Duleep Singh** signed the document ending the last independent state in India: Punjab annexed to the East India Company, the army disbanded, the treasury confiscated, and — itemized in the treaty by name — the **Koh-i-Noor** diamond "surrendered" to the Queen of England. Governor-General Dalhousie, whose ambition had run ahead of his own government's instructions, called it the fulfilment of policy. The boy called it, decades later and in plainer language, robbery; he had signed at an age when signatures bind only the powerless.

The machinery of absorption was thorough. The young Maharaja was separated from his mother **Jind Kaur** — the Company's officials wrote frankly that they feared her influence more than an army — placed with English guardians, converted to Christianity at fifteen, and transplanted to a Suffolk estate to be displayed as a curiosity of empire: Victoria's favourite exotic godson, painted by court painters, the diamond he had signed away worn by the Queen at levees he attended. Mother and son met again after **thirteen years**, in Calcutta; she died in England within two, having reportedly re-taught him in those months who he was. His later attempts to return to Punjab and to Sikhi were intercepted at Aden — he re-took amrit there in 1886 — and he died in a Paris hotel in 1893, asking to be buried in Punjab. He was buried in Suffolk.

For Sikhs the annexation closes the sovereignty chapter and opens the modern one: the questions of identity under foreign rule that the Singh Sabha would answer with schools, presses, and scholarship — the kingdom rebuilt as a community of letters.
