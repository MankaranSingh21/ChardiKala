Kabir (15th century) was raised in a family of Muslim weavers in Banaras — Hinduism's holiest city — and spent his life at the loom, in the market, and in the faces of both establishments. No major poet has ever held organized religion's pretensions in less reverence: his verses ask the mullah why God should be deaf that he must shout from the minaret, and the pundit why bathing makes holiness when the fish lives in the river; caste he dismantled with a weaver's economy — one clay, one potter, many pots. What remained after the demolition was not atheism but the One past all labels — *Ram* and *Khuda* used interchangeably, to the equal irritation of both proprietorships — findable, he insisted, at home, in the breath, mid-work: the loom as altar.

The Sikh Gurus recognized the kinship and acted on it editorially: **over five hundred of Kabir's hymns and couplets** stand in the Guru Granth Sahib — the largest contribution of any non-Guru — woven through the raags beside the Gurus' own, sometimes in canonised dialogue with them. Sikh daily life quotes him constantly without noticing: the salok at hearing of death, the jewel-of-this-birth verses, the warnings to the hoarder.

His death completed the joke he had spent a life telling. Hindus and Muslims, the famous account runs, disputed the body — cremation or burial — and found under the shroud only **flowers**, which they divided. Two memorials stand at Maghar accordingly. Inside the Sikh scripture, no division was necessary: the weaver sits whole, in Gurmukhi, mid-argument, exactly as welcome as the tradition's founder intended every honest voice to be.
