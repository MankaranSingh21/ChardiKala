The Ghadar Party was founded in 1913 by Punjabi immigrants on the American Pacific coast — sawmill workers, farmers, ex-soldiers, and students, a large majority of them Sikhs — with a headquarters in San Francisco (the *Yugantar Ashram*), a printing press, and a single agenda stated in its newspaper's masthead: **Ghadar**, mutiny — the armed overthrow of British rule in India. The paper, published in Punjabi, Urdu, and other languages, was soon read in immigrant bunkhouses from Vancouver to Manila, and its plainspoken accounting of colonial extraction — famine deaths set against revenue shipped — radicalized a generation that empire had assumed safely exported.

The outbreak of war in 1914 looked like the opening. The call went home: thousands of Ghadarites — estimates run to eight thousand — left jobs and farms and sailed for India to raise the army in revolt, a migration of revolutionaries with few parallels. The empire's intelligence had ridden along: arrests met the ships; the planned February 1915 risings were betrayed from inside; and the Lahore Conspiracy trials sent dozens to the gallows and hundreds to the Andamans' cellular jail. **Kartar Singh Sarabha** — Berkeley student, press-hand, organizer, nineteen years old — went to the rope telling the judge he would be reborn to hang again until India was free, and Bhagat Singh would carry his photograph.

As insurrection, Ghadar failed completely. As example, it never stopped detonating: its veterans seeded the Babbar Akali and Kirti movements and the Punjabi left for decades; its press proved the diaspora a political actor; and its memory fixed the pattern this site's history keeps finding — the Punjabi abroad who reads of injustice at home and starts, immediately, organizing.
