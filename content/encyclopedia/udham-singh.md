Udham Singh's childhood was subtraction: mother, father, then brother gone before he was twelve, the boys raised in the Central Khalsa Orphanage at Amritsar — the city whose 1919 Vaisakhi he lived through as a teenager. Whether he stood in Jallianwala Bagh that afternoon or served water to the wounded after (accounts vary; he said both at different times), the garden became his organizing fact. He swore the era's oath — and then, unlike most who swore it, spent **twenty-one years** keeping it.

The interval was a revolutionary's odyssey: Ghadar work in California, gun-running, arrests in Punjab, four years in jail for an arms cache, and a slow circling toward England under a stack of aliases — carpenter, signboard painter, film-set extra (he appears, unverifiably but persistently, in crowd scenes of imperial epics). On 13 March 1940, at a Caxton Hall meeting in London, he shot **Sir Michael O'Dwyer** — the Punjab governor who had approved Dyer's action and defended it for two decades — dead with two rounds across a crowded room, wounding three other imperial worthies, and waited, unresisting, for arrest.

At trial he gave his name as **Ram Mohammad Singh Azad** — Hindu, Muslim, Sikh, and *free*, one man as the indivisible Punjab — and his statement from the dock, suppressed by court order for decades, declared he had done it for the crushed of his country and was unafraid: *what greater honour than to die for the motherland?* He was hanged at Pentonville on 31 July 1940. His remains, returned in 1974 to crowds the empire would not have credited, lie at Sunam and at Jallianwala Bagh itself — the orphan home with the garden at last.
