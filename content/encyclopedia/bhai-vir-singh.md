Bhai Vir Singh (1872–1957) chose, from a scholar's family in Amritsar, the least glamorous instrument of the Sikh renaissance and made it decisive: **print**. Co-founding the Wazir-i-Hind Press and the *Khalsa Samachar* weekly, writing anonymously for decades (he refused bylines for most of his life — the seva theory of authorship), he produced the working literature of modern Sikhi: tracts, the first Punjabi novels — *Sundri* (1898), whose Khalsa heroine armed a generation's imagination and arguably began Punjabi fiction itself — biographies of the Gurus, editions of the classics, and the unfinished monument of his scholarship, the vast *Santhya* commentary on the Guru Granth Sahib.

His poetry renovated the language's possibilities: short lyric forms in modern Punjabi — *Lehran de Har*, the kikar tree, the violet — nature mysticism in a tongue whose poetry had run to epic and qissa; the long poem *Rana Surat Singh* importing blank verse. Critics named him the **sixth river** of the land of five; the metaphor carries the point — irrigation. A language that colonial schooling ranked as rustic was, in his hands, demonstrably an instrument of subtle metaphysics and exact feeling, and the demonstration changed the language's self-esteem: modern Punjabi literature, both Sikh and secular, descends from his proof of concept.

He kept lifelong distance from office and platform — the Singh Sabha's quietest founder, gardening at Amritsar and Dehradun (his roses were famous; the botany shows in the verse), funding scholarship, editing texts. Honours found him late (the Sahitya Akademi, Padma Bhushan); the durable honour is structural: Sikhs read their tradition, to this day, through forms he built — and Punjabi has not stopped flowing through the channel he cut.
