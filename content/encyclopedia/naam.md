Naam translates as "name," and the translation immediately fails. In the Guru Granth Sahib, Naam is the divine reality itself as it can be known, tasted, and carried — the presence of the One saturating creation the way fragrance saturates a flower. When the bani says the whole world ripens through Naam, or that Naam is the medicine of all ills, it is not praising a vocabulary item.

The scripture's economics run on this currency. Naam is wealth that thieves cannot take; the true profit (*laaha*) a human life is for; the harvest the body-field was lent to grow. Guru Nanak's audit of human enterprise is blunt: without Naam, all cargo is sand. With it, the poorest porter is rich. This is why **Naam Japna** — the remembrance and repetition of the Name — stands first among the three pillars of Sikh practice, before honest work and sharing: not because chanting outranks ethics, but because remembrance is the root system that keeps the ethics alive. A person who genuinely carries the awareness that one light fills all beings does not need a separate rule against cruelty.

How is Naam practised? Repetition (jap) of Waheguru with breath and attention; kirtan, where the sangat sings the bani until the words sing themselves; the dawn discipline of nitnem; and — the Gurus insist on this — *remembrance inside ordinary life*: hands in the work, heart with the One, the farmer's plough and the merchant's scale as altars. Sikhi deliberately rejected the renunciant's split between meditation and livelihood.

The promised result is not magic but reorientation: fear thins, sweetness returns, the ego's grip loosens — the state the bani calls being dyed in the Name, colourfast, like good Punjabi cloth.
