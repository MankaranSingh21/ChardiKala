Outside every gurdwara on earth rises a steel or wood mast wrapped in cloth, flying a triangular flag — traditionally **kesri**, saffron (Nihang establishments fly blue) — bearing the **khanda** emblem: a double-edged sword at centre (the amrit-stirrer of 1699, cutting falsehood on both strokes), ringed by the **chakkar**, the circle without beginning or end (the One; also a thrown weapon — the symbolism is comfortable being both), flanked by two curved swords for **miri and piri**. This is the Nishan Sahib — "the revered mark" — and its height is functional, not decorative.

Historically, the flag was a service announcement legible from horizon distance: where the Nishan flies, there is **food** (the langar), **shelter** (the serai), **sanctuary** (the sangat's protection) — for anyone, the traveller's religion not a field on the form. In a Punjab of bandits, famines, and armies, the tall saffron triangle was the original emergency services signage, and Sikhs maintain the obligation it advertises with complete seriousness: a gurdwara that flies the flag and locks the kitchen has, by the tradition's lights, lowered the flag.

The mast itself is tended as the standard of a sovereign order. Each Vaisakhi (and at other festivals), the **chola** — the cloth sheathing the pole — is ceremonially changed: the mast lowered by many hands, washed by the sangat with milk and water, re-wrapped, and raised again with ardaas and the spontaneous unanimous roar of the jaikara. Photographs of the rite — fifty strangers' hands on one pole — circulate every spring, and serve as well as anything in this encyclopedia as a diagram of how the institution underneath the flag actually works.
