A raag, in the North Indian tradition the Gurus inherited and reshaped, is more than a scale: it is a **melodic personality** — a set of notes, characteristic phrases, an assigned time of day or season, and above all an emotional weather it reliably produces. The Guru Granth Sahib is organized by them: after the opening liturgy, the entire body of the scripture is filed under **thirty-one raags**, from Sri to Jaijavanti — the only major scripture on earth whose table of contents is a list of musical moods.

The assignments are exegesis in themselves. **Asa** — hope, the pre-dawn raag — carries Guru Nanak's great var of social critique: hard truths set to the music of expectation, dawn arriving on the lash of the lines about woman and worth. **Ramkali**, the yogis' mode, carries the dialogues *with* yogis — the Sidh Gosht's debate sung in the opponents' own key. Joyful **Basant** (spring) and monsoon **Malhar** carry their seasons; grave **Sri** opens the canon; and the Gurus pointedly *excluded* raags they judged emotionally distorting — too lamenting, too martial — bending the inherited system toward equipoise. Several raags (Majh, Jaitsri among them, by tradition) are the Gurus' own inventions or adaptations of Punjabi folk modes: the scripture's music engineered as deliberately as its words.

For the listener the practical upshot: the same teaching arrives differently dressed by raag, and the tradition considers the dress part of the meaning — *which is why* kirtan in the specified raags (a discipline revived vigorously in recent decades after harmonium-era drift) is treated not as musicology but as fidelity: reading the Guru's words in the Guru's chosen tone of voice.
