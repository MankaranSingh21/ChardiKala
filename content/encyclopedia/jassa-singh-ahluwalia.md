Jassa Singh was orphaned at five, raised partly in the household of Mata Sundari (the tenth Guru's widow) in Delhi and then in the camp of Nawab Kapur Singh, who trained him as a chosen successor — the eighteenth-century Khalsa passing its command not by blood but by apprenticeship. From a boyhood of singing kirtan and grazing the camp's horses he rose to supreme command of the **Dal Khalsa**, and his life maps the era's whole arc: from the forests to the throne of Lahore.

He led through the worst of it. At the **Wadda Ghallughara** of 1762 he fought the length of the day's running massacre — his body, the chronicles record, carrying over twenty wounds — and within months led the reassembled Khalsa back against Durrani. The resilience became victory by instalments: Sirhind taken in 1764 (its conqueror directing its treasury to the rebuilding of Harmandir Sahib, which Durrani had mined), Lahore held, and in 1783, with Baghel Singh and Jassa Singh Ramgarhia, the **entry into Delhi itself** — the Red Fort occupied by the descendants of the hunted. Offered the imperial gesture, he declined enthronement (the Panth, he held, had one sovereign already), and the campaign's lasting yield was Baghel Singh's negotiated gurdwaras at the sites of Sikh martyrdom — memory secured instead of empire.

The Panth titled him **Sultan-ul-Qaum**, king of the nation, and the title's irony was the point: its holder ate in pangat and answered to the Sarbat Khalsa. He died in 1783 and was cremated at Amritsar — the orphan of the sangat, buried by the nation he had general-managed back from extinction.
