The shrines of the Sikhs had drifted, across the colonial decades, into the hands of hereditary **mahants** — custodians who held the Gurus' houses as heritable property, backed by British-granted titles and police, their practices ranging from lax to criminal. The awakened laity of the Singh Sabha generation decided, beginning in 1920, to take the houses back — and to do it in a way consistent with whose houses they were.

The instrument was the **morcha**: jathas of volunteers, sworn before the Akal Takht to total nonviolence, presenting themselves at the contested site in wave after wave, accepting arrest or beating, replaced by the next wave, indefinitely. The movement formed the **SGPC** (November 1920) to receive the shrines and the **Akali Dal** to organize the jathas. Nankana Sahib (1921) showed the price — a massacre inside the Guru's birthplace. **Guru ka Bagh** (1922) showed the method perfected: police clubbed down thousands of unresisting Akalis, hands folded, until the watching world — clergy, journalists, the Indian national leadership — described it as a moral event without precedent, and the beatings stopped because they had become unbearable to the beaters' masters. The **keys affair** (1921–22) ended with the government returning the Golden Temple's treasury keys to the SGPC president, and Gandhi's telegram — "first decisive battle for India's freedom won" — entered the movement's scrapbook. **Jaito** (1923–25) ground on for years, jathas of five hundred marching into firing and prison.

The **Sikh Gurdwaras Act of 1925** delivered the victory: historic shrines vested in committees elected by Sikhs — women voting, decades ahead of the franchise elsewhere. Some thirty thousand had gone to prison; hundreds had died; not one armed reprisal stands against the movement's name. The Panth had fought a five-year war whose only weapon was the willingness to be hurt, and won it.
