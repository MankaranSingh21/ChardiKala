EcoSikh was founded in 2010, out of a United Nations–facilitated initiative inviting the world's faiths to commit their institutions to the planet — and the Sikh delegation could draw on a tradition whose scripture *opens and closes* on ecology: the Mul Mantar's one Creator filling creation, and Japji's final salok — **pavan guru, pani pita, mata dharat mahat** — air the Guru, water the father, earth the great mother. The organization's premise is that for Sikhs, environmentalism is not an import but a *remembering*.

Its instruments are calendrical and botanical. **Sikh Environment Day** — March 14, the gurgaddi anniversary of Guru Har Rai, the seventh Guru who kept gardens and hospitals and wept for broken blossoms — is now marked by many thousands of gurdwaras worldwide: tree plantings, sermon themes, solar commitments, plastic-free langars. The flagship **Guru Nanak Sacred Forest** project, launched for the founder's 550th anniversary with the target of 550 micro-forests, plants dense, fast-canopy native woodland on small plots — gurdwara lands, school grounds, village commons — across Punjab and beyond; the count has long passed the original target, and the method (native species, close planting, community adoption) is designed for replication by any sangat with a spare half-acre.

Its hardest work is at home: Punjab, the Green Revolution's exhausted engine — plunging water tables, stubble smoke, pesticide residues — where EcoSikh's advocacy (paddy alternatives, organic farming pilots, water audits of gurdwaras) addresses the tradition's own heartland with the tradition's own texts. The pitch to a Punjabi farmer is not Geneva's: it is that the bani he recited at his wedding calls the earth his **mother**, and the water table under his feet is her health chart.
