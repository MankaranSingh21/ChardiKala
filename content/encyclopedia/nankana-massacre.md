By 1921 the gurdwara at **Nankana Sahib** — the birthplace of Guru Nanak, richest shrine in Punjab by endowed lands — exemplified everything the reform movement had risen against. Its hereditary custodian, **Mahant Narain Das**, ran the holy site with dancing girls and documented assaults on women pilgrims, treating its revenues as a private estate; and as the Akali campaign to recover shrines gathered, he recruited a private force of mercenaries, laid in arms, and — the inquiries later established — prepared for the reformers' arrival with the connivance of nervous local officials who preferred a violent mahant to an organized laity.

On the morning of 20 February 1921, a jatha of about 150 Akalis under **Bhai Lachhman Singh** entered the shrine for prayers, under vows of nonviolence. The mahant's men barred the gates behind them and opened fire from the rooftops, then finished the wounded with swords. Bhai Lachhman Singh was tied to a tree and burned, by accounts alive; bodies were dismembered and burned with kerosene through the day. A second jatha and individual pilgrims arriving were killed at the gates. The dead numbered well above a hundred — the precise count impossible, given the fires.

The massacre, instead of breaking the movement, settled the country behind it. The government, reading the wind, transferred the shrine to the SGPC within days; Gandhi and the national leadership came to Nankana in solidarity; and the image of unarmed Sikhs walking into gunfire under oath became the moral template for the morchas — Guru ka Bagh the next year, Jaito after — that won the **Sikh Gurdwaras Act of 1925**. The reformers' memorial stands beside the Guru's birth-room: the cradle and the price of keeping it, in one courtyard.
