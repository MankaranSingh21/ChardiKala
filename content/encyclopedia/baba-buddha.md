As a boy grazing cattle near Kartarpur, Bura asked Guru Nanak a question about death so unseasonably wise that the Guru remarked the child spoke like an old man — *buddha* — and the name held for ninety years more. Baba Buddha became Sikh history's great thread of continuity: he knew the founder personally, and he lived — through what the tradition reckons as a century and a quarter — to apply the ceremonial mark of guruship to **five successors**: Angad, Amar Das, Ram Das, Arjan, and Hargobind. The institution of succession, so fatal to other movements, passed in Sikhi through the steady hands of one farmer who had been present at the beginning and wanted nothing.

His offices map the tradition's growth. He supervised the excavation of the Amrit Sarovar; he taught the young Gurus-to-be (Guru Hargobind's training in arms and letters was his charge); and when the Adi Granth was installed at Harmandir Sahib in 1604, Guru Arjan appointed him its first **granthi** — the first person to open the scripture on its throne and read the day's command. The post he inaugurated is the only ministry Sikhi has.

The tradition keeps him close to the ground it found him on. He remained a cultivator; the sakhi most told of him is a blessing delivered through onions — the prophecy to the langar-serving Mata Ganga that her son would crush enemies as he crushed the bulbs in his hand: Hargobind. Bir Baba Buddha Sahib marks his fields. He died in 1631, Guru Hargobind lowering him to the pyre — the sixth Guru, carrying the man who had carried the first.
