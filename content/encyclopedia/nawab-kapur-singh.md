In the killing decades after Banda Singh's execution, when the Sikh nation lived in saddles and thorn-forests with prices on its heads, the man who turned a hunted remnant into an organized power was Kapur Singh of Faizullapur — and the manner of his elevation tells the whole story of Sikh authority. In 1733, the Lahore government, trying conciliation, offered the Khalsa a jagir and the title of **Nawab**. The assembly debated; the title was accepted — and conferred, by the sangat's decision, on Kapur Singh, who was at that moment **fanning the congregation**, and who accepted on condition that his seva not be taken from him. He held a Mughal title of nobility and kept waving the fan; the tradition has never stopped telling it.

His statesmanship built the structures that survival required. He organized the scattered bands into the **Dal Khalsa** — the Buddha Dal of veterans to hold shrines, train, and teach; the Taruna Dal of the young to fight — divisions practical enough to function in famine years and federal enough to forbid warlordism. When the truce collapsed and the extermination campaigns resumed (he led through the Chhota Ghallughara's aftermath), the architecture held: twice-yearly Sarbat Khalsa, gurmatta discipline, rakhi revenues. The misl confederacy that ruled Punjab within a generation was his blueprint scaled up.

He declined to found a dynasty of leadership: at Vaisakhi 1748, aging, he passed command over his own claims to the young **Jassa Singh Ahluwalia** before the assembled Panth. He died in 1753. Sikh institutional memory ranks him with the founders — the proof that the community's eighteenth-century miracle was not only courage but administration, performed by a man holding a fan.
