Shaheed comes through Persian from the Arabic for **witness**: a shaheed is one whose death testifies. Sikh history is so dense with shaheedi that the daily Ardas is, in large part, a recitation of it — those cut limb from limb, scalped, boiled, sawn; the mothers garlanded with their children's bodies who did not renounce the faith. A community that prays its martyrology every morning has made remembrance of sacrifice a permanent organ of its body.

The Sikh doctrine has firm edges, and learners should see them clearly. Shaheedi is **never aggression**: there is no Sikh category of holy death achieved by killing the unconsenting; the martyr accepts death, never deals it to bystanders. It is **never sought**: the tradition has no cult of voluntary self-destruction — Guru Arjan did not seek the hot plate, and the Gurus negotiated, relocated, and defended themselves for decades before accepting the price. And it is **always for others or for truth**: the canonical cases are protection (of Kashmiri Pandits' worship, of the sangat, of the defenceless) or refusal to falsify (one altered syllable of bani, one forced conversion, declined at the cost of everything). Dying in defence of *another* religion's freedom — the ninth Guru's case — remains the tradition's gold standard, precisely because no self-interest can be read into it.

Shaheedi's function is forward-looking: the witness testifies *to the living*. The Ardas recites the martyrs not to nurse grievance — the prayer pointedly curses no perpetrators — but to keep the community's spine calibrated: this is what the truth has cost; hold it accordingly. The week of the Sahibzade each December, the June remembrance of 1984's dead, the name Shaheed before a thousand names: a ledger kept so that courage stays a remembered, practised, ordinary thing.
