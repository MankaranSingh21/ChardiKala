Tegh Bahadur spent decades in contemplative obscurity at Bakala before the dying child-Guru's words — "Baba Bakale" — sent the community to find him. As Guru he travelled the east of the subcontinent for years, founded the town of **Anandpur** in the Shivalik foothills, and composed bani of piercing stillness: 116 hymns on impermanence, fearlessness, and the mind unshaken — including the line that may be Sikhi's shortest complete ethics: **"Bhai kahu ko det neh, neh bhai manat aan"** — *frighten no one, and fear no one.*

In 1675, Kashmiri Pandits facing forced conversion under Aurangzeb's governors came to Anandpur for help. The Guru's nine-year-old son — the future Guru Gobind Singh — is remembered as saying that no one was worthier to answer than his father. Guru Tegh Bahadur went to Delhi knowing precisely what it meant. Offered the standard choice — Islam, miracles, or death — he refused the first two. His companions were tortured to death before his eyes: Bhai Mati Das sawn in half, Bhai Dayala boiled alive, Bhai Sati Das burned. Then, in Chandni Chowk, the public square of the capital, he was beheaded.

He is called **Hind di Chadar** — the shield of Hind — because he died for the right of *another* religion to exist. The distinction matters enormously: this is not tolerance, which permits, but something stronger, which protects at full price. Gurdwara Sis Ganj stands where the sword fell, and Rakab Ganj where a disciple burned his own house down to cremate the Guru's body in secret. His son inherited both the guruship and the question: what must a community become, so that this can never be done to anyone again?
