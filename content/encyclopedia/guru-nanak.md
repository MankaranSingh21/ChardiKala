Guru Nanak was born in 1469 in Talwandi — today Nankana Sahib, Pakistan — into a Punjab layered with Hindu and Muslim life. The stories of his youth all turn on the same hinge: he kept asking what ritual was *for*, and refusing forms that contradicted their own meaning. At the janeu ceremony, where a sacred thread would mark him as twice-born and above others, he declined it and asked instead for a thread of compassion and contentment that would neither break nor burn.

Around 1499, after three days' disappearance in the river Bein at Sultanpur, he spoke the sentence on which everything after rests: **"There is no Hindu, there is no Musalman."** Not that the faiths were false — but that the One does not check labels, and neither should we.

He then walked. For roughly a quarter-century, with his lifelong companion **Bhai Mardana** — a Muslim, a rabab player — he travelled east to Assam, south to Sri Lanka, north into the Himalaya, and west to Mecca and Baghdad, debating priests, qazis, and yogis, always returning to one teaching: *truth is high; higher still is truthful living.*

His last act was perhaps his most radical. He settled at **Kartarpur**, put on work clothes, and farmed. The founder of a faith spent his final eighteen years as a smallholder who hosted dinner — the first langar — for whoever came. Three disciplines crystallised there: **naam japna** (remember the One), **kirat karo** (earn honestly), **vand chhakna** (share before you eat).

He composed 974 hymns preserved in the Guru Granth Sahib, including Japji Sahib, recited by Sikhs every dawn. Before he died in 1539, he passed the light not to his sons but to the disciple who served best — and tradition records Hindus and Muslims arguing over the honour of his remains, both claiming him, which may be the most accurate biography of all.
