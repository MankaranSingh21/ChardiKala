Fariduddin Ganjshakar — Baba Farid — was a master of the Chishti Sufi order at Ajodhan (now Pakpattan, "the ferry of the pure," renamed for him), two and a half centuries **before Guru Nanak**: the earliest voice in the Guru Granth Sahib, and by scholarly consensus the first great poet of the Punjabi language. That the foundational poems of Punjabi are a Muslim saint's, preserved chiefly inside the Sikh scripture, is the kind of fact this region specializes in.

His four hymns and 112 **saloks** in the Granth distil the Chishti disciplines — poverty embraced, the ego starved, the Beloved awaited — into Punjabi of austere, aching tenderness: the crow picking at the lover's wasted body, begged to spare the eyes that still hope to see the Beloved; the long night of the soul counted watch by watch; the warning that the crane of the riverbank, preening, is taken mid-preen by the hawk. His counsel runs all one way — *be the grass on the path*, trodden and fragrant; answer the striker with a blessing; the heart's prayer-mat outranks the mosque's.

The Gurus did him the honour the tradition reserves for the fully serious: they **answered him**. Where Farid's asceticism bent toward self-mortification, Guru Nanak and his successors placed adjacent saloks — agreeing on the ego, amending the method: the body is not the enemy; the householder's road also reaches. The canonised conversation — Sufi proposing, Gurus responding, both preserved — is scripture-as-seminar, and the model of how this tradition disagrees: respectfully, adjacently, and without deleting anyone.
