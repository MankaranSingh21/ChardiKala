The Sikh funeral — antam sanskar, the last rite — is constructed, in every detail, against two things: the fear of death and the theatre of grief. The body is bathed and dressed (an amritdhari's kakaar remain), and **cremation** is the norm — fire or crematorium; the ashes given to flowing water, with the Rehat Maryada's pointed instruction that **no monument** be raised over remains: the tradition that would not let the Guru's body become a relic will not let anyone's become one.

What is said and sung at the pyre defines the theology. **Kirtan Sohila** — the same composition recited every night before sleep — is recited at the fire: the tradition deliberately uses one text for sleep and death, filing them under the same management. Loud lamentation — the breast-beating, professional mourning of the region's older customs — is explicitly discouraged: the bani's position is that the dead have gone home through **hukam**, and wailing argues with the order of things; the prescribed comfort is *Waheguru* on the tongue and the sangat at the family's side. Anand Sahib — the Song of **Bliss**, sung at weddings — is part of the rite: the same song for marriage and death, the tradition's most quietly radical filing decision.

The days after centre on the **Granth**: a complete reading (sahaj path at the family's pace, or akhand path), concluded with ardas, hukam, and langar — the community fed in the name of the departed, grief metabolized as hospitality. Memory is honoured by **seva and remembrance**, not anniversaries of mourning. The dying themselves are eased with Sukhmani Sahib at the bedside — the Psalm of Peace as the tradition's palliative care, in continuous clinical use for four centuries.
