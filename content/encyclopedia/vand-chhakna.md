Vand chhakna means, with grammatical precision, *share-and-then-eat* — the sharing comes first in the verb as in the practice. It is the third pillar of Sikh life, completing Guru Nanak's triad: remember the One, earn honestly, and divide what you earned before you enjoy it.

The sequence distinguishes it from charity as commonly practised. Charity is typically residual — generosity from the surplus after one's own wants are met, at a level chosen by mood. Vand chhakna is structural: the share for others is allocated *first*, as a standing claim on the harvest, not a tip. Its formalized descendant is **dasvandh**, the tenth of income given to community needs; its daily face is the langar, funded and stocked by exactly this habit — the sack of flour brought to the gurdwara before the household bakes its own.

The theology underneath is an audit of ownership. The bani regards wealth as held, not owned: the fields, the rain, the strength of hands are all issued from the One's storehouse, so the harvest arrives pre-encumbered with everyone's claim. Hoarding is thus not stinginess but a kind of accounting fraud. Guru Nanak's image endures: the hoarder is the crane by the pond, hunched over what it cannot keep; the sharer is the river, wealthy precisely because everything moves through.

What should a newcomer do with this? The tradition's answer is unembarrassedly concrete: build the split into your budget the way the gurdwara builds the langar into its floor plan. Feed people as a habit, not a gesture. And notice the pillar's quiet psychology — the practice was prescribed not only to relieve the hungry but to heal the sharer, since the fist that opens on schedule eventually forgets how to clench.
