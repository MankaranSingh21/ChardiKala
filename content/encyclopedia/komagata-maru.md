Canada in 1914 wanted Punjabi labour gone, but the Punjabis were British subjects with formal rights of movement — so exclusion wore procedural dress: the **continuous journey** regulation, requiring immigrants to arrive by an unbroken voyage from India at a time when no shipping line offered one. The law named no race and admitted none of its targets. **Gurdit Singh**, a Sikh businessman of Singapore, decided to break the pretence by satisfying it: he chartered a Japanese steamer, the Komagata Maru, and sailed for Vancouver with **376 passengers** — 337 Sikhs among them, veterans' medals on more than a few chests.

The ship anchored in Burrard Inlet on 23 May 1914 and was held offshore for **two months**: food and water rationed by officials as leverage, lawyers blocked from boarding, the shore community of Vancouver Sikhs raising funds and tempers in equal measure, and a final test case decided — predictably — for the regulation. On 23 July, under the guns of a navy cruiser, the ship was escorted out and turned home. Empire met it at the dock: at **Budge Budge** near Calcutta, police attempting to force the passengers onto a Punjab-bound train opened fire; about twenty were killed, and Gurdit Singh spent years underground.

The episode radicalized the Pacific coast — Ghadar recruitment surged on its wake — and it has lived a long second life as a mirror: Canada's Prime Minister offered a formal apology in the House of Commons in **2016**, and the ship's name now attaches to memorials, scholarships, and every debate in which a wealthy democracy designs a neutral-sounding rule with a precise complexion in mind. The passengers, a century on, keep arriving on other ships.
