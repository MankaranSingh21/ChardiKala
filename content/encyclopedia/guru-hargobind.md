Guru Hargobind was eleven when his father was tortured to death by the empire. His response defined Sikhi's relationship to power ever after. At his succession he asked for **two swords**, wearing one for **piri** — spiritual authority, the inheritance of Nanak — and one for **miri** — temporal authority, the duty to act in the world. The doctrine is not that religion should rule; it is that the spirit cannot stand by while the body of the innocent is broken.

Facing Harmandir Sahib, he built the **Akal Takht** — the Throne of the Timeless One — a raised platform from which worldly matters were addressed: disputes settled, strategy debated, justice done. The architecture is the theology: the shrine of prayer and the seat of responsibility look at each other across the water, neither permitted to forget the other.

He kept an army; he hunted; he wore a warrior's dress; and the tradition was careful to note what he did with strength. The story Sikhs tell most is **Bandi Chhor** — released from imprisonment in Gwalior Fort, he refused to leave unless the fifty-two Hindu princes held there came too, and won their freedom on the hem of his cloak. Sikhs still light Divali lamps for that day: liberation that counts only if it is shared.

He fought four defensive battles against imperial forces and won them, but built no kingdom and annexed nothing. The sword, in his teaching, has exactly one legitimate work: protection. His grandson Tegh Bahadur and great-grandson Gobind Singh would spend the rest of the century proving how much that costs.
