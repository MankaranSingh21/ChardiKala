Sophia Duleep Singh was born in 1876 into the empire's most ornamental cage: daughter of the deposed Maharaja of Punjab, goddaughter of **Queen Victoria**, granted apartments at Hampton Court Palace and a debutante's London of couture and lapdogs — the society pages' favourite exotic princess, bred, as the arrangement intended, to decorate and to forget.

Two journeys to India broke the arrangement: visiting in 1903 and 1907 — the second for the centenary commemorations around Lahore, where she saw famine, plague, the surveillance her family's name attracted, and the country her father had died asking for — she came home altered. Her cause found her in 1909: **votes for women**. She joined the Women's Social and Political Union and became one of its most photographed weapons, precisely because of the address: the princess **selling *The Suffragette* outside Hampton Court's gates**, under the Crown's own windows. She bankrolled the movement; she refused taxes ("no vote, no tax") and let the courts seize her jewels — her diamond ring auctioned and bought back by suffragettes, repeatedly, as theatre; she was in the front rank of the **Black Friday** deputation of 1910, when police violence against the women shocked even Edwardian opinion; and the file the India Office kept on her concluded, in the era's weariest official prose, that nothing could be done — imprisoning Victoria's goddaughter was the one scandal the cause could not be given.

She drove ambulance funds and nursed Indian soldiers in the Great War (turbaned sepoys startled to be tended by their Maharaja's daughter), kept faith with the movement to the franchise and past it. Asked late in life by a census of notable women for her interests, she wrote one line: *the advancement of women.* She died in 1948 — the year after the Punjab her family lost was lost again, differently.
