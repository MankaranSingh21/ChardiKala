The Guru Granth Sahib repeatedly names five forces that rob the human house from inside: **kaam** (lust, craving), **krodh** (anger), **lobh** (greed), **moh** (attachment, infatuation), and **ahankar** (pride). The tradition calls them the five thieves — *pañj chor* — and the metaphor is exact: they do not break the door; they live in the home, know where everything is kept, and steal the one treasure, attention, a little every day.

Two features of the Sikh analysis are worth a student's care. First, these are **energies, not demons** — distortions of capacities that are themselves necessary. Desire builds families; anger powers justice; acquisition feeds children; attachment is loyalty's raw material; self-respect holds spines straight. Each becomes a thief at the point where it stops serving and starts steering — where the master of the house is bound and the servant drives. Hence the goal is governance, not amputation: Sikhi pointedly rejected the renunciate solution of fleeing the objects of desire, since the thieves travel inside, and a cave contains them as well as a city.

Second, the counterforce is not willpower but **occupancy**. The bani's prescription is to fill the house — with naam, with sangat, with seva — so the thieves find the rooms lit and the owner home. A mind anchored in remembrance is not heroically resisting greed at every moment; it is interested in something else. The five virtues (truth, contentment, compassion, humility, love) are the furniture of the occupied house.

The five appear constantly in Sikh self-examination — before the amrit ceremony, in daily ardaas, in the elder's quiet question to the angry young man: *which of the five is driving right now?* Naming the thief, the tradition has found, is half the arrest.
