By the fifth Guru's time, the hymns of Nanak and his successors lived in scattered collections — and forgeries had begun to appear, verses of rivals circulating under the Gurus' names. Guru Arjan's answer, completed in 1604, was an act of textual engineering that secured the faith permanently: a single, authenticated, sealed canon.

The work took years at Ramsar, on Amritsar's edge: **Bhai Gurdas** as scribe, the Guru dictating and editing. Authentic compositions of the first five Gurus were gathered (the collections of Guru Amar Das's grandson Sahansram Mohan, the *Mohan Pothis*, among the sources), verified, and arranged on a plan of startling originality — not by author or theme but by **raag**, the musical mode of each hymn, thirty-one of them, each with the Gurus' hymns in order of succession followed by the bhagats'.

The inclusion of the **bhagats** was the edition's boldest decision: Kabir, Farid, Ravidas, Namdev and others — Muslims, low-caste Hindus, a butcher, a barber — canonised as equals inside the scripture of a third tradition. Guru Arjan was constructing, deliberately, the first interfaith canon: the truth honoured wherever it had been sung.

In August 1604 the completed volume was carried to the newly finished Harmandir Sahib, the aged **Baba Buddha** appointed its first granthi, and the Guru installed it with the protocol that has never lapsed: the Granth on the throne, the Guru sleeping on the floor beside it. When the emperor's court, fed complaints that the book insulted Islam and Hinduism, demanded changes, Guru Arjan declined to alter a word. Two years later that integrity helped cost him his life — making the Adi Granth perhaps the only scripture proofread in its compiler's blood.
