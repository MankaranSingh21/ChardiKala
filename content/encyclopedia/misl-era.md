Between the collapse of Mughal Punjab and the rise of Ranjit Singh, the Sikhs governed their homeland through a system with few parallels: **twelve misls** — confederated military brotherhoods (the word likely from the Arabic for "file" or "equal") — each sovereign in its territory, all answerable to the **Sarbat Khalsa** assembling twice yearly at Amritsar, decisions taken as gurmatta before the Guru Granth Sahib and binding on chiefs who had argued against them.

Entry was open in a way Europe's contemporaries would not have believed: a misl was joined, not inherited into; a capable rider of any birth could rise — several founding chiefs were carpenters, herdsmen, or village menials by origin, a fact their enemies recorded as scandal and the Khalsa as proof of concept. Under the **rakhi** system, villages of any faith paid the protecting misl a fifth of harvest — typically far below Mughal or Afghan extraction — for genuine protection from both raiders and rival misls, an arrangement historians have called the era's most effective public-safety contract.

The misls quarrelled — territory, precedence, blood feuds the Sarbat Khalsa repeatedly composed — and they also, when the horn sounded, combined: against Durrani's nine invasions; for the 1783 entry into Delhi itself, where Baghel Singh took gurdwaras instead of the throne.

Verdicts on the era differ by what is measured. As a state, it was untidy and finally consumed by its strongest member. As an *experiment* — a stateless nation running on assembly, audit, and oath for a third of a century, between the wreck of one empire and the birth of another — it remains the largest-scale demonstration of Sikh political theory ever conducted: Guru Panth, with cavalry.
