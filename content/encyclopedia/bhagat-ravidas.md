Ravidas (15th–16th century) worked leather in Banaras — a *chamar*, of the caste whose touch and trade the social order ranked as pollution itself — and from that workshop produced devotional poetry so unanswerable that brahmins, kings, and (tradition holds) the princess-poet Mirabai took initiation from a man whose shadow they had been taught to avoid. His method was not protest but demonstrated sanctity: the verses concede the assigned degradation in order to detonate it — *my caste is low, my lineage low, my work is low,* he sings, and yet the One has taken him; the dyer does not check the cloth's pedigree before the colour takes.

His masterpiece is fifty words of political theory disguised as longing: **Begampura** — *the city without sorrow* — where there is no second or third class of citizen, no tax, no fear, no torture, and where (the line lands quietly) *those who walk beside me do so because they wish to*. Composed by a man legally untouchable, it is among the earliest visions in any literature of a society with caste deleted — and it stands, in full, inside the Guru Granth Sahib, alongside forty more of his hymns.

The Gurus' embrace was total: his bani in the canon, his theology in the langar's floor-plan. And his name became a banner beyond Sikhi: the **Ravidassia** communities of Punjab and the diaspora, and Dalit movements across north India, claim him as founder-saint — with tensions and tragedies (the 2009 Vienna shooting and its aftermath) that belong to honest accounts. The cobbler's city remains unbuilt; the blueprints, as he intended, are in everyone's scripture.
