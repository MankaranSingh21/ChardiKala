In the first days of June 1984, the Indian Army — with tanks, artillery, and commandos — assaulted the Harmandir Sahib complex in Amritsar to remove **Jarnail Singh Bhindranwale** and his armed followers, who had fortified the Akal Takht amid the spiralling violence of the Punjab crisis. Whatever the operation's stated necessity, three of its choices seared it into Sikh memory beyond any possibility of neutral commemoration. It was launched across the **martyrdom anniversary of Guru Arjan**, when the complex held thousands of pilgrims; the assault proceeded with those pilgrims inside, and they died in numbers that official figures (about 500 dead overall) and independent accounts (well over a thousand, many noncombatants) dispute to this day. The **Akal Takht** — the throne of Sikh sovereignty, built by the sixth Guru — was reduced to rubble by tank fire. And in the aftermath, the **Sikh Reference Library** — manuscripts, hukamnamas, relics of the Gurus' own hands — burned or vanished into army custody; much has never been returned.

Simultaneous operations struck dozens of other gurdwaras; Punjab was sealed from the press; and several Sikh army units, hearing the news, mutinied — a measure of the shock inside the state's own institutions.

The operation killed Bhindranwale and ended the fortification. What it began was larger: Sikh opinion worldwide, including the great majority with no sympathy for militancy, experienced the assault as a desecration without precedent since Durrani — Ahmad Shah had at least faced armed enemies. The Prime Minister who ordered it was assassinated by Sikh bodyguards within five months; the pogroms that followed killed thousands of innocents; and a decade of insurgency consumed Punjab. Every June, the Akal Takht — rebuilt by the sangat's own kar seva — hosts the remembrance. The word Sikhs most often use is **ghallughara**.
