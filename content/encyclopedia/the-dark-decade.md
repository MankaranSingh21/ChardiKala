Between 1984 and the mid-1990s, Punjab passed through a war that official vocabulary called a law-and-order problem. Militant groups fighting for Khalistan — their ranks swollen after the twin traumas of 1984 — carried out assassinations and, increasingly as the years ground on, massacres of civilians: bus passengers pulled out and shot, Hindu families targeted, Sikh dissenters and rivals killed, extortion shading into banditry. The state's response, particularly in the campaigns toward the end of the period, applied what its own commanders later described frankly as a policy of extermination: cordon-and-sweep operations, torture as routine procedure, bounty payments per kill that incentivized fakery, and the disappearance of young men on a scale the region is still counting. Both directions of the violence made the ordinary Punjabi villager its primary victim; tens of thousands died in all.

The era's moral landmark is an accountant of it. **Jaswant Singh Khalra**, a human-rights worker, traced the disappeared through the most prosaic of records — municipal firewood purchases for cremations — and documented thousands of secret cremations of unidentified "unclaimed" bodies in just three crematoria of one district. He took the evidence abroad, was warned explicitly by police officials, and in September 1995 was abducted from his home and later killed in custody; six policemen were convicted a decade on. His remark before the end — "I challenged the darkness; if nothing else, around myself, I will not let it settle" — has entered the Ardas-adjacent memory of the Panth.

The insurgency ended by attrition and exhaustion by 1995. The accounting — of the disappeared, of mass graves, of impunity on all sides — remains open, carried now by the families and the human-rights organizations that inherited Khalra's ledgers.
