Seva is service without wage, recognition, or test of the recipient — and it is the practice most visitors to Sikh spaces notice first, because it is everywhere: the rows of volunteers rolling roti in the langar kitchen, the elder sweeping the courtyard at dawn, the teenager stacking the sangat's shoes, the medical camp, the flood boats, the free kitchen at the protest line.

In Sikh theology, seva is not charity. Charity flows downhill, from haves to have-nots, and quietly flatters the giver. Seva is understood as flowing *toward the One*, who is present in the served: tradition repeats that the Guru is in the sangat, the light is in every face — so the dishes you wash are, in the only sense that matters, the Guru's dishes. This inversion does serious work. It removes the means test (you do not interrogate the Guru about whether he deserves lunch), removes condescension, and removes the donor's plaque. Classical seva is anonymous, menial by preference, and aimed at the giver's own disease: **haumai**, the imperial self, which cannot survive sustained, attentive toilet-cleaning.

The tradition grades it: seva of the body (tan — labour), of mind (man — teaching, organizing, attention), of means (dhan — giving, formalized in dasvandh, the tenth). All three are expected of the able as ordinary practice, the way exercise is ordinary, not heroic.

Its exemplars mark the range. Bhai Kanhaiya watering wounded enemies on the battlefield, because he saw no enemies. Bhagat Puran Singh carrying a disabled boy on his back for years, founding Pingalwara for the abandoned. Khalsa Aid trucking water into Yemen and Ukraine under the line *sarbat da bhala*. And, daily and unglamorously, several hundred thousand people peeling garlic in gurdwara kitchens so that anyone on earth who walks in hungry walks out fed.
