The eleventh Guru of the Sikhs is a book, and has been since 1708 — but "book" undersells what Sikhs mean. The Guru Granth Sahib is treated as a living sovereign: ceremonially woken (**prakash**) each morning and rested (**sukhasan**) each night, housed under a canopy, attended with a whisk, carried on the head, given the centre of every room it enters. A Sikh wedding circles it; a Sikh child's name begins with a letter taken from it at random; the dying are eased with its hymns.

This is not idolatry of paper and ink, and the tradition is careful on the point. Reverence attaches to the **shabad** — the word, the teaching — for which the volume is the throne. The deepest statement of Sikh theology is that the Guru was never the body anyway: the same light, the tradition says, passed through ten human lamps, and now burns in the word itself, where no assassin or empire can reach it.

The arrangement has a second half: **Guru Panth**. Guru Gobind Singh vested authority jointly — in the scripture as the source of teaching, and in the community gathered before it as the agent of decision. Five initiated Sikhs deliberating in the Guru's presence can speak with the Guru's authority; this is the constitutional theory behind the Sarbat Khalsa assemblies and the Akal Takht's edicts.

For new learners the practical upshot is this: when Sikhs rise as one when the Granth enters, they are not worshipping an object. They are keeping a 300-year-old promise — that the teacher would never again be a target, because the teacher would be everywhere the words are sung.
