Rai Bhoi di Talwandi, a country town southwest of Lahore, renamed itself across the centuries for the child born there in 1469: **Nankana Sahib**, the place of Nanak. Gurdwara Janam Asthan marks the birth-room itself, and a constellation of shrines around the town preserves the boyhood the Janamsakhis tell — Bal Lila of the play, Patti Sahib of the schoolroom (where the boy's acrostic on the alphabet became a hymn now in the Granth), Mal Ji Sahib of the tree whose shadow, the story insists, did not move from the sleeping child, and Kiara Sahib of the miraculously unharmed crop.

Its modern history carries both the tradition's wounds. In **1921** the shrine's corrupt hereditary custodian massacred a nonviolent reform jatha inside the compound — well over a hundred burned and butchered — the atrocity that broke the mahant system and built the SGPC; the martyrs' memorial stands beside the birth-room. In **1947** Partition placed the town in Pakistan, and the birthplace of the founder passed beyond the easy reach of most of his community: pilgrimage reduced to quota'd jathas on the great anniversaries, the Janam Asthan maintained by Pakistan's small resident Sikh community and its waqf authorities.

That community is the site's living fact, easy to miss behind the monuments: a few thousand Pakistani Sikhs — many of Pashtun trading families relocated from the frontier — keep the gurdwaras, run shops, and staff the great **Gurpurab of Kattak** (Guru Nanak's birth anniversary), when tens of thousands arrive — Indian jathas, diaspora pilgrims, local Muslims joining the langar — and the town of the man who said *there is no Hindu, there is no Musalman* briefly demonstrates the proposition.
