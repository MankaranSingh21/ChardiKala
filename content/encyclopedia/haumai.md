Haumai is a compound of two pronouns: **hau** (I) and **mai** (me). I-me, I-me, I-me — the looping background hum of self-reference that the Guru Granth Sahib identifies as the root sickness of the human condition. Not the healthy sense of being a self, but the compulsive centring of every perception on it: *my* gain, *my* slight, *my* name, *my* tribe.

The bani's analysis is precise. Haumai is called the great disease (*haumai deeragh rog hai*) — and, in the same line, the medicine is named within it: remembrance dissolves what self-reference congeals. Haumai is the wall (*kood di paal*, the wall of falsehood) between the person and the One — not built by sin in the Christian sense, but by misperception: the light is one, and haumai insists on a private flame. From it spring the five thieves — lust, anger, greed, attachment, pride — each one haumai wearing a different coat.

What makes the Sikh treatment distinctive is its sociability. Traditions that diagnose ego often prescribe withdrawal: the cave, the cloister, the silence. The Gurus prescribed the opposite — **seva**, service, preferably menial and anonymous: cleaning the sangat's shoes, sweeping the floor, washing dishes in the langar by the thousand. You cannot polish a stranger's muddy shoes attentively and maintain the imperial self at full volume. The langar floor, where the surgeon sits beside the sweeper, is an ego-reduction machine disguised as lunch.

The cured state is not self-erasure. The Gurus signed their compositions, led armies, founded cities — agency intact. The difference is the axis: the gurmukh acts from the One outward; the manmukh from the me outward. Same hands, different sun at the centre of the orbit.
