Sikh temporal authority is seated at five **takhts** — thrones — whose locations write the tradition's history onto the map of the subcontinent.

The **Akal Takht** at Amritsar (1606, Guru Hargobind) is first and supreme: the seat of miri facing Harmandir Sahib, source of the hukamnamas that address the whole Panth. **Takht Kesgarh Sahib** at Anandpur marks the spot of Vaisakhi 1699 — where the five rose, the amrit was stirred, and the Khalsa was born; the hill town's name, "city of bliss," carries the ninth Guru's founding and the tenth's forge. **Takht Damdama Sahib** at Talwandi Sabo, in the Malwa, is the "breathing place" where Guru Gobind Singh halted after the battles of 1705 and where, tradition holds, he dictated the final recension of the Guru Granth Sahib from memory — the scriptural capital, seat of the Khalsa's scholarship. **Takht Patna Sahib**, far east in Bihar, guards the birthplace of Guru Gobind Singh (1666) — the tenth Guru born on the Ganges, a reminder that the Gurus' geography was never merely Punjab. And **Takht Hazur Sahib** at Nanded, on the Godavari in Maharashtra, holds the place of his passing (1708) — where the guruship was vested in the Granth and the line of human Gurus closed.

Each takht keeps its own traditions (Patna and Nanded preserve weapons and relics of the tenth Guru; Hazur Sahib's rites retain distinctive old forms), and each anchors pilgrimage routes that knit the community across two thousand kilometres. Together they are the tradition's federal architecture: authority distributed across the map the Gurus actually walked — from the river of the founder's birth to the river of the founder's succession.
