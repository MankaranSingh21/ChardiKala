Smallpox took his left eye and his looks in childhood; he learned the horse and the sword instead of letters; and at ten he was in his first battle, at seventeen heading the Sukerchakia misl, at twenty-one Maharaja of Punjab. Europeans who met Ranjit Singh in his pomp left the same disoriented portrait: a small, plain, one-eyed man in no finery, sitting cross-legged on a chair's edge, asking machine-gun questions about everything — and unmistakably the most intelligent presence in any room of the subcontinent.

His state, the **Sarkar-e-Khalsa**, was the era's anomaly: built by conquest yet nearly bloodless in governance — the death penalty effectively abolished across four decades, a record no contemporary monarchy approached; staffed by talent over tribe (Muslim foreign ministers, Hindu treasurers, Italian and French generals drilling the Fauj-i-Khas); and styled, on every coin, as the Gurus' government, not his. He took fines from the Akal Takht like any Sikh — summoned for a moral lapse, he bared his back for the lash and was pardoned; the episode, true to form, increased his authority by advertising its limits.

He closed the Khyber to invasion for the first time in eight centuries — Nalwa's frontier — and his court at Lahore glittered with the Koh-i-Noor he had extracted from Afghan refugees-royal. His flaws were a king's: wives and intrigues in quantity, and a succession left to chance, which destroyed his house within a decade of his death in 1839. His monuments chose themselves: the gold on Harmandir Sahib is his sheathing, and Punjabi speech still measures good government against *Ranjit Singh da raj* — the one-eyed man's forty years when, as his Muslim chroniclers wrote, the wolf and the lamb drank at one ghat.
