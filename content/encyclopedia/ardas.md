The Ardas is not in the Guru Granth Sahib. It is the community's own composition, evolved across three centuries, and it is prayed standing, hands folded, at the close of every service and at every threshold of Sikh life — before a journey, after a birth, beside a grave, before opening a shop, before an exam, before battle.

Its structure is a teaching in itself. It begins with **remembrance**: the ten Gurus invoked by name, then the Guru Granth Sahib. Then it walks through the community's history of sacrifice in unflinching detail — those who were cut joint by joint, scalped, broken on wheels, sawn in half; the mothers who watched their children cut apart and did not abandon their faith; the Ghallugharas; the shrines lost and the shrines defended. A people that prays its own martyrology daily cannot be made to forget what survival cost — and the prayer names this suffering without cursing the inflictors, which is its quiet discipline.

Then come the petitions: for the gift of remembrance, for humility, for the protection of the Panth, for the welfare of the shrines, and — the line every Sikh waits for — **"Nanak naam chardi kala, tere bhane sarbat da bhala"**: through Nanak's name may spirits rise ever higher, and in Your will, may there be the welfare of all. Not of Sikhs. Of all.

A faith reveals itself in what it asks for when it has permission to ask for anything. The Ardas asks for high spirits and universal good — and adds, in its closing couplets, a personal note for whatever specific occasion gathered the sangat that day. The congregation answers **Waheguru ji ka Khalsa, Waheguru ji ki Fateh**, and sits, and the work resumes. The prayer assumes the work resumes.
