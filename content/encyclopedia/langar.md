Langar is the community kitchen attached to every gurdwara on earth, serving free vegetarian meals to absolutely anyone — Sikh or not, believer or not, hungry by poverty or hungry by lunchtime — with no payment, no sermon attached, and no questions about deserving. At Harmandir Sahib in Amritsar it feeds on the order of a hundred thousand people a day; globally, Sikh kitchens serve millions daily, which makes the langar very likely the largest continuous free-food operation in human history. It has been running since the sixteenth century.

It began as Guru Nanak's table at Kartarpur and was institutionalised under Guru Angad — whose wife **Mata Khivi** ran it so memorably that the Guru Granth Sahib itself praises her by name — and Guru Amar Das, who made eating in it a precondition for audience. The meal is vegetarian not as doctrine but as hospitality: the table must exclude no one's conscience, so it is set to the widest common denominator.

Everything about its design encodes teaching. The food is funded by the sangat's own offerings and **dasvandh**; cooked and served by volunteers doing seva (rolling pins as spiritual equipment); eaten in **pangat**, rows on the floor at one level. The langar thus runs the whole Sikh curriculum — sharing, service, equality, community — without a word of instruction. It is catechism by chapati.

And it travels. The langar's logic — show up, cook, feed everyone, ask nothing — has appeared at earthquake zones and refugee borders, in pandemic lockdowns, and most famously at the 2020–21 farmers' protest, where the world watched a year-long encampment feed its own opponents. For this site's purposes, note what the langar proves daily: the open door is not an aspiration of Sikhi. It is a kitchen schedule.
