The great Sikh shrines are built not on summits but beside — or within — **water**: the sarovar, the engineered sacred pool, with bathing steps on all sides and, at Amritsar, the temple itself islanded at the centre. The form was set by the fourth and fifth Gurus: Guru Ram Das excavated the **Amrit Sarovar** — the pool of nectar that named the city — and Guru Arjan placed Harmandir Sahib in it, low, so that the house of the One sits *in* the water rather than above it.

The choice of water over height carries the tradition's signature. A summit ranks its climbers; a pool levels its bathers. At the sarovar's steps, the principle of pangat extends to immersion — every body in the same water, and the historic pools were pointedly open to the castes whom the era's sacred tanks excluded (Guru Amar Das's stepped **baoli** at Goindval made the point earlier: everyone draws the same water). **Ishnaan** — bathing, particularly at amrit vela before prayer — is cherished practice, but the bani polices its meaning relentlessly: ritual bathing washes nothing the mind keeps dirty; the true sarovar, Guru Nanak insists, is the inner one where naam is the water. The pools are reminders, not detergents.

The sarovars are also the community's oldest civil engineering: fed by canals (the hansli channels cut to bring Ravi water to Amritsar), de-silted by **kar seva** in which millions have stood hip-deep passing baskets of mud — emperors and grandmothers in the same line, the maintenance schedule doubling as the social doctrine. Pilgrims carry the water home in flasks; koi and reflections share the surface; and the famous image of the Golden Temple doubled in its pool is, theologically, exact: the shrine and its reflection at the same level.
