In late November 2020, columns of farmers — Punjab's Sikhs at the front, Haryana and western UP alongside — marched on Delhi against three hastily passed farm laws they read as the corporate dismantling of the procurement system their livelihoods hung on. Stopped at the capital's borders by trenches, water cannon, and container walls, they did the most Punjabi thing imaginable: they parked, and moved in. The encampments at **Singhu, Tikri, and Ghazipur** became, for over a year, functioning towns — tractor-trailer dormitories, libraries, medical tents, laundry, solar panels, a protest newspaper — with populations in the hundreds of thousands at peak.

The operating system was visibly the Sikh one. The camps ran on **langar** at a scale observers called the largest continuous free kitchen ever assembled — rotis by the hundred thousand daily, served to protesters, journalists, locals, and, in the movement's most photographed gesture, to the **police deployed against it**. Discipline followed the **morcha** template: oaths, jathas, elder marshals, the explicit instruction that the opponent was the law, not the lawmaker's constituents. Gurdwara networks and the diaspora supplied logistics; Khalsa Aid ran foot-massage tents for elders. The movement weathered a violent January 26 crisis, vilification as separatist, internet cuts, and some seven hundred deaths in the camps from cold, heart attack, and accident — and held.

In November 2021, on Guru Nanak's birthday — the choice of date was its own communiqué — the Prime Minister announced **repeal**. The tractors went home garlanded. Whatever else history makes of it, the protest gave a planetary audience a live demonstration of the tradition this encyclopedia documents: sangat, seva, langar, morcha — the software running, in public, under load.
