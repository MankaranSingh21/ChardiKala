In 1675, Kashmiri Pandits led by Kirpa Ram came to Anandpur with an extremity: Aurangzeb's administration was forcing conversions in Kashmir, and they had nowhere left to appeal. The Guru's nine-year-old son — the future tenth Guru — is remembered as supplying the unbearable arithmetic: who worthier than you, father? Guru Tegh Bahadur sent the empire a message of pure logical exposure: if Tegh Bahadur converts, the Pandits will follow. The empire accepted the wager.

He travelled toward Delhi and was arrested with three companions. What followed was designed as public pedagogy and became it, though not as intended. **Bhai Mati Das** was sawn in half alive; **Bhai Dayala** boiled in a cauldron; **Bhai Sati Das** wrapped in cotton and burned — each offered Islam, each declining, the Guru made to watch. Then, on 11 November 1675, in Chandni Chowk — the most public square of the imperial capital — the Guru, who had refused conversion and refused to perform miracles ("tricks for jugglers," the tradition has him say), was beheaded.

A dust storm, the tradition records, covered the square; Bhai Jaita carried the head three hundred kilometres to the son at Anandpur, while Bhai Lakhi Shah Vanjara burned his own house down around the body to give it cremation. Gurdwaras Sis Ganj and Rakab Ganj mark the two sites today.

The unique structure of this martyrdom bears stating plainly: a Guru of one faith died for the right of *another* faith — one whose doctrines he did not share — to practise freely. Not tolerance; protection at the price of the protector's head. His son would spend his life answering the event's question, and the answer was the Khalsa.
