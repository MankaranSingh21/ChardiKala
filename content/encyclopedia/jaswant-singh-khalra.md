Jaswant Singh Khalra was a bank director in Amritsar — great-grandson of a Ghadar veteran; the lineage matters in Punjab — when colleagues began not coming to work. Tracing one friend's disappearance in the counterinsurgency years, he found the method that made him historic: the police could hide killings, but municipalities keep ledgers. In the **firewood purchase records** of three cremation grounds in a single district (Amritsar), he documented thousands of cremations of "unidentified" young men — over two thousand entries, many with names recoverable, delivered by police, burned at state expense, several to a pyre. Extrapolated across Punjab, the arithmetic indicted a policy, not excesses.

He took the evidence to the courts, the press, and — when India's avenues closed — abroad, addressing Canada's Parliament in early 1995. The warnings he received were not anonymous: senior police told him, in terms he repeated publicly, that persons more famous than he had vanished. His answer survives on tape, in the parable he told that year: when the sun set, the story runs, it asked who would light the world now — and **one small lamp** answered, *I will do what I can* — and from lamp to lamp, the dark was held off. *I have challenged the darkness,* he said. *If nothing else, around myself, I will not let it settle.*

On 6 September 1995 he was abducted from his home in daylight; he was tortured for weeks in custody and killed. It took ten years to convict six policemen; the senior architects died unprosecuted. His files seeded every subsequent accounting of the era. The lamp metaphor is now recited at vigils on three continents — generally, as he intended, while lighting them.
